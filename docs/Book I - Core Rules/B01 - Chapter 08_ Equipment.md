# **CHAPTER 8: EQUIPMENT**

A character without equipment is a collection of ability scores. Equipment is what makes those scores useful — the armor between a character and a sword, the rope between a character and the pit, the coin between a character and the next day's food.

This chapter covers what things cost, how much they weigh, and what they do. The prices are guidelines. They reflect the average value under normal supply conditions. The GM should adjust freely for scarcity, politics, and the particular economics of any specific settlement. Haggling down or being gouged up are both possible, especially for unusual items.

---

## **Currency**

Five coins circulate in the Known World. The gold piece is the standard unit for adventurers, and most prices in this chapter are given in gold. The names used here are generic — the GM may rename currencies to fit specific nations or regions.

All coins weigh 0.5 kg per 50 units regardless of metal.

### **Table 8.1: Exchange Rates**

|  | CP | SP | EP | GP | PP |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Copper Piece (CP) | 1 | 1/10 | 1/50 | 1/100 | 1/500 |
| Silver Piece (SP) | 10 | 1 | 1/5 | 1/10 | 1/50 |
| Electrum Piece (EP) | 50 | 5 | 1 | 1/5 | 1/10 |
| Gold Piece (GP) | 100 | 10 | 2 | 1 | 1/5 |
| Platinum Piece (PP) | 500 | 50 | 10 | 5 | 1 |

The copper and silver pieces are common currency — the coins that change hands in markets, taverns, and daily trade. The gold piece circulates among merchants, banks, and adventurers. Electrum and platinum are no longer minted; they survive only in old treasure hoards and private collections. Most merchants will accept them, but not without calculating the exchange rate and probably applying a discount for inconvenience.

Metal bars also function as portable wealth. A 500-gram gold bar is worth 50 gp. Objects, land deeds, livestock, and rare goods serve as currency in many transactions where large coin transfers would be impractical.

Starting funds represent accumulated savings, gifts, windfalls, or anything else that fits the character's background. These funds are personal — they cannot be shared or pooled during character creation, even if the characters know each other. Multiclass characters use the most favorable row.

**Holy Items.** Religious symbols, blessed water, sacred scripture, and other items sanctified by a faith. Holy items are powerless without priestly blessing through a formal ceremony. Most cannot be purchased outright — they must be requisitioned through the appropriate religious institution. Non-faithful may obtain them only when need is pressing, and the institution reserves the right to decline.

**Lanterns.** Hooded lanterns project light in a 9-metre radius and include a shutter to dim the light. Bull's-eye lanterns project a 20-metre cone 6 metres wide from a single shutter. Beacon lanterns project a 70-metre cone 30 metres wide and are mounted to buildings, ships, or wagons rather than carried. Hooded and bull's-eye lanterns burn one flask of oil every 6 hours. Beacon lanterns burn a flask every 2 hours.

**Locks.** Quality determines the difficulty for a Thief attempting to open them.

**Elephants** require dense vegetation for adequate feeding. In sparse terrain they spend most of the day eating and can only move at one-quarter normal rate. They cannot navigate steep declines without a Fortitude save against falling.

## **Weapons**

Characters may own as many weapons as they can carry, subject to encumbrance. The GM should apply reasonable judgment about what a character can realistically have accessible in combat.

**Type** indicates damage kind: Bludgeoning (B), Piercing (P), Slashing (S). Some weapons deal multiple types; a knife deals both P and S. Damage type has no general effect but matters in specific circumstances.

**Combat Speed** determines priority when initiative results tie. Lower speed strikes first in a tie. A weapon's combat speed is reduced by its enchantment bonus (minimum 0).

**Damage (M/L)** indicates dice rolled against medium-sized targets and large targets respectively.

**Rate of Fire (ROF)** for ranged weapons: two numbers separated by a slash. ROF 1/2 means one attack every two rounds; ROF 2/1 means two attacks per round.

**Range** for ranged weapons: three numbers in metres (short/medium/long). Attacks at medium range suffer −2 to the attack roll; at long range, −5. A weapon cannot be used beyond its maximum range.

**Lances.** Each lance is designed for a specific mount size. Using a lance larger than the mount can handle unseats the rider on a successful strike. Medium and heavy lances require breaking off after engagement — at least one round to resituate. Light lances allow continued engagement. On a successful hit, the GM rolls an item saving throw for medium and heavy lances; light lances save only when the modified attack roll equals exactly the target's AC.

**Man-catcher.** A long pole with spring-loaded arms designed to capture rather than harm. The target's AC is treated as 10 (modified by Dexterity) when attacked with a man-catcher. Once trapped, the victim loses their Dexterity and shield AC bonus, can only move when the wielder moves, and takes 1d2 damage automatically each round with a 25% chance of being pulled prone. The jaws can be removed with a Bend Bars roll (1d2 additional damage). The victim can pull the pole free with a Strength check; anyone can pick it up and continue using it the following round.

**Spear.** Can function as both melee and ranged weapon. If a thrown spear strikes a Huge or larger creature, or one with Strength 18/00+, the creature may attempt to break it. Intelligent creatures are 75% likely to pull the spear out and hurl it back, 25% likely to break it unless the spear saves vs. crushing blow. Unintelligent creatures have a 25% chance to break it.

## **Armor**

Wearing armor sets a character's base Armor Class to the listed value. Higher AC is harder to hit. An unarmored character has AC 10\.

If a character's AC from other sources exceeds what the armor provides, use the higher value — the armor provides no additional benefit in that case, but all other properties (weight, encumbrance, class restrictions) still apply.

The armor names here are generic. The GM may use specialized historical names where appropriate.

Armor is not equally effective against all damage types. Rigid metal plates deflect or absorb a sword's edge but transmit the force of a hammer blow directly into the wearer. Chain resists slashing but gives way to a precisely placed thrust. These interactions are captured by two tags.

* **Vulnerable:** The attacker rolls the weapon's damage dice twice and uses the higher result.  
* **Resistant:** The attacker rolls the weapon's damage dice twice and uses the lower result.

Tags apply only to the damage type listed. All other types deal damage normally. Full rules for applying these tags appear in Chapter 9: Combat.

## **Donning and Removing Armor**

Armor takes time to put on properly. Donning hastily or removing quickly are both possible with trade-offs.

| Armor Type | With Assistance | Without Assistance |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Brigandine, leather, padded, studded | 1 round | 2 rounds |
| Banded, chain, scale, splint | 2 rounds | 4 rounds |
| Plate variants | 1d6+4 rounds | Triple time |

**Hasty donning.** Non-plate armor donned hastily takes 1 round but reduces the AC bonus by 1 (minimum AC 12 — no worse than leather). Plate armor hastily donned improves AC by 1 per round of effort — each round spent adds 1 AC above base 10 until fully donned.

**Removing armor.** Non-plate takes 1 round. Plate takes 1d4+1 rounds. Plate can be cut off in half the time (round up) by cutting straps, but the armor is useless until repaired.

## **Encumbrance**

Encumbrance measures the effect of carried weight on movement and combat effectiveness. There are two distinct limits to track: **maximum weight** and **maximum carrying capacity**.

**Maximum weight** is derived from Strength (Table 1.1). Exceeding it imposes movement penalties.

**Maximum carrying capacity** is determined by the character's size and the physical bulk of their possessions — the number and size of objects, not their weight. A character may hit their weight limit before their capacity limit, or vice versa. Additional attack and AC penalties only apply when both limits are reached simultaneously.

### **Encumbrance Stages for Man-Sized Bipeds**

| Stage | Attack Modifier | AC Modifier |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Non-encumbered | — | — |
| Lightly encumbered | — | — |
| Moderately encumbered | −1 (at max capacity) | — |
| Heavily encumbered | −2 (at max capacity) | −1 (at max capacity) |
| Severely encumbered | −4 (at max capacity) | −3 (at max capacity) |
| Overwhelmingly encumbered | −4 (at max capacity) | −3 (at max capacity) |

*At max capacity* means both weight and bulk limits are reached. Either limit alone imposes only the movement penalty.

## **Spell Components**

Arcane spells draw on the world through physical materials that correspond sympathetically to the spell's nature. These materials range from items so common they are assumed to be present to singular objects that require expeditions to acquire.

**Component Tiers**

| Tier | Cost | Availability | Notes |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Common | Free – 1 gp | Anywhere | Assumed present by default. Bat guano, sand, fleece, chalk, candle wax, common herbs. Do not track. |
| Uncommon | 5 – 50 gp | Any town with a market | Powdered gemstones, rare herbs, preserved animal parts, and alchemical basics. Track as standard equipment. |
| Rare | 100 – 500 gp | Cities and specialist vendors | Quality gems, refined alchemical compounds, dragon-adjacent materials. Significant acquisition cost. |
| Exceptional | 1,000 gp+ | Quests, specific locations | Singular or irreplaceable materials. Often tied to specific narrative events. |

**Component Pouch.** A small belt pouch or satchel stocked with Common and a selection of Uncommon components appropriate to the owner's school or general practice. 

* Cost: 15 gp  
* Weight: 1 kg  
* A component pouch covers all Common components automatically and up to 10 gp worth of Uncommon components. It must be restocked after each significant adventure or downtime period. Losing a component pouch while stripped of gear means losing all Common availability — the assumption of presence no longer applies until replaced.
