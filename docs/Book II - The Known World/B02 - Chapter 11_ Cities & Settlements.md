# Chapter 11: Cities & Settlements

*The Places That Matter and Why*

 Every city in the Known World was built to solve a problem. Wintergate solved the problem of needing to move Dead Lands material out of Bellaria's northern mountains and onto the Inner Sea. Iól Vithura solved the problem of needing a place where three competing families could argue without anyone getting to declare final victory. Skyport solved the problem of keeping the Order of Magnus's most dangerous research far enough from civilian populations that an accident would affect only the people who chose to be there.

The cities in this chapter have been shaped by those founding purposes in ways that outlasted the people who made the original decisions. The Chroniclers did not build Wintergate, but they have remade it to the point where distinguishing their hand from the original architecture is an exercise for specialists. The Orchestra did not found Iól Vithura, but the city without the Orchestra is impossible to imagine now.

What follows is not a comprehensive atlas. These are the cities that campaigns gravitate toward — not because they are the most important on the map, but because they are the places where the world's current pressures produce the specific kind of friction that generates work, reputation, and sometimes, when things go badly enough, a song.

# **BELLARIA**

The kingdom's identity was built on the north — on the mountain fortresses, the cold harbors, and the Dead Lands approaches that no one else could hold. The wealth came later, when the obsidian deposits turned what had been a survivalist's geography into a prize. Bellarian cities carry both inheritances: the practical architecture of people who did not expect to be comfortable, layered with the Chronicler infrastructure that serious money builds. What neither inheritance prepared them for is the political consequence of being necessary to three different powers simultaneously.

## **WINTERGATE**

Most port cities have a face they show arriving ships and a different one they show the rest of themselves. Wintergate's division runs deeper than most. The upper city — the merchant quarter, the Chronicler Office, the customs houses — presents a functioning administrative order. The lower city, built on the tidal flats where the mountains meet the sea, runs on the same transactions without the documentation. The Chroniclers are aware of both. They have chosen to fully control one and monitor the other, which is not quite the same as tolerating it.

The city's position matters: it is the last settled harbor before Winter's Bay's approach channels become genuinely hostile, and the first harbor a ship reaches coming out of those channels with a hold full of obsidian. Every expedition to the Dead Lands launched from the Bellarian coast goes through here, and every expedition coming back passes through here on its way to markets that will pay more per kilogram than most tradespeople earn in a year. The Chroniclers understood this before the city had proper streets.

### **Economics**

Wintergate runs two economies. The licensed one — obsidian shipments, expedition contracts, crown port taxes, and Chronicler processing fees — moves the largest volume of wealth in Bellaria. The unlicensed one — Splinter Market relics, Crimson Company transit arrangements, unregistered expedition material — is smaller but untracked, and therefore impossible to squeeze in the way that licensed wealth can be squeezed.

The Chroniclers pay the Bellarian crown an annual rent for their operational presence in the port — a fixed sum negotiated once per decade rather than a percentage of their transactions. The distinction is deliberate on the Chroniclers' side: a percentage would give the crown visibility into their revenue and, by extension, their operation's scale. The annual rent gives the crown a predictable income and no visibility at all. In exchange, the Chroniclers provide two services: they act as intelligence advisors to the Portmaster, supplying assessments of expedition parties, arriving cargo, and known threat actors that the crown's own apparatus could not produce; and they hold supplemental seats on the City Council, giving them formal standing in municipal decisions without the accountability that elected representation would require.

The arrangement makes the crown dependent on Chronicler goodwill in a way that the crown has noticed and occasionally resents. It also makes Wintergate wealthy enough that the resentment remains quietly filed.

The Kilo price is posted weekly on the Chronicler Office's exterior wall — the table that tracks obsidian values by type and rarity, published by the crown's treasury office and updated from demand signals flowing primarily from the Magic Bloc. The number that expedition parties watch is the middle column: common obsidian, standard grade, the price at which the Bellarian crown buys what comes back through Frostfall. The numbers the Chroniclers and the crown's treasury analysts watch are further right, where the rare grades sit — deep-stratum material, Illyan-associated deposits — and where the margins that fund Bellaria's military apparatus are determined by how much comes out of the Dead Lands in any given month. When the rare-grade column drops, there is a meeting at Stormkeep. When it rises sharply, there is a different kind of meeting: a price spike means a significant find, and a significant find means someone was operating in a Dead Lands sector that produced something the Chroniclers have not yet classified and may not have licensed.

The Crimson Company operates in the city's lower districts with the City Council's unofficial tolerance. The council's logic is consistent: Company activity generates port traffic, port traffic generates tax revenue, and the specific goods the Company moves generate tax revenue that the crown cannot afford to lose. The Chroniclers' tolerance runs considerably shorter. The Company is their primary antagonist in the Dead Lands — the organization most responsible for smuggling unlicensed expeditions past their patrols, moving unregistered obsidian, and otherwise undercutting the access monopoly the Chroniclers have built their institutional identity around. The Chroniclers permit a minimal Company presence in Wintergate because eliminating it entirely would require enforcement action the Council would resist. They watch it with the attention they give to things they intend to address when the moment is right.

### **Politics**

Prince Andreas governs Bellaria from Stormkeep. Wintergate has its own City Council of merchant representatives, with a crown-appointed Portmaster sitting above them and the Chroniclers holding supplemental seats. The council makes decisions about municipal infrastructure, market regulation, and civic appointments. It does not make decisions about the port's operational structure, which is the Portmaster's domain, or about customs procedure, which is the Chroniclers'. In practice, the three authorities manage overlapping jurisdictions through a combination of formal meetings and informal agreements that work until they don't.

Val Cadma has a diplomatic presence in Wintergate — an ambassador maintained at the crown's sufferance, holding a post that carries little political influence and is explicitly excluded from any intelligence-advisory role. What the ambassador actually does is serve as official cover for a network of agents whose primary function is intelligence collection on the obsidian trade, expedition activity, and the Magic Bloc's supply chain through the Bellarian port. The crown knows the ambassador's secondary function in broad terms. The Portmaster knows it in specific terms. Neither has moved to expel him, because expulsion would clarify an ambiguity both sides currently find useful.

A third party — potentially a cell from the College of Whispers — is aware of the ambassador's secondary function in considerably more specific terms than either the crown or the Portmaster has acknowledged knowing. What that party intends to do with the information has not been demonstrated, which is its own form of pressure.

The Church of Tar'amaan has no official presence in Wintergate, no permission to operate there, and no prospects of obtaining either. A Cadman citizen in the city faces social friction in certain neighborhoods and indifference in others; a Church member, once identified, is escorted out of the city by whoever identifies them — a municipal officer, a dock worker, or an Iron Brothers chapter member who happened to be nearby — with a speed and consensus that suggests the city's various factions have agreed on very little except this. The Church maintains a hidden cell regardless. It has been doing so for twelve years, and the cell's primary function is identifying magic-users moving through the port and passing that information to the Cadman ambassador's network. The Chroniclers have been tracking the cell's personnel rotation for eight of those twelve years and have elected not to share that information with the crown.

### **The Customs House and the Chronicler Office**

The Portmaster is an appointed official of the Bellarian crown, answerable to Prince Andreas at Stormkeep, and his authority over the port is formally absolute. The Chronicler Office sits adjacent to the Customs House, linked by an interior corridor that the Portmaster has a key to and uses when the Chroniclers tell him to. This arrangement has been in place long enough that newer port officials understand it as simply how the port functions; the older ones understand what it replaced and are careful not to say so.

Obsidian shipments are logged twice: once for the crown's tax records and once for the Chronicler classification system, which categorizes material by estimated Dead Lands origin, expedition licensing status, and chain of custody. The second log determines whether a shipment continues to its destination or is held for further verification — a process that takes between three days and three months depending on what the Chroniclers found in the first log. Most expedition parties learn to file their documentation precisely. The ones who don't find out about the verification process directly.

The Chronicler Office is staffed by four field archivists and a senior administrator named Correlan Mast, who has held the post for nine years, speaks four languages including Val'arian, and has not spent a night outside Wintergate in the past seven. The archivists rotate through on two-year postings. They arrive thinking the customs work will be temporary before they're assigned to something more interesting. They leave having concluded that Mast's judgment about what is interesting differs substantially from their own.

### **The Sable Anchor**

A two-story inn at the edge of the upper city, close enough to the customs district that Chronicler agents eat their meals there and far enough from it that they are technically off duty when they do. The proprietor, Dagna Sveikert, is a retired Iron Brothers tracker who lost two fingers on a Dead Lands expedition fifteen years ago and invested the payout into the building she had been renting. She does not publicize either credential, but they have circulated among the mercenary community sufficiently that the Sable Anchor functions as the default hiring point for expedition-bound companies that want muscle which has already been to the Dead Lands and come back.

The board inside the main room has pegs for contract notices, available from midnight to midday before Dagna takes them down. Contracts that stay on the board past two days are either poorly worded or poorly compensated; experienced operators read the board daily. Dagna maintains three guest books — one for walk-ins, one for long-term residents, and one she keeps behind the bar and does not show anyone unless they ask twice and she has decided they are the kind of person who should be asking.

The Sable Anchor's back room is available for private meetings by prior arrangement. Dagna charges double the standard rate for the room and does not remember having rented it.

**The Brotherhood Hall** sits two streets from the Sable Anchor in the upper city, identifiable by the iron sigil above the door and by the fact that the door is rarely locked during daylight hours. The local Warden, a woman named Torva Kell who has guided twenty-three licensed expeditions and lost count of the creatures she's killed on them, maintains an open appointment book for expedition parties that want to consult the Brotherhood's creature files before descending into the Dead Lands. The consultation is free. The expectation, never stated, is that the party will report any new creature behavior they encounter when they return. Parties that don't report tend to find the files less complete on their next visit.

### **The Undertow**

Four blocks east of the fish market, a tavern occupies the ground floor of a building that has no identifying sign and insufficient business to explain its continued operation. The barkeep, who gives his name as Kolm when pressed, serves three kinds of ale, all mediocre, and a fish stew whose daily quality suggests it is assembled from whatever was not sold that morning. He does not encourage customers to stay and does not seem troubled when they leave.

The Undertow is a Crimson Company contact point. This is not common knowledge. It is, however, the kind of not-common-knowledge that people in the relevant business are aware of — the question is whether they know it in the way that causes them to visit, or in the way that causes them to walk a different street. "Kolm" receives messages through a rotating system of delivery intermediaries whose identities he does not know and does not want to. On no occasion does anything resembling cargo move through the building, because that would be the thing Chronicler patrols would watch for.

The Undertow's value to the Company is not transactions, but the knowledge that a party looking for passage or distribution outside the Chroniclers' framework can, through a conversation that sounds entirely like discussing the stew's contents, be redirected to someone who can make arrangements. Kolm never mentions names. This is the point.

### **The Splinter Market**

Below the upper city, the tidal flats district operates on a schedule that inverts the customs house's — busiest during the hours that official channels are closed. The name comes from the obsidian fragments sold openly here: small pieces, technically below the Chronicler threshold for controlled materials, practically the residue of larger shipments processed through less official channels. What the Splinter Market sells alongside the fragments is harder to classify.

Relic dealers occupy three rows of semi-permanent stalls. The merchandise is mixed: genuine Eymanarynn fragments and carved stone from the outer ruins, forgeries produced in a workshop above a tanner's on the market's eastern edge, and occasionally something legitimately unusual that a licensed expedition member failed to declare on their Chronicler form. The dealers know which category each item falls into. Their customers are not always told.

The Chroniclers are aware of the Splinter Market. They have not closed it, which tells a careful observer one of two things: either they are satisfied that nothing significant passes through it, or they have concluded it is more useful as a known quantity they can monitor than as a prohibition they cannot enforce. The dealers on the eastern edge have noticed their stalls are never raided and have arrived at their own conclusions.

### **The Chapel of the Crossed Roads**

A minor religious house on the upper city's southern edge, maintained jointly by followers of Wysla and Ayla — the sea sister and the wandering sister — in an arrangement that the Church of Tar'amaan would find theologically incoherent if they had jurisdiction here, which they do not. The arrangement suits Wintergate: most of the people in the building at any given time are either about to depart for somewhere dangerous or have recently returned from somewhere dangerous, and the distinction between asking for safe passage and giving thanks for having received it is not always clear at the moment of prayer.

The chapel maintains a book of names — expedition members lost in the Dead Lands and not recovered. Families who cannot visit contribute an entry through letter; the priest copies them in. The book is full, and a second one has been started.

### **What brings adventurers to Wintergate**

Contracts, primarily — the Chronicler Office and the Sable Anchor's board represent two ends of the same market, and most parties with Dead Lands ambitions pass through here to access one or both. The Splinter Market draws buyers looking for artifacts without the documentation that official channels require and sellers looking to move material they cannot document. The Undertow draws parties that need passage or distribution outside the Chroniclers' framework. The combination means that Wintergate is a city where most of the Known World's Dead Lands interests, legal and otherwise, intersect within a few square kilometers — and where knowing which interests are currently in conflict is worth more than the artifacts themselves.

## **FROSTFALL**

The settlement grew around the Lift, not the other way around. What started as a military post from the Northern Wars — the fortified barracks necessary to control the mountain approach to the Dead Lands interior — accumulated residents over three generations: guides who preferred to live near their work, suppliers who discovered that expeditions needed provisions they couldn't carry from Wintergate, families of people who made careers of going into Eymanarann and surviving it. Frostfall is half a day's mountain travel from Wintergate on a route safe enough that most parties manage it without incident. The town exists because the Lift exists, and the Lift exists because Eymanarann's northern cliff face drops four hundred meters in a near-vertical descent, and getting down it any other way is a decision that has killed experienced climbers.

The Lift is old — older, according to the Chroniclers' materials assessment, than the Northern Wall fortifications it runs alongside, and built with techniques that no active engineering tradition in the Known World currently uses. It is Vysandri construction. The Chroniclers hold this knowledge and the maintenance expertise that goes with it; over the years, several expeditions have been funded not for relics or obsidian but specifically to salvage replacement components from Vysandri sites in the Dead Lands interior. The guides who operate the Lift daily treat this history the way they treat most theoretical questions: it goes down and it comes back up, and everything else is the Chroniclers' problem.

### **Economics**

Frostfall's economy has exactly one source: the Lift. The Chroniclers collect the descent and ascent fees; the Bellarian military collects nothing from the mechanism itself, holding authority over the town's governance while the Chroniclers hold authority over everything that moves through it. The provisioning, guide contracts, lodging, and equipment repairs that expedition parties need before going down — and the same needs on return — support a permanent population of roughly four hundred people who have concluded that a captive market justifies the cost of living in a mountain military post.

Prices are high and everyone knows it. The Last Hearth charges what it charges because there is nowhere else. The Guides' Collective keeps rates standardized — and above what individual negotiation would produce — because the Collective has the leverage. The only genuinely negotiable prices in Frostfall are on second-hand equipment from expeditions that didn't fully return, which is Frostfall's secondary economy and the one its residents discuss least in the presence of strangers.

Obsidian recovered from the Dead Lands becomes Bellarian crown property the moment it arrives in Frostfall. Parties that bring it back cannot sell it freely; they sell it at the Kilo price, a public table that tracks the per-kilogram value of obsidian by type and rarity and that fluctuates on a schedule tied to supply from the Dead Lands and demand from the Magic Bloc. Common obsidian — the dense black glass used for standard arcane applications — trades near the table's floor. Rare grades, including the deep-stratum material associated with ancient Illyan activity, can reach thirty times that value. The table is published by the crown's treasury office, updated monthly, and consulted by every expedition party making decisions about what to carry out versus what to leave.

The Chroniclers charge customs on every relic brought out of the Dead Lands — every carved stone, every inscribed fragment, every artifact that isn't raw obsidian. Parties that pay the customs assessment receive an authorization letter allowing them to board ships in Wintergate with the cargo. Parties that don't receive the letter don't board. This is the mechanism the Chroniclers use to extend their control past Frostfall: they cannot stop unlicensed expeditions from going into the Dead Lands, but they can prevent unlicensed material from reaching markets. The Chroniclers maintain no commercial infrastructure in Frostfall and run no black market suppression operations themselves — the authorization letter requirement does that work without the overhead.

### **Politics**

The military holds governing authority in Frostfall. A garrison commander — currently Major Heln Drast, a career officer on his third northern posting — runs the town with the practical focus of someone responsible for a settlement that exists to support a strategic asset. Municipal disputes, infrastructure decisions, and security matters go through Drast's office. The Chroniclers handle customs and Lift operations without interference from the garrison, and the garrison handles everything else without interference from the Chroniclers. This division has been stable for decades, and neither side has had reason to test its limits.

The political tension that matters in Frostfall comes from above it, not within it. The Bellarian crown receives no direct revenue from the Lift fees, which flow entirely to the Chroniclers under the same arrangement that governs Wintergate's port operations. The crown's position is that it provides the territory, the military security, and the political legitimacy that makes Chronicler operations possible, and receives in return an intelligence relationship and a fixed annual rent that has not been renegotiated since it was first agreed. This calculation might be acceptable under a unified monarchy. Bellaria is governed by twin princes — Andreas at Stormkeep and Yelena at Frosthold — who share authority following their father's death under a succession arrangement that has no defined endpoint. The Aegis, the royal family's ancient shield relic missing for a thousand years, is the only mechanism that can resolve the succession: by tradition and by the legal instruments their father left behind, the prince or princess who recovers it becomes the undisputed heir. Until then, the Chronicler arrangement sits in a political holding pattern that neither prince can fully renegotiate without the other's cooperation, and that cooperation has not been forthcoming on questions of this size.

The succession's most attentive observers are not inside Bellaria. The Chroniclers have held their current rent arrangement since before the old king's death, and their institutional logic regarding its renegotiation is not difficult to follow: a split crown cannot unilaterally renegotiate anything, which means the fixed rent — favorable to the Chroniclers when it was agreed and more favorable to them with each decade it goes unrevised — stays fixed. The Cadman ambassador in Wintergate maintains a specific correspondence channel with Val Cadma's foreign ministry dedicated, among other things, to the question of which twin would prove more amenable to reconsidering Bellaria's relationship with the Magic Bloc. Prince Andreas, governing from Stormkeep alongside the Chroniclers' primary northern installations, has the longest working relationship with their intelligence services. Princess Yelena, at Frosthold in the north, has been less cooperative with that relationship and has said so in correspondence that has been cited — in edited form, through channels that reach Val Cadma's analysts — as evidence of a more tractable disposition toward negotiation with the Empire.

The Aegis has been missing for eleven centuries. The Chroniclers' Dead Lands archive contains three documents referencing its last known location. All three are classified at a tier requiring Archivistor-level access to retrieve. Whether those documents have been accessed in recent years, and by whom, is a question the archive's access logs could answer. The access logs are maintained by the Chroniclers.

### **The Lift Station**

The structure at the cliff's edge houses the Lift's operating mechanism — a counterweight system of cast materials whose precise composition remains partially unknown despite eleven years of Chronicler analysis — and the checkpoint where expedition permits are verified against the master register, updated by courier from Wintergate twice weekly. The fee is assessed per person, per piece of equipment above a standard load, and per animal. It is paid to the Chroniclers, not the crown. The Lift runs once per week: down on the fourth day, up on the fifth, unless weather makes the descent unsafe.

Groups that miss the scheduled descent wait in Frostfall. Groups in Eymanarann that miss the scheduled ascent also wait — there, not here — and the stories about what that entails have done more to ensure punctual returns than the Chroniclers' late-return fee schedule.

### **The Guides' Collective**

Dead Lands guiding is a skilled profession with minimal formal regulation and strong informal standards, most established by guides who lived long enough to establish them. The Collective is a shared logbook, a reputation system, and a meeting place in a building at Frostfall's center where guides post their rates and available dates. Members have signed its charter — vouched for by at least two existing members, with at least three guided expeditions to the outer ruins completed — and agreed to report unusual conditions they encounter in the Dead Lands to the Collective's logbook rather than exclusively to their clients.

That last condition is the contentious one. Some clients want confidential guides, and some of those clients offer significant money for it. Guides who take those arrangements and the Collective find out lose their charter. The Collective loses guides to that arrangement every few years, which tells you something about the size of the financial incentive being offered.

The senior guide operating out of Frostfall in 1,380 TE is a woman named Erika Strand, who has been inside Eymanarann's outer ruins forty-seven times, has lost six clients to the Dead Lands over that career, and has opinions about why each of those losses happened and what she would have done differently. She is available for hire and charges accordingly.

### **The Last Hearth**

Frostfall has one inn and one common room, and the common room is the inn. The Last Hearth operates as a gathering point in ways that larger cities distribute across multiple establishments: contracts negotiated, guides announcing availability, equipment evaluated for trade, and the knowledge that cannot be put in the Guides' Collective logbook circulating between people who have reason to share it.

The owner, Pask Vren, claims to have done one Dead Lands expedition in his younger years. He claims this in a way that does not invite follow-up questions about which expedition, or when, or what happened on it. He keeps a good fire, charges a fair price, and knows something about everything that has happened at the Lift for the past fifteen years.

### **The Brotherhood's Frostfall Chapter** 

It occupies a building attached to the northern end of the Guides' Collective hall — a decision that was either practical or pointed, depending on who you ask. Warden Asel Dorn maintains the chapter's Dead Lands creature files, updated monthly from reports sent by chapters operating in the southern Dead Lands sectors. The files are available to any expedition party preparing for descent, on the same terms as Wintergate's chapter: free consultation, expected reporting on return. Parties that have worked the Dead Lands before notice that Dorn's files describe recent behavioral anomalies in Darkness-corrupted creatures that the Guides' Collective logbook does not contain. The gap is not because the guides haven't seen the same things. It's because some of them have reported to the Collective and some of them have reported to Dorn, and the two organizations have not arrived at an information-sharing agreement that satisfies both sides.

### **The Wall**

On the stone face of the Lift Station's eastern exterior, expedition members have been carving names since before Frostfall existed as a town — names of people who went down and did not come back up. There is no formal organization to it, no maintained registry, just whoever had a chisel and knew someone. The oldest carvings are weathered to near-illegibility. The newest are crisp. There is always room for more.

Expedition parties tend to look at it before they go down. They tend not to mention having done so.

### **What brings adventurers to Frostfall**

Frostfall is a threshold, not a destination. Parties come here because Eymanarann is on the other side of the cliff and the Lift is the only reasonable way to reach it. What keeps people in Frostfall longer than transit time are the same things that keep any staging point populated: provisioning, guide contracts, information about current Dead Lands conditions, and the specific conversations that happen between parties about to go into the same place. The Collective's logbook is accessible to charter members; the Last Hearth's conversations are accessible to anyone who stays long enough and listens carefully enough. The Kilo price table, posted on the Lift Station's exterior wall and updated monthly, tells arriving parties what the market currently values — which is its own kind of intelligence about what other expeditions have been finding.

# **SOLARA**

The republic is three centuries old and has survived two attempted monarchist coups, the loss of its primary northern trade route when Norderenn fell to Val Cadma, and the specific institutional challenge of governing a nation that formally values debate while watching a neighbor state that has eliminated debate outperform it militarily. Solaran cities reflect this tension: built for commerce, culture, and intellectual life, quietly adding to their defenses for thirty years.

## **IÓL VITHURA**

The capital earns its title as the Known World's cultural center in the straightforward sense that most of what the Known World calls culture either originates here or is recognized here before it spreads. The Teatro Solar has been staging works for three centuries. The Order of Magnus's most accessible research facility draws scholars from every nation with money to charter passage. The Vithurian Orchestra's headquarters means that the final arbiters of who receives a song — and what the song says — sit in this city, which every ambitious mercenary company operating anywhere in the Known World is aware of.

The city is built on a bay-facing slope above the Bay of Kings, white stone architecture in the Solaran style extending up from the water in visible bands of wealth — the embankment district at the base, the commercial quarter above it, the parliamentary complex at the hill's midpoint, the estates of the three major families wrapping the upper levels in calculated asymmetry. The design is not accidental. Iól Vithura was laid out by people who understood that a republic needs to look like it works.

### **Economics**

Solara's economy runs on a mutual dependency that neither side of it can afford to acknowledge too openly. Solara imports all of its obsidian from Bellaria under a bilateral trading agreement — obsidian for food and for flying ships, neither of which Bellaria can produce for itself. Bellaria's northern soil is largely inhospitable to sustained agriculture; Solara's Emerald Plains surplus feeds it. And the flying ships that give the Magic Bloc its strategic mobility and communication advantage operate on principles that only the Order of Magnus fully understands, maintained by Order engineers whose knowledge is not available for purchase or transfer. Bellaria brings the raw material. Solara keeps the fleet flying.

The arrangement works until one side calculates that the leverage has shifted. Val Cadma's sustained pressure on Bellaria is partly aimed at exactly this calculation: if Bellaria can be convinced that the Chronicler arrangement with Solara is more burden than benefit, the obsidian supply that powers the Magic Bloc's aerial capacity disappears without a military campaign against Solara itself.

Within the city, the revenue base is unusually diversified. The Vithurian Mines produce rubies and iron of export quality; the Solaran Mining Company moves both through the port and takes a margin at every step. Harbor trade brings in luxury goods from Messalya and raw materials from Endoras. The Order of Magnus's facility generates arcane legal services revenue — spell consultations for court proceedings aren't cheap. The Orchestra and Teatro Solar generate cultural tourism that fills inns, restaurants, and the specific category of high-end shops that cater to people who came to see a première and need something to bring home.

The three families' wealth is old and institutional. They do not own specific businesses so much as they own the conditions under which businesses operate: land, harbor access, the contracts that the Mining Company's board controls. The Reformist Assembly's political project is partly about breaking that stranglehold without having the legal tools to do so directly, which means it has been running for forty years and shows no signs of resolving.

### **Politics**

The parliament's three dominant blocs are the Commerce Coalition, backed by the Oluwafemi and Diop families; the Reformist Assembly, drawing support from the professional and educated middle class; and the National Alliance, where the Diallo family's monarchist sympathies find their most direct political expression. The Commerce Coalition and the Reformist Assembly maintain an uneasy alliance on most foreign policy questions. The National Alliance holds enough seats that no budget can pass without their cooperation on at least two provisions.

The parliament's intelligence committee has been in possession of evidence connecting the Diallo family to Cadman diplomatic representatives for approximately two years. It has not acted on that evidence, and the reason is arithmetical rather than principled: pursuing the Diallo connection openly would collapse the budget coalition, freeze the Mining Company's operational contracts pending parliamentary review, and hand Val Cadma the spectacle of Solara's republic publicly tearing itself apart over a succession question that Cadman propagandists would immediately characterize as proof that democracy cannot sustain itself under external pressure. The committee has concluded that managing the Diallo situation quietly is preferable to the alternative, and the Diallo family has concluded that the committee has concluded this, and both sides are operating accordingly.

The committee has not acted publicly. Its quiet management of the situation suggests that someone with capabilities the committee lacks is providing operational support — the kind of support that leaves no parliamentary record and requires no vote.

The Orchestra occupies a distinct political position — fully independent by charter, operating from lands within Solara that were granted under an agreement that has two binding provisions. First, the Orchestra's property is held in perpetuity and cannot be taxed, appropriated, or conditionally revoked. Second, the parliament may summon an Orchestra representative to appear before the intelligence committee up to three times per year to answer questions bearing on Solaran security. The representative is obliged to attend and to answer honestly. Three questions per year, honestly answered. The Orchestra negotiated hard for that limit, and the parliament has never failed to use all three.

### **The Parliament**

The Solaran Parliament occupies the city's geographic center in a building whose construction predates the republic itself — the site was a noble assembly chamber before the revolution, and the founders chose to occupy it rather than demolish it, reasoning that taking power's buildings changes their meaning more effectively than destroying them. The Senate Chamber where the full parliament meets is on the second floor; the ground floor houses the administrative offices and the public gallery where citizens can observe proceedings, a right that is formally guaranteed and occasionally exercised by citizens whose attendance has nothing to do with civic interest.

### **The Vault and the Great Vithurian Library**

The Chroniclers' headquarters in Iól Vithura occupies a walled complex three blocks from the parliamentary district — close enough to the seat of government that the institutional relationship is visible without being stated. The building is called the Vault, and the name is accurate in the way that understatement is accurate: the structure houses what is probably the most significant collection of Eymanarynn and Vysandri primary documentation in the Known World, alongside the most valuable relics the Chroniclers have recovered from the Dead Lands. "Probably" is doing work in that sentence, because no comprehensive catalogue has been published. Independent scholars granted access to specific documents report a collection larger and better organized than they expected.

Access requires a formal application, a letter of institutional sponsorship from a recognized academic body, and approval by the senior administrator — currently Archivistor Dael Renneth, who has held the post for twenty-two years and whose decision criteria for access applications have never been fully explained. The final requirement is the Dream Orb.

The Dream Orb is an artifact of unknown origin kept in a chamber at the Vault's entrance. Visitors who seek access to the library approach it and touch it willingly — the contact is the mechanism. From each visitor, the Orb takes a memory: not their most painful, not their most valued, but something specific and private that the person realizes, in the moment of its absence, that they had never shared with anyone. A secret held entirely by them until that moment. The memory's worth to the visitor determines how long they are permitted in the library. Scholars who have traded something genuinely precious have been known to spend weeks inside. Scholars who approached the Orb with something they considered expendable found their appointment scheduled for a single afternoon.

No one has declined the Orb and been granted access. The Chroniclers do not discuss what purpose the collection serves.

What the Chroniclers do not discuss, even among lower-ranking members, is what lies beneath the Vault. In a chamber accessible only to the faction's senior hierarchy, the Oracle rests over a water font — a sphere of light roughly two meters across, shifting through colors that have no consistent pattern, hovering without apparent support. The Oracle is fed by the Dream Orb's collected memories. It answers questions about events that have occurred in the world: witnessed moments, conversations held in private rooms, decisions made without witnesses. The accuracy is not total and the mechanism is not understood even by the Archivistor who maintains access to it, but its track record across two centuries of Chronicler use is sufficient that the senior hierarchy regards it as their most important intelligence asset, more valuable than any observer network or informant arrangement. Entrance to the Oracle's chamber is granted only to full Archivistors and above. It has never been independently confirmed to exist.

### **The Vithurian Orchestra**

The Orchestra's headquarters sits in the Embankment district on the waterfront — the position reflects the organization's founding logic: the Orchestra does not govern from above, it observes from where everything passes in front of it. The building is identifiable by the bronze reliefs along its exterior depicting scenes from its most famous compositions: campaigns, final battles, the specific moments that became songs. This serves the dual purpose of artistic display and advertisement.

Inside, the organizational structure is not publicly discussed. The Composers who produce the songs work in residences throughout the city and elsewhere. The administrative staff who run the intelligence network, the archives, and the commission review process work in the building. The Conductors who oversee both functions occupy the upper floors and are not routinely accessible. Petitions for commission review — the formal process by which a mercenary company requests evaluation as a potential song subject — are accepted through a specific ground-floor office, reviewed on a schedule that is not published, and answered through intermediaries. A company submitting a petition should expect to wait. A company that lacks the patience to wait has answered one of the Orchestra's implicit questions about itself.

### **The Solar Theater**

The most prestigious performance venue in the Known World has been staging works for three centuries — original compositions, historical revivals, and the current standard for what an Orchestra commission looks like in performance. Tickets to premieres of new commissions are allocated through a system that the Teatro officially describes as a public lottery and that produces outcomes consistent enough with wealth and social connection that the lottery mechanism's credibility is maintained primarily by the sophistication of the people discussing it.

For a mercenary company, having an Orchestra commission performed at the Theater is the highest possible form of recognition — not because the performance adds to the song's official standing, but because the performance is what makes it real for everyone who wasn't there. The lobby during intermission at a commission première is among the most information-dense social environments in the Known World: everyone who matters in Solaran politics, everyone who wants to matter, and the Orchestra's audience-observation agents, who are not there for the music.

### **The Solaran Mining Company**

An official body of the republic, operating under parliamentary charter, with a board appointed through a selection process that nominally involves the Commerce Coalition but practically involves the Oluwafemi family, which has held three of the five board seats continuously for sixty years. The Company controls extraction rights to the Vithurian Mines — rubies and iron in the mountain foothills south of the city — and the infrastructure of roads, warehousing, and security that moves that material to port.

The security contracts for the road between the city and the mining district are valuable enough that two of the city's most established mercenary companies maintain retainers with the Company on a renewable basis, and a third is currently undercutting their rates in a procurement contest that has been running for four months. The Mining Company's procurement director has shown no urgency in resolving it, which the two current retainer holders have interpreted as a negotiating position and which the Mining Company's audit records, if anyone were to examine them closely, might suggest is something more complicated.

### **The Arcane Quarter**

The Order of Magnus's facility in Iól Vithura is its most accessible installation — a published address, walk-in hours, and staff whose names appear on official correspondence. The research conducted here is the Order's public-facing work: approved spellcraft research, historical analysis of magical traditions, and arcane expertise provided to Solaran legal proceedings. The sensitive research takes place elsewhere, at facilities whose locations are not published, and the Arcane Quarter is the face the Order shows a republic whose parliament votes on the Order's operating license every six years.

The facility's open-access library on magical theory is the best available outside the Chroniclers' collection, accessible to anyone with student credentials or a day-rate reading fee. Among its most consistent users are the Teatro Solar's set designers, which produces a working relationship that neither institution has found reason to discourage.

### **What brings adventurers to Iól Vithura**

The Orchestra is the simplest answer — Iól Vithura is where songs are approved and performed, and companies with enough reputation to attract evaluation will come here to navigate that process. Beyond that: the Parliament offers contracts at state scale for parties capable of operating at that level; the Vault and the Great Vithurian Library mean that research into Dead Lands history must pass through here in some form; the Mining Company's security contracts represent steady mid-level work; and the Arcane Quarter's resources draw parties with research needs that cannot be addressed in the field. The three major families produce their own contract work, some published through official channels and some not. The Diallo family, in particular, has been offering contracts through intermediaries for two years that experienced parties have learned to evaluate carefully before signing.

# **THE ORDER OF MAGNUS: DOLMAS AND ITS APPROACHES**

The Order's inner sanctum, Dolmas, does not appear on civilian maps. Most people who know its location learned it the way knowledge passes in the Order — through a process that confirmed they had already earned the right to know it. The cities that exist in its orbit exist because of the services it requires and cannot conduct inside its walls.

## **SKYPORT**

The city's name predates the Order's primary use of it — it was a navigation marker, a high promontory settlement that ships approaching from the west used to establish position before entering the Messalyan trade lanes. What it has become is the largest concentration of magical commerce in the Known World, which is a function of where its closest neighbor is and who needs to reach them.

Skyport sits two hours on horseback from Dal'barann, the port town on the coast. The road between them is unremarkable in appearance. The eleven magical traps embedded in it at irregular intervals are not visible, produce no advance warning, and are calibrated to disable rather than kill — a distinction the Order's security division has found preferable, since corpses on the road attract official attention in ways that incapacitated trespassers do not. Visitors who arrive through Dal'barann by legitimate means travel the road safely. The traps respond to the waystation's pass-tokens in the same way the Portal responds to the Gauntlet: they are not deactivated, but they do not trigger.

### **Economics**

Skyport's economy is a controlled monopoly operating behind the Order's security apparatus. The Order does not run the markets directly — it licenses the right to operate in them, and the license fees are a significant revenue stream. The three established vendors on the Market Row each pay annually for their position. New vendors can theoretically apply for a license; in practice, the approval process has not produced a new license in fourteen years, which is either because the current vendors are adequate or because the current vendors have relationships with the approval process that new applicants do not.

Dal'barann is a normal Solaran port town with normal Solaran economics. The connection to Skyport makes it busier and more prosperous than its size would otherwise justify. The waystation the Order operates there is a revenue checkpoint dressed as a travel service: pass-token fees, documentation processing, and the premium charged to parties who arrive without prior arrangement and need same-day clearance.

Housing in Skyport is priced by magical seniority in an informal but consistent way — Down Quarters rents are high but accessible; High Quarters properties are effectively controlled by the Order and allocated rather than sold on open market. Senior mages who leave Skyport for undisclosed independent research locations generate a persistent rumor economy in the Down Quarters about where they went and what they found. Some of those rumors are planted. The Order has never confirmed which ones.

### **Politics**

The Order of Magnus pays no taxes to the Solaran Republic for Skyport's operation. The arrangement that replaced that obligation is more valuable to both sides: the Order appoints a mage to serve as councillor to the Solaran Parliament, and the Parliament selects from three candidates the Order presents. The selected councillor serves at parliamentary pleasure but reports to no Solaran authority — they vote, they advise, and they provide the Order with a formal voice in Solaran legislative affairs. The conservatives inside the Order have never been comfortable with this arrangement, arguing that a councillor tied to parliamentary proceedings is a councillor whose neutrality is compromised. The progressives argue that absolute neutrality is a position that serves the Order's institutional comfort while Solara, the Magic Bloc's intellectual and logistical core, navigates an existential political threat without the Order's direct support.

The Order's other significant political asset is one that is rarely discussed openly: the flying ships. The magical principles behind their propulsion and maintenance are held exclusively by the Order's engineering division, and even the Chroniclers — who have studied the ships for decades — lack the knowledge to keep them operating without Order involvement. This makes every nation that depends on aerial capacity dependent on the Order's goodwill, and the Order's position of formal neutrality is substantially underwritten by the fact that the major powers have concluded that antagonizing the Order has a clear downside and an unclear upside.

Inside the Order, a political division has been running for roughly thirty years and has accelerated in the past decade. The conservative faction maintains that the Order's value derives from its independence — from being the institution that no power can fully claim and that therefore serves as a stabilizing force. The Order does not take sides. It trains mages, maintains knowledge, and keeps the ships flying. The progressive faction, drawn largely from younger mages, argues that this position has become an excuse to avoid difficult choices: that the Trials of Magnus are brutal in ways that produce capable mages and damaged people simultaneously; that the Order's refusal to authorize expeditions to recover lost relics — including potential Vysandri knowledge caches — is institutional cowardice dressed as caution; that the Order's interest in Mynyaraan, the pre-Darkness elvish city preserved in Alvengaard's Húldar territory, should be pursued through diplomacy rather than the unauthorized access that conservative members have been advocating for quietly. The conservative position on Mynyaraan is that the political complications of pursuing Húldar authorization are not worth the delay. The progressive position is that the political complications of proceeding without it are exactly the kind of thing that destroys institutions.

The division between the Down Quarters and High Quarters is the spatial expression of this conflict. Junior mages in the Down Quarters trend progressive; senior mages in the High Quarters hold both positions, but conservative voices are louder there because conservative voices have had longer to become the voices that matter. The Steward who administers Skyport is a High Quarters appointment and manages the city with a conservative orientation that produces specific decisions about license approvals, quota allocations, and Down Quarters infrastructure investment that the junior mages' association has been formally contesting for six years.

### **The Portal to Dolmas**

A stone arch in Skyport's eastern district, without visible markings, set into the wall of a building the Order uses for administrative functions. The Portal is permanently active — correctly understanding it as a door requires abandoning most intuitions about what open doors look like — but passage through it is only possible for someone holding a Gauntlet and holding it willingly. The Gauntlet is the sigil earned by mages who complete the Trials of Magnus, inscribed on the mage's dominant hand: perceptible to touch but invisible to non-magical sight. It cannot be transferred, removed, or replicated; it responds to the bearer's life-force rather than their decision-making, which is why the Portal requires willing passage. An unconscious or coerced bearer does not produce the response the Portal needs. This is the security design, not a loophole in it.

### **The Markets**

Skyport's commercial district is expensive in a way that requires context to fully appreciate: the magical components, tools, and worked items available here are priced relative to a customer base whose alternative purchasing option is a flying ship charter to somewhere with lesser selection. The three established vendors on the Market Row — Edara's Materials, the Compound Yard, and the establishment that everyone calls the Old Black and that has no sign — have maintained their positions through genuine quality, sole-source access to certain materials, and the specific social capital that comes from having served Order members long enough to know how to do so correctly.

The Old Black sells items whose origin documentation is not a topic for discussion. The proprietor, who calls herself Mirven, is approximately sixty years old, has no documented magical ability, and has been operating in Skyport longer than most current Order members have been alive. The Order is aware of her operation. She is aware that they are aware. This arrangement has continued for over two decades through a mechanism that neither party has seen fit to explain.

### **The Eyepoint**

On Skyport's western edge, a cleared promontory with benches arranged facing west provides a direct sightline to the Eye of Magnus, the permanent storm system over Dolmas — visible as a rotating atmospheric formation roughly sixty kilometers off the coast. In clear weather, it appears as a grey-green column spanning from cloud level to the sea, with occasional flashes of light deep within it that scholars who have written about the phenomenon disagree about the source of. The Eyepoint fills at dusk, when the light conditions make the Eye most dramatic. Rich merchants, traveling nobles, scholars who have made the journey specifically to see it — the Order treats this tourist function as useful: it provides cover for the travel patterns of people who actually have business at the Portal, and it produces a category of visitor who is thoroughly uninteresting to anyone conducting surveillance.

### **Down Quarters and High Quarters**

Skyport's residential districts divide by magical seniority in a way that has calcified into neighborhood identity and political alignment. The Down Quarters occupy the lower streets: junior mages maintaining apartments and laboratories, working component preparation, spell scribing contracts, and research assistant positions for senior members who can outsource parts of a project. The work pays adequately and provides access to the networks a junior mage needs to advance. The Down Quarters' junior mages' association negotiates scribing rates collectively, disputes housing allocations with the Steward's office, and holds a political orientation that the Steward describes as idealistic and that the Steward's critics describe as correct.

Mages who reach senior level move to the High Quarters, with larger properties and the privacy that extended independent research requires, or to undisclosed locations elsewhere in the Known World. The distinction between mages who leave for locations the Order knows about and mages who leave for locations the Order does not is, from the Order's perspective, the more important one. The High Quarters do not discuss the second category.

### **The Gauntlet Registry**

A small administrative office attached to the Portal building, where mages who complete the Trials receive their formal Registry entry confirming Adept status. The Registry records suspension and revocation as well: a suspended entry means the Portal will not respond until the suspension is lifted; a revoked entry means the inscription has been formally dissolved and the mage is no longer recognized by the Order in any official capacity. Revocations are rare and known within Order circles within days. The process requires the Archmagister's direct authorization. There have been four in the past thirty years. All four involved actions that the Order found incompatible with continued membership — professionally and, in three cases, in ways that the Order's internal records describe with careful specificity and that have not been disclosed publicly.

### **What brings adventurers to Skyport**

The markets are the primary draw for parties with magical needs and the funds to address them at Skyport's prices. The Portal is the draw for mages whose business is in Dolmas and who have a Gauntlet to use it. The Eyepoint draws visitors whose function is, mostly, to be there — though the information available in a tourist crowd composed of the Known World's wealthier travelers has not gone entirely unnoticed by parties who collect information for a living. The Down Quarters attract mages at the career point where proximity to the Order's resources is worth the cost of Skyport's rents. The Old Black attracts parties who need materials that proper channels will not provide and have the judgment to use them correctly — or who lack that judgment and are about to discover the consequences.

# **FOR THE GM: CITIES AS CONTESTED GROUND**

The cities above share a structural property: the visible authority in each of them is not identical to the actual authority. The Portmaster in Wintergate and the crown he reports to have formal jurisdiction; the Chroniclers have operational control. The Parliament in Iól Vithura makes policy; the three major families execute it, contest it, and in one case actively undermine it. The military holds governing authority in Frostfall; the Chroniclers hold everything that moves through it. The Order of Magnus keeps Dolmas inaccessible and Skyport useful, and the line between those two functions is managed rather than natural.

Running these cities well means running the gap between visible and actual authority as a consistent presence. When the party needs something in Wintergate, the first question is whether they need the Portmaster's authority or the Chroniclers' — and the answer will tell them who they're actually negotiating with.

## **Information Has Price and Source**

Each of these cities has an informal information economy operating alongside the visible one. The Sable Anchor's back room, the Last Hearth's common room conversations, the Teatro Solar's lobby during intermission, the Old Black's proprietor — these are the places where what is actually happening circulates. Parties that invest in these networks will get better intelligence than parties that rely on official sources. They will also become known to the people who run those networks, which has its own consequences.

## **Chronicler Presence Is Never Passive**

Wherever the Chroniclers operate — Wintergate's Customs House, Frostfall's checkpoint, the Vault in Iól Vithura — they are not only administering access. They are observing, recording, and adjusting their assessments of every party that passes through. A party that has completed multiple licensed expeditions has a Chronicler file. A party that has triggered confiscation orders or arrived with unlicensed material has a different Chronicler file. The Chroniclers' response to a given party depends on what is in that file, and the party may not know what is in it. The Oracle, if the rumors about it are accurate, knows considerably more.

## **The Order Is Watching the Same Things Everyone Else Is Watching**

The Order's political split between conservative and progressive factions is not an internal abstraction — it produces specific decisions that affect parties operating in Skyport or seeking Order support elsewhere. A party that has been denied a relic recovery authorization by a conservative Steward might find a different reception from a progressive mage operating outside Skyport's formal structure. A party that gets involved in the Mynyaraan question — whether the Order should pursue unauthorized access to Húldar territory — has entered one of the Order's live political contests, and the side they appear to support will determine which Order members treat them as useful and which treat them as a problem.

## **Cities Generate Their Own Pressure**

Frostfall is a staging point, which means it is full of parties either about to go into a dangerous place or having just come back from one — and the information differentials between those two groups are significant and exploitable. Skyport is a city of people with serious magical ability and varying commitments to institutional loyalty, which produces specific kinds of conflict that do not resolve through conventional means. Wintergate is where the obsidian economy's above-ground and below-ground operations are physically adjacent, which means choosing one involves the awareness of the other. The specific texture of each city is the tool — not the generic fact that there is a city there.

| GM NOTE: The Kilo and the Dead Lands Economy *The obsidian price table — "the Kilo" — is a useful tool for establishing the economic stakes of a Dead Lands expedition without requiring detailed accounting at the table. Before a session involving a significant obsidian recovery, decide roughly how many kilograms of which grade the party might find and check where the Kilo price currently sits. Even approximate numbers (this load, at current prices, is worth roughly what a skilled craftsperson earns in two years) help players understand what they are risking their lives for and make the crown's and Chroniclers' competing interests feel concrete. When the Kilo price fluctuates dramatically — due to a major expedition finding or a supply disruption — that fluctuation is world news, and the party probably hears about it before they get back to Frostfall.* |
