# Chapter 2: History

### ***The Ages of the Known World — What Happened, What Remains, and What Hasn't Healed***

---

History in the Known World is not a neutral record. Every major power uses the past to justify what it is doing right now, and every account of the past reflects the interests of whoever preserved it. Val Cadma cites the Second Era's horrors to argue that magic must be eradicated before it invites another catastrophe. Solara cites the same horrors to argue that the knowledge to prevent them must be preserved and studied. The Chroniclers cite both and then lock the primary sources in King's Hold.

What follows is the account the scholarly consensus — such as it is, contested and incomplete — has arrived at after eleven centuries of argument. Where the sources disagree, this chapter says so. Where the evidence thins into rumor, this chapter says that too.

The safest thing to tell a player who wants to know the real history of the Known World is this: there isn't one. There are competing versions, and the version a person has been raised on tells you more about their politics than about the past.

---

### **Before Memory: The First and Second Eras**

The First Era left almost nothing. The Eymanarynn and the Vysandri were its dominant peoples — the first to awaken, the most developed before everything collapsed — and the ruins they left behind are what the Chroniclers mine for obsidian and the Ordo Draconis mines for ideology. The elves and dwarves were present. Humans arrived near the end, barely established, still finding their footing when the Second Era broke over everything.

The Second Era is covered in Chapter 1 as cosmological history. Its political consequences need stating plainly here.

The Darkness's rule was not a single catastrophic event. It was centuries of subjugation. Dwarves and humans were enslaved. The elves who survived did so by making choices — some chose resistance, some chose accommodation, and the community they formed after has never fully decided what to do with that distinction. The Vysandri disappeared. No body of records survives to say how or why — and the ruins they left behind do not read the way the Darkness's violence reads elsewhere. The marks of destruction, forced removal, the specific damage military power leaves in a place it has moved through: none of that is present in the deep Vysandri sites. What is present is something closer to departure. Structures sealed rather than breached. Tools set down mid-use. An absence that suggests something was taken out of them, and they continued to function, simply without whatever had been taken. The expeditions into Vysandárien keep going because the question of what that something was has not been answered, and the mechanisms still running in those ruins suggest the answer would be worth finding.

The Eymanarynn led the resistance, fought the war, and won it at the cost of Ilya's presence in the mortal world. The dominant account holds they were consumed by the weight of the final conflict — that what destroyed them was the magnitude of what they fought and what it cost. A minority of historians, working from the same fragmentary sources, argue that the end does not read like destruction. That the final accounts describe not a people being overrun but a people preparing. That the consumption, if that is the right word, was voluntary — that the Eymanarynn chose what happened to them, and chose it in full knowledge of what it would produce. The sources do not close the question. They simply stop.

The survivors of the Eymanarynn — those who had fled the final confrontation rather than died in it — were condemned by Ilya before she descended into the Abyss. Their descendants became the Vartrans of Bal'mekh, stripped of the memory of their original civilization and rejected by what that civilization left behind. The ruins in the Dead Lands behave strangely around Vartran blood — mechanisms dormant for three thousand years will activate against them; passages that open for anyone else will not open for them. Whether this is Ilya's condemnation made structural, or something stranger, the curse's exact terms have been debated by Ilianist theologians for a thousand years without resolution, and Val Cadma's Church ignores the question in favor of a simpler reading: draconic ancestry, therefore heresy.

These are the wounds the Third Era was born with. They have not closed.

### **The Long Recovery**

The century immediately following the Sacrifice — roughly the first hundred years of what scholars now call the Third Era — was not a time of rebuilding. It was a time of hiding.

The Darkness was gone, but its influence was not. Corrupted animals moved through forests that had been under the Darkness's rule for generations. Undead walked in the places where the worst of the war's violence had happened. The mortal races that survived the Second Era retreated into the defensible spaces they could hold — mountain valleys, river confluences, coastlines with natural harbors — and waited for the open world to become survivable again.

Nations did not form quickly. What formed first were communities of survival: the group of humans who made it through winter together, the elvish families who kept to the deep forest and refused contact with outsiders, the dwarven clans who sealed the lower galleries of their mountain holds and waited. These clusters became the ancestral communities of the nations that exist now, which is why the Known World's nations look the way they do — not drawn by geography or ideology, but by whoever happened to be in the same defensible location when the monsters came.

The Ûrgons and Badakis, created by the Sisters specifically for the war, found themselves in a world where the war was over and they had been built for nothing else. That they became functioning nations at all — Urgônia and Gwan'tor, stable and distinct — is the detail Third Era historians tend to understate. The arc is more impressive than the outcome suggests.

By roughly three hundred years into the Third Era, the major population centers were established. Trade routes were forming between them. The mortal world had recovered enough that people were arguing about property and succession rather than surviving winter.

The arguments that followed were, in several cases, worse than winter.

### **The God That Rose From the Ruins**

Tar'amaan did not begin as a conquest theology. That needs saying clearly, because the Church's current behavior makes its origins almost unrecognizable.

The earliest records of Tar'amaan worship date to the first century of the Third Era, among human communities in what is now Val Cadma's heartland. These were people who had survived the Darkness's rule, who had watched what magic wielded without conscience or oversight had done to the world, and who were looking for something that explained why the Illyans — divine creatures, created by a goddess, entrusted with stewardship — had been capable of the Second Era at all.

The answer they arrived at was the sun. Specifically: that the Darkness's power had been most complete at night, that the sun was the one thing in the world that nothing had been able to corrupt or subjugate, that Tar'amaan — the god embodied in that light — was the force that had persisted through the Second Era when every other power, including Ilya herself, had either failed or paid an impossible cost. The theology was, in its earliest form, a reasonable response to genuine trauma. People who had lived under the Darkness were suspicious of magic, of things that operated beyond mortal comprehension, of the argument that power should be trusted to those who understood it. Tar'amaan offered simplicity: the sun rises, the light comes, the Darkness cannot follow it.

What the Church became over the following centuries is not what it was. The transformation is not a mystery — it follows the same logic that turns every sufficiently successful institution in the same direction — but it is worth naming. The early Church offered comfort. The later Church discovered that defining who was outside its protection was more powerful than defining who was inside it. The theology did not change. The application did.

By seven hundred years into the Third Era — approximately when the Val'arians were arriving on the Known World's eastern shores — the Church of Tar'amaan had developed its doctrine of magical corruption: the argument that the practice of magic is not merely dangerous but spiritually polluting, that magic-users draw on the same source as the Darkness drew on, and that tolerating them in a society is equivalent to welcoming the Second Era back. More than fringe theology, it was the official position of an institution with substantial political reach.

From there, the slide to inquisition was short, and the slide from inquisition to military conquest was shorter. Archbishop Kranzler did not invent the Church's expansionism. He is its current expression. He believes, sincerely, that he is protecting the world from another Darkness. That sincerity is not a comfort to the people of Norderenn, but it explains why he functions as he does — why the Church cannot simply be outnegotiated, why the people who serve it are not acting from naked cynicism, and why the war it is preparing to fight will not end with a peace treaty.

Val Cadma did not invent religious war, but the Cadmans have refined it into something their predecessors would recognize with admiration and their victims with despair.

### **The Elves and Their Long Memory**

The Aelár and the Húldar share the same root. The Húldar are the original elvish people of Alvengaard, the forest nation that has stood since before the Darkness fell. The Aelár are their descendants — those who left.

The departures happened across centuries and for different reasons. Some were expelled by the Hyerophancy for refusing the druidic tradition. Some left voluntarily, preferring integration into the human world over the slow isolation that Alvengaard's politics produced. Some simply drifted — moved by commerce or marriage or the specific restlessness that afflicts people who can see past the treeline and choose not to. Over enough time, the divergence became biological. The Aelár who spent generations in human cities, adapting to different climates and different diets and different social pressures, became something distinct from their cousins who remained. The Húldar find the word "cousins" inadequate. The Aelár find the Húldar's preferred framing — "those who left" — a reduction of a more complicated history.

The Húldar's current isolation in Alvengaard is simultaneously voluntary and enforced. They are not welcome in most of the Known World's cities. The Church of Tar'amaan classifies them, like all elves, as heretics, but reserves a particular accusation for the Húldar: that their practice of Vengardian magic — raw, druidic, outside the Church's theological framework — constitutes a direct rejection of Tar'amaan order. The Húldar themselves do not help their case. They practice that magic openly, and have no particular interest in performing innocence for an audience that has already decided their guilt. Their numbers have been falling for three hundred years by conservative accounting, longer by the Húldar's own. No study has identified a cause. The Húldar, when asked, give answers that amount to grief, which is not informative but is probably honest.

What they know is more concerning than what they do. The Húldar's territory borders Mynyaraan, an elvish city from the pre-Darkness era that their ancestors managed to preserve, and which contains records from before the Second Era that exist nowhere else. The Chroniclers have been trying to gain access to Mynyaraan for two centuries, for which the Húldar have been declining since then. Whatever the city contains, both parties have concluded that knowledge is worth a great deal of discomfort.

The Aelár's relationship with this situation is complicated. They benefit from not being the Húldar — from the distinction the rest of the Known World draws between the elves who integrated and the elves who didn't. They are also not blind to the fact that the distinction carries a judgment their ancestors did not fully earn, and that the Húldar are bearing accusations that belong to a more complicated history than the Church prefers to tell. Some Aelár say this privately. Very few say it publicly.

### **The Sea Delivers Strangers**

Roughly seven hundred years ago — midway through the Third Era, long enough back that the event itself has become contested legend rather than verified record — ships appeared on the Known World's eastern shores that did not match any design the local peoples recognized.

The Val'arians who came off those ships were competent, composed, and remarkably incurious about explaining themselves. They established communities near the dwarves of Endoras and the Vartrans of Bal'mekh without apparent difficulty, trading navigational knowledge and maritime techniques for the space to settle. They did not ask for territory. They worked within whatever political arrangements existed in the regions where they arrived. They were, by every contemporary account, good neighbors who happened to have come from somewhere nobody knew.

Two centuries ago, after five hundred years of steady presence, they established the kingdom of Val'aria. The timing suggests the decision was deliberate — not the organic consolidation of a people who had finally grown large enough for statehood, but a calculated move by people who had been waiting until their position was secure enough to formalize. Val'arian historians have offered other interpretations. None of them are entirely convincing.

Where they came from, what circumstances drove them across the sea, and what they knew when they left are questions the Val'arians have declined to answer for seven centuries. The Faith of the Nine — their religion, with its Chorus of Nine divine beings who sang the world into being — does not map onto Ilianist or Tar'amaan theology in ways that illuminate their origins. It does suggest they came from somewhere where the cosmological history looks different than it does in the Known World, which is either evidence that their homeland had a genuinely different creation or evidence that the Known World's cosmological consensus is not as complete as it believes.

The Val'arians are not hostile. They are reserved about specific things. Those things are presumably related.

### **The Dragon Glass**

The obsidian economy is young, measured against the Known World's history. The grandparents of people alive now can remember — or remember being told — what travel looked like before a handful of flying ships changed everything.

The discovery that obsidian extracted from the Dead Lands could power airborne vessels was made by the Order of Magnus, working from components recovered from the ruins that the Chroniclers had supplied in exchange for research they wanted conducted at arm's length. The Order's engineers understood the components well enough to build around them — wooden hulls and rigging, the familiar parts of a ship married to things that should not, by any reasoning the engineers could articulate, have produced flight. What the components actually were, what principles governed their function, and why Dead Lands obsidian specifically powered them when nothing else did: these questions the Order documented carefully and did not answer. The Chroniclers understood immediately what the discovery meant. They moved to control access to the Dead Lands before the Order's findings were public.

Bellaria, which sits at the Dead Lands' geographic gateway, became the leverage point. The Chroniclers established their base at King's Hold, offered Bellaria economic partnership in exchange for administrative cooperation, and within a generation had turned a scholarly organization into the most consequential gatekeeper of strategic resources in the Known World. The transformation was clean, institutional, and entirely self-interested. The Chroniclers would say they were preserving dangerous knowledge from dangerous hands. They are not wrong that the knowledge is dangerous. They are not disinterested parties.

The airships that resulted — flying ships, a handful of them, eye-wateringly expensive — did not change the Known World's economy by moving goods. They changed it by moving the *meaning* of distance. A diplomat who can cross the Known World in days rather than weeks operates differently from one who cannot. An army whose commanders can coordinate across regions without waiting for messengers operates differently. A scholar who can reach a site in the Dead Lands and return within a season can conduct research impossible for those who must plan a year in advance.

What the obsidian economy did was make the gap between those who had it and those who didn't visible in ways it hadn't been before. Solara has it. Bellaria controls it. Val Cadma does not, and the Church's position on magic — that it is corrupting, that it draws on the same power as the Darkness — happens to align very conveniently with Val Cadma's material interest in denying its enemies a resource they cannot currently match. Whether the theology followed the strategy or the strategy follows the theology is a question the Cadman court has presumably answered to its satisfaction and declined to publish.

### **Fifteen Years**

Norderenn was founded by Boris, a prince of Bellaria, who took a portion of his family's followers north and east and built a kingdom out of forested hills and cold rivers that nobody else had formally claimed. The kingdom was two generations old when Val Cadma invaded.

The occupation is fifteen years old. That is long enough for children to have grown up knowing nothing else. Long enough for some of the occupied population to have built lives under Cadman administration — not from enthusiasm, but because the alternatives were unemployment, exile, or the resistance, and not everyone can afford any of those. Long enough for the Kronn, Norderenn's currency, to have been officially abolished and its circulation criminalized, meaning that using a Norderenn coin in a Norderenn market is now an act of cultural defiance with legal consequences attached.

King Boris I governs from Castle Jaeger in the north, controlling what territory his military can hold. The Cadman administration controls the rest. The Cadman-aligned Archbishop governing the occupation has authority over inquisitional proceedings, which means authority over who is declared a heretic and who is merely a collaborator, and that distinction has become the primary tool of political control in the occupied south.

The Chroniclers, characteristically, maintain operations in Norderenn through both the resistance and the occupation simultaneously. They have taken no position on the political question. They are interested in the Dead Lands access routes that pass through Norderenn, and both sides of the conflict need them more than they need the conflict resolved.

Bellaria has not sent troops. The occupied territory borders the Dead Lands access routes Bellaria controls, and a Cadman military already present in Norderenn is one that could redirect at Bellaria with much less travel time than would otherwise be required. They are weighing options. The people of Norderenn are living in the gap between Bellaria's calculations and the moment those calculations produce a decision.

None of this is abstracted by being in a fantasy world. The mechanics of occupation, the economics of collaboration, the difficulty of choosing resistance when the resistance cannot guarantee it will win — these are recognizable. Write them that way at the table.

### **Year 1,380**

The Third Era numbers its years from the Sacrifice. Year 1 was the year Ilya descended into the Abyss and the Darkness went with her. Year 1,380 is now.

The known fault lines: Val Cadma has completed the first phase of a strategic encirclement and is preparing the second. The obsidian economy has concentrated enough power in Bellaria that every other nation has noticed, and several are actively working to break the monopoly, find a substitute, or steal enough to stop being dependent. The Emerald Corridor Alliance — Endoras, Gwan'tor, and Urgônia — is the most functional inter-racial political arrangement in the Known World's history and has between two and three years before the pressures currently destabilizing it produce a crisis it cannot manage. Val'aria is in a civil war. Bal'mekh knows it is a target and has run out of time to decide between its options.

The parties who might stop any individual thread from pulling loose are occupied with the others. Solara is the intellectual center of the resistance to Val Cadma and currently cannot get Alvengaard to commit to a coordinated response because Alvengaard's internal Aelár/Húldar tension makes commitments difficult and because the Húldar's Second Era history gives Val Cadma's propaganda a ready-made lever. Messalya's city-states have factions being quietly courted by Val Cadma's agents with offers of autonomous commercial arrangements that sound better than the alliance they're already in.

The Chroniclers are in the middle of all of it and have taken no position on any of it, which is itself a position.

Adventurers moving through the Known World in Year 1,380 are moving through a place that is about to change and that does not yet know in which direction. That is the right time to be a mercenary. The jobs are real, the stakes are legible, and the people giving the orders have comprehensible reasons for what they want.

Whether those reasons are good ones is, as always, a separate question.

### **For the GM: History as a Tool, Not a Lecture**

A player who has read this chapter knows the framework. A player who has not read it will still encounter history at the table through NPCs who have opinions, ruins that have histories, and factions whose conflicts have causes. Both players can function. The chapter is background, not homework.

**Use specific historical wounds as NPC motivation.** An Aelár scholar who flinches when someone mentions the Húldar too casually is a character. A Norderenn blacksmith who will accept Cadman coins but gives worse work to Cadman customers is a character. These are not moral lessons delivered by the setting — they are people shaped by history behaving consistently with that shaping.

**The Kronn are black-market currency.** Norderenn coins have been officially abolished for fifteen years, which means they circulate illegally at a discount among people who consider using them a political act and among people who just need change and don't care about politics. A pocket full of Kronn tells you something about whoever's carrying them, and different things depending on where they're carrying them.

**Tar'amaan's sincerity is the point.** The Church is not cynical at the street level. The Acolyte who reports a magic-user to the Inquisição believes they are protecting their community. The Lorde Solar, who authorizes a purification rite, believes in the doctrine that authorizes it. Playing these figures as cartoonishly evil misses the actual horror — that the institution produces true believers who do terrible things with clean consciences. The cynicism, where it exists, lives at the top, and even there it is wrapped in enough theology that the people holding it can't always tell the difference anymore.

**The obsidian economy has a floor price.** A single obsidian crystal costs between 120 and 240 silver pieces — roughly one year's wages for a skilled artisan. This is not an abstraction. When a flying ship passes overhead, the people watching it from the ground know exactly what that passage cost and what it means that someone had the money to pay for it. Let that knowledge shape how NPCs relate to the technology.

**Val'arian NPCs know something.** Not necessarily something that will resolve in this campaign or this session, but something. The knowledge is present in how they change the subject. Play it that way.
