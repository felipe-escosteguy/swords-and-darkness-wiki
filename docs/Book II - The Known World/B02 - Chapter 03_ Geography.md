# Chapter 3: Geography

### ***Regions, Climate, Trade Routes, and What Lies Between***

---

The Known World is built around water. The Inner Sea is its center of gravity: the body of water that the largest nations border, that the most important trade routes cross, and that every major military strategy in the current crisis is designed to control, deny, or circumvent. The nations that ring its shores are the Inner Nations — Solara, Bellaria, Val Cadma, Norderenn, Alvengaard — and they are the ones whose rivalries and alliances define the present moment. Beyond them, the Outer Nations — Messalya, Endoras, Urgônia, Bal'mekh, Gwan'tor, Val'aria — occupy the world's edges, far enough from the Inner Sea's center that they built their own logic, close enough that the center's instability reaches them anyway.

Then there are the Dead Lands, which are not a nation and have no logic a living government can claim. Eymanarann and Vysandárien sit at the world's northern extreme, past the point where sustained habitation is possible, and they do not belong to the political map. They belong to the past. Every power in the Known World is currently arguing about who gets to extract value from them.

This chapter describes the physical world: what each region looks like, who controls it, what makes it strategically important, and what brings adventurers through it. The major cities and named sites within each region are covered in Chapter 11\. What follows is the territory those cities sit inside.

---

### **Climate**

The Known World runs cold. This is the first thing a traveler from a temperate background notices, and it does not stop being relevant.

The northernmost regions — Eymanarann, Vysandárien, and the northern edge of Bellaria — are functionally uninhabitable for extended periods. Eymanarann's interior sustains temperatures of −40°C to −60°C, compounded by magical storms that appear without warning and carry obsidian fragments in the wind. Once a year, the great winter storms that churn perpetually over Eymanarann push south, burying Norderenn, Val Cadma, and Solara under weeks of ice. Communities in these regions build accordingly: deep cellars, covered markets, livestock quarters connected to living quarters by interior passages. A traveler who arrives in Solara in midsummer and returns in deep winter is visiting two different cities.

Endoras is the anomaly. The mountain range that borders it to the north acts as a barrier against the polar winds, producing a climate mild enough for year-round agriculture and warm enough that the pink fruit trees along the mountain's lower slopes — yielding fruit dense enough in nutrients to sustain a family from a single harvest — can survive without shelter. The Endorans are aware that this is unusual. So is everyone else.

The south grows warmer. Messalya's desert reaches temperatures that render midday travel dangerous for the unprepared, and Gwan'tor's arid mountain terrain combines altitude cold with sun exposure in ways that require specific adaptation. Val'aria and Urgônia occupy a middle band — neither arctic nor desert — that is the closest the Known World comes to temperate in the conventional sense.

The Inner Sea itself moderates the climate of the nations that border it, keeping winters marginally less severe on the coast than inland. This is one of the reasons the coastal cities are where they are.

---

### **The Inner Sea**

The Inner Sea is not large enough to be an ocean and not small enough to be a lake. It is large enough that crossing it requires a ship and a crew who know what they are doing, large enough that the weather over it develops independently of the coast, and divided by its geography into three bodies that behave differently from one another.

**The Bay of Kings** occupies the Inner Sea's southern portion, bordered by Solara to the west, Val Cadma to the east, and Alvengaard's northern coast to the south. It is the busiest bay in terms of commercial traffic and the most politically fraught. The current arrangement — an informal truce between three powers who cannot afford open war in each other's home waters — holds because all three fear what the others would do to their coastal infrastructure if it broke down. It holds, as these arrangements tend to, more from mutual deterrence than mutual trust. The bay's major ports handle the bulk of the Known World's luxury trade: rubies from Solara's Vithurian Mines, elvish components from Alvengaard, and finished goods from Val Cadma's manufacturing centers. The Cadman warships that patrol their portion of the bay are not there to suppress piracy.

**Winter's Bay** lies to the northwest, its waters cold enough that ice floes are a navigation hazard for three months of the year. Bellaria's coast defines one shore; the bay's northern edge bleeds into the open sea beyond which the Dead Lands lie. The Crimson Company operates freely here, although avoiding entering the heavily patrolled bay itself, exploiting its treacherous approach channels — particularly the stretch called the Eye of Darkness, which requires local knowledge or a native pilot to navigate safely — as protection against Chronicler patrols. The Company's de facto headquarters, Wightport, sits in a natural harbor further west of it, accessible only through those channels. Bellaria's formal navy does not go there. Whether this reflects an inability or arrangement depends on who you ask.

**The Bay of Dragons** separates Val Cadma's northern territories — which now includes Norderenn’s coast to the east — from the remainder of the Known World. Its name predates the current conflict by several centuries — the bay's deep waters are associated, in older accounts, with marine creatures that respond to arcane calls — but the name fits the current situation as well as the historical one. Val Cadma patrols these waters heavily, and to reach the coast of Norderenn without proper authorization is a dangerous — and very expensive — endeavor taken almost solely by the Crimson Company, for a handsome amount of gold.

---

### **The Emerald Corridor**

If the Inner Sea is the world's center of gravity, the Emerald Corridor is its spine. The Emerald River runs from the Eastern Sea to the Bay of Kings — the longest river in the Known World, passable by barge for most of its length, cutting through Urgônia, Alvengaard, and Endoras before it reaches the sea. The trade route that uses it as its backbone connects the eastern regions of the world to Solara and the Inner Sea in a continuous chain of river traffic, overland legs, and portage points.

Endoras sits at the Corridor's geographic center, which is one reason the nation matters beyond its agriculture. The river passes through it. So does every commercial caravan that does not want to pay Bay of Dragons crossing fees or navigate Winter's Bay ice. Control of the Corridor means influence over the food supply and the trade supply simultaneously, which is why Val Cadma's economic pressure on Endoras is not simply opportunism, but strategy. If Endoras can be forced into the Cadman orbit, the Corridor comes with it, and the Outer Nations become dependent on Cadman goodwill for access to the Inner Sea.

Travel along the Emerald Corridor by barge, with favorable current, covers roughly 40 to 50 kilometers per day. The full length of the navigable river — from the eastern ports to the Bay of Kings — takes six to eight weeks under normal conditions. In winter, sections freeze. In spring, sections flood and run fast enough to make downstream travel dangerous. The river has moods, and experienced barge crews know them.

### **The Inner Nations**

#### **Solara**

Solara is a republic in a world that does not have many of them, and it has spent the better part of three centuries making the idea work. The capital, Iól Vithura, sits on the southern coast of the Bay of Kings — a city of white stone and open forums where the parliament meets, where the Teatro Solar stages productions that have made Solaran opera the cultural benchmark for nations that affect to dislike Solaran politics, and where the Order of Magnus maintains its most accessible research facility. The countryside inland from Iól Vithura produces grain and olives; the Vithurian Mines in the foothills yield rubies and iron of quality high enough to attract buyers from across the Inner Sea.

The current situation is a republic dealing with the political reality that republics, like all governments, contain people who would prefer a different arrangement. Three influential families — the Oluwafemi, the Diop, and the Diallo — represent different visions of what Solara should become, and the Diallo faction's monarchist leanings happen to align with Cadman interests in ways that the parliament's intelligence committee has been documenting for two years.

Adventurers come to Solara for the Order of Magnus's library access, the legal market in Dead Lands artifacts (better regulated but less lucrative than the alternatives), contracts from merchant houses with money to spend on problems they cannot address through official channels, and the Vithurian Mines, which pay well and have the kind of security situation that generates steady work.

---

#### **Bellaria**

Bellaria is old money in a new crisis. The kingdom traces its ancestry to the First Men — the early human settlers of the northern coast — and the twin rulers, Princess Yelena in the mountain fortress of Frosthold and Prince Andreas at the coastal seat of Stormkeep, govern a nation that has been managing the tension between its northern and southern halves for two generations without resolving it.

The obsidian deposits discovered in the mountains near the Dead Lands' edge made Bellaria the wealthiest nation in the Known World within a generation. It also made Bellaria the most necessary — and the most resented. The Chroniclers established themselves at King's Hold before anyone else had the institutional capacity to contest it, and now the partnership between the Bellarian crown and the Chroniclers is sufficiently intertwined that distinguishing their interests requires legal expertise the crown does not always have available.

The bay to Bellaria's south is the Winter's Bay, difficult to navigate and controlled by the Crimson Company in the portions the crown cannot reach. The land routes through Norderenn — now occupied — have been cut. Bellaria is more isolated than it was fifteen years ago, and the obsidian monopoly is the thing keeping it from being simply squeezed.

Adventurers come to Bellaria for Chronicler contracts, Dead Lands expeditions launched from the Winter's Bay ports, and the specific kind of work that arises when a crown is wealthy enough to have enemies but politically constrained enough that it cannot address them officially.

---

#### **Val Cadma**

Val Cadma is the most powerful nation in the Known World by military measure, and it has arrived at that position through a combination of genuine strategic competence and an ideology that eliminates the internal debates that slow other states down. When Archbishop Kranzler recommends a course of action and the High Pontiff endorses it, the Cadman military moves. The deliberative councils that govern Solara or Endoras do not have an equivalent mechanism.

The heartland is the rolling agricultural plain between the Iron Mountains and the Bay of Kings coast — well-watered, productive, and large enough to sustain the empire's population without depending on the trade routes it is simultaneously trying to control. The Iron Mountains yield the ore that feeds the largest military-industrial apparatus in the Known World: weapons, armor, siege equipment, the infrastructure of a state that has been preparing for a long war and is now ready to fight it.

The occupied territories — Norderenn being the current example — are administered by Archbishops who report to Kranzler, not to the Empress directly. This keeps the conquest from destabilizing the internal power structure. The Empress governs. The Church extends.

Adventurers do not often choose to operate inside Val Cadma proper. Those who find themselves there are either Tar'amaan faithful with institutional support, smugglers using the Crimson Company's networks, or people on missions they would prefer to complete quickly and leave before anyone official takes an interest.

---

#### **Norderenn**

Norderenn is occupied territory and a functioning kingdom simultaneously, which requires understanding what each term applies to. King Boris I controls the northern forests and the mountain approaches from Castle Jaeger. The Cadman administration controls the southern lowlands, the river crossings, and the border posts. The resistance — loose, regional, sustained by Bellarian material support and local popular anger — controls the Varengard Darkwood and the mountain passes that the Cadman administration has decided are not worth the casualties to hold.

The terrain is northern forest: dense, cold, difficult to move armies through, which is the main reason the occupation has not been completed fifteen years on. The Cadmans are not losing Norderenn. They are not winning it either, and the calculation of what it costs to hold against what value the territory provides is one that Kranzler has to make each year.

The Kronn currency question is present in every market and every transaction in the occupied south. Using one is a political statement. Accepting one in change is a smaller one. The Cadman administration knows this and tracks it.

Adventurers come to Norderenn to work for the resistance (rescue operations, intelligence runs, supply escort), to work for the Chroniclers through the Dead Lands approach routes that cross the northern territory, or because they are from there and have reasons of their own.

---

#### **Alvengaard**

Alvengaard is the northern forest nation, and its forests are not navigational challenges the way Norderenn's are — they are the point. The Aelár built their settlements around the trees, not despite them, and the major population centers exist in clearings that the forest permits rather than in locations a military planner would have chosen. The practical effect is that Alvengaard is difficult to occupy: there are no central cities to take and hold, no roads that remain passable when the forest chooses otherwise.

The western forests are Húldar territory. The border between Aelár and Húldar land is not marked on most maps and is not enforced by fences, but by the Húldar's reputation, which has successfully discouraged casual exploration for several centuries. Mynyaraan, the pre-Darkness elvish city that the Húldar have preserved and the Chroniclers have been trying to access, is somewhere in that territory. The Chroniclers know roughly where. The Húldar know exactly where they are not welcome.

Alvengaard's political paralysis on the question of coordinating with the Magic Bloc against Val Cadma is real and frustrating to its allies. It is also comprehensible: committing to a war requires the Aelár council and the Húldar elders to agree on something, which they have historically avoided as a structural principle.

Adventurers come to Alvengaard for elvish components unavailable elsewhere, for Chronicler missions that require navigating the Húldar border, and for whatever is actually inside Mynyaraan, which remains sufficiently unknown that the possible answers cover a wide range of values.

### **The Outer Nations**

#### **Messalya**

Messalya is a confederation of city-states with no fixed capital and, officially, no unified foreign policy. Achelon, the mobile capital that travels on the back of Kaira — a giant turtle whose circuit through the southern desert takes approximately eight months to complete — serves as the seat of whatever collective governance the confederation can agree to at a given moment, which is less than its members would prefer and more than their opponents expected.

The desert geography is the fact that shapes everything else. Water, shade, and navigable routes are the three resources that matter, and the city-states that control oases control the negotiating table. Trade through Messalya runs in specialized tracks: desert spices and medicinal herbs that grow nowhere else in the Known World, the gems and rare crystals that the Stone Raiders harvest from Kaira's path, and a transit economy for goods moving between the eastern and western halves of the world that cannot or will not use the Emerald Corridor.

Adventurers come to Messalya for the Stone Raiders' gems, contracts from city-states that have no standing army and need problems solved, and occasional work involving Kaira directly — the turtle's passage creates geological disturbances, and what comes up in those disturbances is not always benign.

---

#### **Endoras**

Endoras feeds the world. The Emerald Plains — the broad, mild flatlands protected from the polar winters by the mountain barrier to the north — produce food surpluses that sustain populations in half a dozen nations that cannot sustain themselves. The pink fruit trees along the mountain's lower edge are Endora's most distinctive agricultural product: dense in nutrients, long-lasting in storage, valuable enough per unit that the trade in them alone would justify most nations' diplomatic attention.

The Merchant Council that effectively governs Endoras's economic life is not the same body as the Great Council that officially governs the nation, and the gap between the two is where most of Endoras's political energy goes. The guilds want prices optimized; the council wants stability maintained; Val Cadma wants both bodies penetrated by agents who can shift decisions in the Cadman direction without anyone noticing the pressure.

Adventurers come to Endoras for the Emerald Corridor's escort work — caravans moving through the corridor are a perpetual source of contracts — for the Covenant Stones at the mountain's edge, where inter-racial agreements have been formalized for centuries and where the records of those agreements are occasionally contested in ways that require people who can move fast, and for the Valdan Iron Mines, which have a security situation that varies from manageable to serious depending on the political season.

---

#### **Urgônia**

Urgônia occupies the Shattered Plains — a region of broken terrain, mineral outcroppings, and river cuts that makes large-scale agriculture impractical and large-scale mining extremely productive. The Ûrgons have been quarrying the Zurnak Quarries for construction stone of a quality that the Inner Nations import at significant expense, and the artisanal tradition around working that stone has produced craftspeople whose work commands prices the Ûrgon clans have learned to negotiate for with considerable sophistication.

The clans are autonomous, governed by elders, and coordinated through the Elder Council for questions that affect more than one clan's territory. The coordination mechanism is slow and requires consensus, which makes Urgônia a stable partner in the Emerald Corridor Alliance and a frustrating one when rapid decisions are needed.

Adventurers come to Urgônia for the river trade that passes through the Shattered Plains section of the Emerald Corridor, for clan contracts involving territorial disputes that require outside arbitration, and for the ruins that surface periodically when the quarrying opens new sections of the plains — not Dead Lands ruins, but old enough that the Chroniclers maintain an observer presence in Korthak Citadel.

---

#### **Bal'mekh**

Bal'mekh is frozen deserts and high mountains, and the Vartrans who live in it have adapted to conditions that would kill most other people within weeks. Geothermal springs make settled habitation possible; rare crystals form in the thermal vents and are the basis for the limited trade that keeps Bal'mekh from complete isolation. The Northern Wall — the range of frozen cliffs along the territory's northern edge — contains vestiges of Eymanarynn construction that predate the Second Era and that nobody, including the Vartrans, has fully catalogued.

The Order of Magnus built the Tower of Nyrün on Bal'mekh's eastern coast without the Vartran clans' full understanding of what the Order intended to do there. What the Order has actually been doing there remains imperfectly known to anyone outside the tower's senior staff. The Vartrans permits it. The Vartrans need allies more than they need answers.

Bal'mekh's strategic situation in 1,380 TE is straightforward and grim. Val Cadma considers the Vartrans a religious target — draconic ancestry is heresy by Tar'amaan doctrine — and the occupation of Norderenn removed the overland route to Bellaria that might have served as a deterrent. The Vartrans have options, none of them comfortable: alliance with the Emerald Corridor Alliance, partnership with the Order of Magnus, mercenary acquisition using the Eymanarynn artifacts the Northern Wall keeps producing, or organized evacuation before the Cadman expansion reaches them.

Adventurers come to Bal'mekh for the Northern Wall's artifacts, for contracts that the Vartran clans are increasingly willing to pay well for given the geopolitical situation, and for the Tower of Nyrün, which has a standing offer for qualified researchers and an equally standing reputation for not sharing what it finds.

---

#### **Gwan'tor**

Gwan'tor is arid mountains and the people who thrive in them. The Badakis — organized in warrior clans, devoted to Boros — have been the Emerald Corridor Alliance's military backbone since the alliance formed, and the alliance has been the best thing that happened to Gwan'tor since the Badakis decided that exporting their fighters was more economically sustainable than raiding. The training traditions that produced them are housed at Duskhold; the spiritual center that gives those traditions their coherence is the mountain sanctuary of Karvak.

The internal tension in Gwan'tor is clan-based and currently manageable: some clans favor the alliance's deepening integration, others resist anything that looks like subordinating clan honor to collective interest. The clans that resist are the ones Val Cadma's agents have been cultivating for two years, offering arrangements that allow specific clans to maintain autonomy in exchange for undermining Gwan'tor's contribution to the alliance. Whether those arrangements will pay off before the clans involved notice what they are actually agreeing to is an open question.

Adventurers come to Gwan'tor for mercenary contracts, for the mountain resources that the clans control and that several nations want access to without going through official channels, and for the training available at Duskhold to those who can earn a sponsor willing to attach their name to the candidate.

The pride Badakis take in Duskhold is not only martial. Every warrior the fortress produces carries the community's name with them. That accountability is not incidental to the training, but part of what the training is.

Duskhold sits deep in Gwan'tor territory, carved into the mountain with the evident intention of being found only by those meant to find it. The fortress is where the Way of Boros is transmitted to warriors — Badaki by right, others by sponsor — and where the final trial that produces Duskhold Weapon Masters is administered. See Chapter 5\. The faith's spiritual center, distinct from the training, is the mountain sanctuary of Karvak.

---

#### **Val'aria**

Val'aria is a country in the process of becoming two countries, and both halves are using the civil war as a reason not to address the question directly. The north, conservative and self-sufficient, holds its Ancestors' Fields and its traditional maritime identity with increasing defensiveness. The south, oriented toward Endoras and open to foreign commercial relationships, holds Shadowport and most of the nation's current economic activity. The High King at Tor Mantar has maintained formal neutrality between them for long enough that his authority has become primarily ceremonial.

The Val'arian navigational tradition — the result of seven centuries of accumulated knowledge about the Inner Sea and the Eastern Sea both — is what every external power actually wants. Val'arian pilots can navigate routes and conditions that other crews will not attempt. Val'arian ship designs incorporate techniques the Known World's other shipbuilders have been trying to reverse-engineer since the first arrival seven centuries ago. Both the northern and southern factions understand this leverage, and both have been selling it selectively, which has accelerated the civil war's financing on both sides.

Adventurers come to Val'aria for navigation contracts, for the specific artifacts that Val'arian culture produces and that collectors in Solara and Bellaria pay well for, and for the work that civil wars generate: escort, intelligence, the retrieval of people or materials from the wrong side of a moving front line.

---

### **The Magic Bloc**

The Magic Bloc is Solara, Alvengaard, and Messalya — the three nations most dependent on magical practice and most directly threatened by Val Cadma's expansion. Geographically, the alliance spans a wide arc: Solara anchors it from the Bay of Kings' southern shore, Alvengaard holds the Inner Sea's northwestern coast, and Messalya's free cities sit along the western trade routes connecting both to the broader continent. That arc looks stronger on a map than it functions in practice. Alvengaard's Aelár council and Húldar elders remain divided on the depth of their commitment, making the bloc's most magically potent member its least predictable. Messalya's confederation of free cities can field elite soldiers and open trade corridors, but a confederation votes slowly and commits reluctantly. Solara, the Known World's only republic, is the bloc's intellectual and logistical core — which also makes it the target Val Cadma most wants to isolate.

The bloc's obsidian supply runs through Bellaria, which is not a formal member. Bellaria controls the Dead Lands access points where the Chroniclers regulate extraction, and has enriched itself accordingly — but a formal alliance would invite the Cadman military pressure it has so far managed to avoid. The bloc depends on a nation whose survival is calculated around staying outside it. Val Cadma understands this. Its sustained pressure on Chronicler neutrality is not aimed at the Chroniclers themselves; it is a method for making Bellaria's neutrality untenable — collapsing the supply chain that makes Solaran flying ships, Alvengaard's arcane infrastructure, and Messalya's trade advantage possible, without a single shot fired at any of the three.

### **The Dead Lands**

The Dead Lands are not a region in the same sense as the nations above. They have no government, no population centers, and no defined boundary that all parties agree on. What they have is Eymanarann and Vysandárien — the ruins of the two civilizations that preceded the Second Era — and the obsidian that the Darkness's presence forged into the bedrock over centuries of occupation.

The Chroniclers nominally control access. Their patrol routes, launched from King's Hold in Bellaria's northern territory, cover the most economically productive sections. Beyond those patrol routes, and increasingly within them, the control is theoretical.

**Eymanarann's** ruins extend farther than most expeditions have gone and look stranger than most expedition reports prepare new arrivals for. The Chroniclers have named the most accessible zones — First City, Ner Aak, Kalazym — as if they were discrete settlements, because the naming gave them something to map. The evidence on the ground argues otherwise: towers of grey material in clustered patterns, connected by subsurface corridors running in straight lines between them, open stretches between the clusters that read like streets rather than wilderness. The three named zones are not separate ruins. They are the sections that protrude above the ice.

The outer zones can be reached by a prepared expedition in a week of hard travel from the nearest Bellarian staging point. The inner ruins require longer, better preparation, and tolerance for conditions that make equipment fail. The magical storms that arise without warning carry obsidian fragments sharp enough to cut exposed skin; the *eternal ice* — the ice that the Illyans' dying breath formed and that no ordinary fire can melt — absorbs heat on contact, including magical heat. Expedition parties that push into the inner regions without specific countermeasures tend not to come back with only the planned problems.

The corrupted creatures that move through Eymanarann — undead, animals warped by sustained exposure to the Darkness's residue, constructs that the Eymanarynn left running and that treat intruders as threats — are covered in Book III. What matters geographically is that they do not respect the Chroniclers' patrol boundaries and they do not distinguish between licensed expeditions and unlicensed ones.

**Vysandárien** is on Eymanarann's eastern flank, and the formal prohibition on deep exploration is more consistently enforced there — not because the Chroniclers have more resources in the region, but because the three expeditions that have attempted serious exploration of Vysandárien's inner sections have returned with survivor accounts that made subsequent volunteers difficult to find. The underground railway system that connects Vysandárien's major sites to points in Norderenn, Bal'mekh, and Bellaria remains partially active in ways that no one has been able to fully explain or fully shut down. The stations at the Bal'mekh end — Zyzak and Dymoras — are known. What is in between them is not.

The dragons that the Second Era's historical records place in Vysandárien's deepest chambers are either dead, dormant, or something else. All three possibilities are currently under active investigation by parties that have not published their findings.

### **Movement and Distance**

The Known World is large, and the distances between its nations are not abstractions. A merchant moving goods from Bal'mekh to Solara by overland route and river barge should expect two to three months of travel under good conditions. The same journey by flying ship — if one can be chartered, if the cargo justifies the expense — takes under two weeks. The gap between those two timelines is the obsidian economy expressed as felt experience.

Standard travel by river barge along the Emerald Corridor covers 40 to 50 kilometers per day with favorable current, less in winter, more in the spring surge if the pilot is willing to accept the associated risk. Overland travel through open terrain averages 30 to 40 kilometers per day for a mounted group; through heavy forest or mountain terrain, half that or less. The winter months — specifically the six to eight weeks when the polar storms push south — effectively halt overland travel in the northern regions and make river travel dangerous on the Corridor's upper sections.

The cost of distance matters in a second way. Information takes as long to travel as the fastest messenger carrying it. Events in Gwan'tor do not reach Bellaria for weeks. Military decisions made in Val Cadma's war room are weeks old by the time their consequences arrive at the far end of the occupied territories. The Known World runs on information that is always somewhat out of date, and the advantages of flying ships — which move faster than any other means of communication except magical ones — are as much informational as logistical.

### **For the GM: Geography as Pressure**

The physical world is not a backdrop, but the mechanism that makes certain problems hard and certain solutions available.

**Distance creates pressure.** A party escorting a diplomat from Urgônia to Iól Vithura has roughly three weeks of travel during which things can go wrong, resources can run out, and the political situation they were sent to address can change. That pressure is specific and manageable. Use it.

**Chokepoints are adventure sites.** Every major trade route has points where it narrows — river crossings, mountain passes, and the Winter's Bay's approach channels. These are where tolls are collected, where smugglers are caught, where ambushes are laid, and where a small number of people can influence the movement of significant value. The Emerald Corridor has a dozen such points. So does every route through occupied Norderenn.

**Weather is a complication, not a setback.** The polar storms that descend on the northern nations are the Known World's seasonal reality, and they should affect play the same way they affect the world: supply costs go up, travel becomes harder, and people make decisions they would not make otherwise. A siege that began in autumn becomes a different siege in the middle of the polar winter.

**The Dead Lands are never just a dungeon.** Every expedition there is also a geopolitical act. Who licensed it, who is watching for it, what the party brings back, and to whom — all of these have consequences that reach beyond the expedition itself. A party that returns with significant obsidian has something that multiple powerful organizations want, and the Chroniclers' reaction to unlicensed extraction is not bureaucratic.

**Bal'mekh is running out of time.** This is not a condition that resolves itself while the party is doing something else. If the GM wants it to feel urgent, it should feel urgent: each session that passes without the Vartrans finding allies or options is a session in which the Cadman expansion consolidates. Geography made Bal'mekh vulnerable. Only choices — someone else's or the party's — can change that.
