# Chapter 5: Cultures, Languages & Factions

### ***Who Holds Power, How It Moves, and What That Means for People Without It***

---

Power in the Known World does not stay inside national borders. It never has. The Chroniclers operate in Bellaria and Norderenn simultaneously. The Order of Magnus maintains facilities in Solara, Bal'mekh, and wherever else it has decided a research installation would be useful. The Church of Tar'amaan is technically Val Cadma's state religion and actually a transnational institution that Val Cadma's crown has concluded it cannot afford to contradict. The Crimson Company answers to no flag.

Understanding the Known World means understanding this layer — the institutions that cross borders, the languages that make crossing possible, and the cultural textures that shape how people inside any given nation actually live. Ancestry explains where someone came from. This chapter explains what they walked into.

---

### **Languages**

The Known World has no universal language, but it has Common — a trade tongue that developed along the Emerald Corridor and the Inner Sea ports over centuries of commercial necessity. Common is functional and unattractive. It has a limited vocabulary for concepts that do not involve commerce, navigation, or dispute resolution, and speakers who use it exclusively are operating with approximately two-thirds of the expressive range available in any of the regional languages. It is, nevertheless, what a merchant from Urgônia and a merchant from Messalya use when they need to settle a price, and it is what adventurers from different backgrounds default to when they cannot assume a shared tongue.

Regional languages cluster by ancestry and geography, with significant overlap at borders and ports.

**Elvish *(or Vengardian)*** is spoken across Alvengaard by both Aelár and Húldar, though the dialects have diverged enough that a native speaker of one can identify a speaker of the other within a sentence. The divergence is partly linguistic drift — eleven centuries of geographic separation — and partly deliberate, each community having developed vocabulary and idiom that reflects its distinct experience. Scholars from Solara's universities study Classical Elvish, the pre-Second Era written form preserved in Mynyaraan, and find it mutually intelligible with neither modern dialect in the way they hoped.

**Dwarven *(or Endoran)*** is spoken in Nara'baraan and in the Duardeen communities of Endoras, with the same divergence pattern as Elvish — shared root, two centuries of separation producing distinct vocabularies. Dwarven is a precise language with extensive technical vocabulary for stonework, metallurgy, and clan law, and a relative poverty of terms for anything that cannot be measured. Traders who work the Emerald Corridor learn enough Dwarven to negotiate; scholars learn it to access the Duergar's institutional records, which are not translated and are not going to be.

**Ûrgon** and **Badaki** are structurally unrelated despite the peoples' shared origin — the Sisters apparently did not standardize their languages when they created the two peoples, or the standardization collapsed over eleven centuries of separate development. Both languages are spoken across the Emerald Corridor Alliance's territory; both have enough Common loanwords that communication is manageable for a patient speaker. Badaki has a rich vocabulary for gradations of honor and obligation that Common cannot adequately render, which creates ongoing difficulties in contract negotiation when one party is Badaki and the other assumes the Common terms are sufficient.

**Val'arian** is the language that linguists find most interesting and most opaque. It has no demonstrable relationship to any other language spoken in the Known World, which either means the Val'arianos developed in complete isolation from every other people on the planet, or they came from somewhere far enough away that the linguistic connection has dissolved beyond recovery. The written form uses a script that no other Known World culture employs. The Chroniclers have a dedicated team working on a comprehensive grammar. They have been working on it for forty years.

**Vartran** is descended from the Old Tongue — the language of the Exalted and of the Second Era's dominant powers — but the two are no longer mutually intelligible. Eleven centuries of isolation have changed the grammar, shed vocabulary, and introduced sounds and idioms that the original would not recognize. What remains is structural: root words appear in both, certain constructions echo each other, and a trained speaker of one can sometimes guess at meaning in the other without understanding it. This partial relationship means fluent Vartrans are the most valuable living translators of Dead Lands inscriptions, a fact that the Chroniclers have leveraged into a specific type of contractual relationship and that the Ordo Draconis has leveraged into something considerably less official. The Old Tongue itself survives as a learned language — studied by scholars, preserved in Eymanarynn ruins, and spoken fluently by very few people outside Bal'mekh.

**Cadman** is spoken throughout Val Cadma and its occupied territories. In Norderenn, it is the language of administration, legal proceedings, and Inquisition hearings. Norderenn's native tongue — a regional version of Bellarian — is still spoken in homes, in the resistance, and in the occupied south's markets, where using it is technically not illegal and practically noted. A Cadman administrator who speaks Bellarian fluently is either a committed occupier who learned the local language to be effective or an intelligence asset. There is no third reading.

**Other human languages** are Solaran, spoken in Solara and Messalya, and Bellarian, spoken in Bellaria and by the resistance in Norderenn. There’s also a dialect in Solara called High Solaran, spoken only by the highborne and scholars \- like mages, making it the standard language of the Order of Magnus and modern arcane writings.

**The Druidic tongue** is its own category: a secret language known only to those who have undergone Druidic initiation, used exclusively for communication about natural and spiritual matters. Non-druids cannot learn it. Druids do not teach it outside initiation. What the language actually sounds like is not something the druidic orders have agreed to demonstrate.

## **Cultures**

Languages tell you what people speak. Cultures tell you what they argue about, what they owe each other, and what they will not forgive.

### **Solaran culture** 

Is the Known World's ongoing experiment in self-governance, and like most experiments, it produces results that complicate the hypothesis. The parliamentary system produces genuine debate and genuine deadlock in roughly equal proportions. The arts receive public funding because the early republic decided that a democracy needed citizens who could think critically about what they saw, and the Teatro Solar has spent three centuries confirming that this produces work that makes everyone uncomfortable, which is either evidence that the system is working or evidence it has gone too far, depending on which family you belong to. Class in Solara is expressed through education and patronage rather than title — the distinction is functional rather than principled, since the families with old money fund the education and the patronage and therefore the access, but it is a distinction that Solarans maintain with some vigor.

The three major families — Oluwafemi, Diop, Diallo — are not noble houses. They are powerful families. The difference is that nobility is an inherited status with legal weight, and the Solaran Republic abolished legal nobility in its founding documents. What these families have is money, institutional relationships, and the specific influence that comes from having funded enough of the city's infrastructure that the city has trouble imagining itself without them. The Diallo family's monarchist sympathies are not illegal. They are, in the current political climate, extremely convenient for Val Cadma.

The three great families — Oluwafemi, Diop, Diallo — are Messalyan in origin, which is either a story about the republic working as designed or about old money finding its way to the top regardless of the system, depending on how cynical you are about the Solaran experiment. Their full names follow the hybrid register of their ancestry: a Messalyan family name paired with a Solaran given name. Idris Oluwafemi. Mireia Diop. Tariq Diallo. The founding documents say nothing about birthplace as a condition for civic standing, which was either principled or convenient, since the families who arrived with capital also arrived when the republic needed capital to survive. They have been inseparable from its institutions ever since.

The general Solaran population names follow an Iberian-Arabic register: soft consonants, open vowels, names ending in \-a, \-o, \-ar, \-in. Family names in the Spanish style — maternal and paternal combined, shortened in practice — mean that a Solaran's full formal name is rarely used outside legal documents. Meeting someone who insists on their complete name is either encountering a lawyer or someone with a very specific point to make.

### **Cadman culture** 

Cadman culture has been shaped so thoroughly by the Church that the two are no longer separable in practice. A Cadman child grows up in a society where the confession schedule is monthly, where sunrise prayers are public enough that missing them is noticed, and where the categories of "heretic" and "non-Cadman" overlap in ways the Church has never fully clarified, because the ambiguity is useful. The culture values discipline, hierarchy, and the specific pride of belonging to a civilization that believes it is saving the world from magical corruption. That belief is sincere at the popular level. A Cadman farmer who reports a neighbor's illegal magical practice to the local Acólito is not performing loyalty — they genuinely believe they are doing the right thing. The apparatus that processes the report and decides what to do with it operates at a level the farmer never sees.

Social class in Val Cadma runs through the Church. The nobility exists and has formal functions, but real upward mobility for a person without a title runs through clerical appointment, which the Church controls and which the Church uses to manage political loyalty. The Invictus — the ultra-conservative faction within the Church that considers the current Empress insufficiently devoted — represents what happens when the clergy concludes that their nominal political superiors have become an obstacle rather than an instrument.

The Church's naming system runs in parallel to its hierarchy. At ordination, a cleric receives a devotional name drawn from a formal theological lexicon — always a virtue or a tenet of Tar'amaan doctrine, never a personal name from outside Church canon. These names follow a consistent phonology: Latin-inflected roots ending in \-ar, \-is, \-mar, \-ian, \-thar. Puritar. Fidmar. Vigilar. Ardentis. This is the name a cleric's congregation uses, the name on Church correspondence, the name the Inquisition files under. The birth name is not erased — erasing it would imply the individual existed fully before the Church made them, which is not the theology — demoted instead. Private. Retained for family and for prayer. A cleric who gives their birth name to someone outside family is doing something intimate and deliberate.

At elevation to Archbishop or above, a pontifical name may replace the devotional one in all Church contexts — longer, drawn from the names of martyred Church figures rather than abstract virtues. Archbishop Kranzler's devotional name and birth name are both known to the Pontiff. In the Empress's court, he is Kranzler. Before the High Pontiff, he is something else. Both are real. Neither is the full picture.

The Invictus have an informal inversion of this convention: members meeting in private use birth names. They consider this a return to honesty. A GM who hears a senior cleric address another by an unfamiliar name in a private setting has not encountered a mistake in the chapter text.

### **Bellarian Culture**

Bellaria is governed by a dynasty with a southern name ruling a nation with a northern memory, and the gap between those two things has not closed in the two generations the Jaeger crown has been managing it. Frosthold sits in the mountains. Stormkeep faces the sea. Neither half has forgotten which twin rules which seat, and the arrangement has survived primarily because both halves agree on the one thing that matters: the obsidian revenue does not flow without the Chroniclers, and the Chroniclers do not stay without political cover, and political cover requires the kingdom to present a coherent face to the outside. The face is coherent enough. The interior is not.

The northern clans are organized around lineage, not geography. A clan elder's authority is generational — it derives from ancestors who made specific decisions at specific moments that the clan remembers in detail, and is therefore not transferable to someone who lacks that ancestry, regardless of how the kingdom's official succession is structured. The southern coastal cities operate on merchant logic: money, contract, and the specific influence that comes from being the port through which the obsidian actually moves. The two frameworks are not incompatible in theory. In practice, they produce a steady low-intensity argument about whether a contract signed by a southern merchant house binds a northern clan that never agreed to it, which is the question underlying approximately half of Bellaria's internal legal disputes.

The Jaeger dynasty's surname is southern-register — Germanic-compound, indistinguishable at a glance from the Cadman names on the other side of Norderenn's border. This is useful diplomatically and costs something at home. Northern clan elders use the dynastic name in formal address because they are required to. Among themselves, in the high-mountain meeting halls where the crown's representatives are not invited, they use the old forms. Princess Yelena's given name is northern-register, Slavic-rooted. It is not an accident. Whether it was a political calculation by her parents or simply the name that fit is a question the northern clans have spent a generation debating, and the answer they have arrived at is that it does not matter — it works, and things that work do not need to be explained.

The obsidian wealth has not distributed itself evenly, which is the single sharpest internal tension Bellaria is not discussing publicly. The Chronicler operation at King's Hold generates revenue that flows primarily through the southern port infrastructure and the noble families with existing commercial relationships. Northern clan territories, closer to the Dead Lands by geography, have absorbed more of the creature threat that Dead Lands proximity produces and seen proportionally less of the money. The Iron Brothers chapter in Bellaria's northern territories handles more Dead Lands-adjacent contracts than any other chapter in the Known World, and their files on what is moving out of the ruins in the current period are the most current available — and the most alarming.

Adventurers come to Bellaria for Chronicler contracts launched from the Winter's Bay ports, for the specific work that arises when a wealthy crown cannot officially address a problem, and for the northern territories, where the combination of Dead Lands access and under-resourced local defenses produces steady, dangerous, and well-compensated work.

Names. Bellarian names split along the same north-south fault as the kingdom's politics. Northern Bellarians — the old clans, the steppe-descended families who followed the First Men into the mountain territories — use names drawn from a Mongol-Turkic register: short, direct, often ending in a vowel or a clean consonant stop. Taban, Sura, Orun, Borte. Their surnames are clan-heritage markers, spoken with the lineage first and the personal name second in formal address. In the south, the coastal families use Germanic-compound surnames — Brandt, Kesler, Vornwald — and personal names close enough to Val Cadman register that Cadman administrators processing Norderenn occupation paperwork rarely flag them as foreign. The Jaeger dynasty's name is southern-register, chosen or evolved to signal legitimacy to the coastland cities. Northern clan elders use it when they have to. Among themselves, they use the old forms.

### **Vengardian Culture**

Alvengaard's political paralysis is real, consistently underestimated by its allies, and comprehensible from the inside. Making a decision that commits Alvengaard to anything — a war, a formal alliance, a unified policy — requires the Aelár council in the eastern forests and the Húldar elders in the west to agree. They have built a functional refusal to do this into the nation's governing structure across eleven centuries, and at this point the structure is not a flaw, but the point. Both communities concluded, at some moment early in their coexistence, that their respective interests were served better by maintaining independence from each other's decisions than by achieving the efficiency of unified governance. The Magic Bloc finds this frustrating. The Aelár and Húldar find the frustration clarifying — it confirms they made the right calculation.

**Aelár culture** is the culture of long memory and high tolerance for time. An Aelár who has been watching a political situation develop for fifty years is not being patient in the way a human understands patience — they are operating on a timeline where fifty years is a recent development and the full context is not yet visible. This produces a specific quality of decision-making: careful, contextually deep, and resistant to urgency that the Aelár read as pressure rather than information. Their settlements are built into the forest rather than against it; the great clearings where their councils meet are negotiated spaces, opened with the cooperation of the trees that border them, and the relationship between settlement and forest is one the Aelár maintain actively. Magic is not separate from this, but the mechanism through which the relationship is maintained, and the threat Tar'amaan represents to magical practice is therefore a threat to the specific ecological arrangement that makes Aelár habitation possible in the eastern forest.

Class among the Aelár runs through lineage and house affiliation rather than wealth or political appointment. The great houses — whose names appear in the pre-Darkness records that the Chroniclers keep trying to access and that the Aelár have not volunteered — hold social weight because they have held it for centuries, and the cultural legitimacy of that weight does not depend on anyone's formal endorsement. New wealth does not translate quickly into house standing. A human merchant who becomes rich trading in elvish components and then tries to operate in Aelár political space using that wealth as leverage will find that the leverage does not reach far enough to matter.

**Húldar culture** is organized around the druidic tradition, and the druidic tradition is organized around the specific relationships between practitioners and the living world they tend. A Húldar Druid is not a generalist who knows nature — they are the custodian of a particular forest section, a particular watershed, a particular set of relationships that were established by a predecessor and will be continued by a successor. The individual is important primarily as the current holder of a continuous responsibility, which produces a cultural relationship to personal identity that is more collective and longer-horizon than the Aelár's, which is itself longer-horizon than anything the human nations practice.

The Húldar's western territories include Mynyaraan, the pre-Darkness elvish city that has been continuously inhabited by the Húldar since before the First Men arrived in the Known World. The Chroniclers know roughly where it is. The Húldar know exactly where the Chroniclers are not welcome, which has been sufficient to maintain the boundary. What Mynyaraan actually contains — the preserved knowledge of the pre-Darkness civilization, the specific druidic lineages that the Húldar have maintained unbroken since Ilya's time, the things that the Darkness's imprisonment made possible to preserve — is not something the Húldar have described to outsiders in terms that outsiders have been able to verify.

Adventurers come to Alvengaard for elvish components unavailable elsewhere, for Chronicler missions that require navigating the Húldar border, for whatever is actually in Mynyaraan, and for the occasional contract from an Aelár house that has decided a human party is more deniable than an Aelár one for a problem they do not want officially attributed to them.

**Aelár names** are long, vowel-rich, built on liquid consonants — the phonological signature of a people who developed their language inside deep forest, where sound carries far and urgency is rarely the register. Vaenith, Celarel, Ithavar, Endrian. Family names are lineage designations centuries old, rarely used in daily interaction — Aelár identify through house affiliation when it matters and by first name otherwise. When an Aelár gives a human a family name unprompted, they are usually signaling either deep trust or a formal request that the encounter be treated as such.

**Húldar names** are shorter and more grounded — earthier sounds, consonants that feel like something a person could hold. Brekk, Orun, Mira, Veth. Their surnames are nature-based and druidically specific: not categories like "oakwood" but the particular feature that marked an ancestor's bond with a place or a living thing. Ashfall, Stonestep, Coldbrook, Ironroot, Deepfrost. A Húldar who introduces themselves by their nature surname to a non-Druid is sharing something they do not have to share. The surname is a druidic bond made public. Most Húldar use only the first name until they have decided the encounter warrants more.

### **Norderenn culture**

Norderenn culture is fifteen years into the process of deciding what it is under occupation. The answer varies by region and class. In the occupied south, a generation of children is being educated in Cadman-administered schools that teach Cadman history, Cadman language, and Tar'amaan theology. Their parents remember what was taught before. Some of those parents have made arrangements with the occupation that they tell themselves are pragmatic. Others have not, and the households that have not are the ones where the Kronn are kept in a box under the floor, and the children are taught Nordish songs after dark. The resistance in the northern forests operates on Bellarian supply chains and popular anger, and the popular anger is real, but popular anger does not win sieges.

In the occupied south, a name is a declaration that does not require words. Cadman administration records use family names in the Germanic register — Kellarn, Vosswald, Bergisch — because these are the names that process through Tar'amaan administrative forms without friction and sound, to Cadman ears, like someone who has made their accommodation. The patronymic surnames of old Norderenn — Ivanich, Voronin, names built in the form of Mira-of-Dragos — are not illegal. They are noted. A census clerk in the occupied south who encounters a full Slavic-register name in a household report will mark the file. Whether that mark goes anywhere depends on how much the local Inquisition has on its plate. In the northern forests, under Boris I's writ, arriving with a Cadman-register name marks you as either an occupier or someone who spent time in the south and came back changed. Neither reading is neutral. The children being educated in Cadman-administered schools are growing up with both sets of names available to them and no clear instruction on what choosing one means.

### **Endoran culture**

Endoran culture reflects the nation's founding logic: cooperation between peoples who had to coexist or starve. The result is genuinely multicultural in the sense that matters — not symbolic diversity maintained for appearances, but the actual adaptation that comes from centuries of shared institutions. The Great Council's multi-ancestral representation is functional rather than ceremonial. The Merchant Council's guild structure mixes Duardeen expertise, Ûrgon mediation traditions, and human commercial networks in arrangements that work because they have been working for long enough that the alternatives have been forgotten. The cultural tension is not between ancestries but between the institutional class — the guild leadership, the senior council members — and everyone outside it, because Endoras's democratic structures have developed the same way democratic structures tend to develop, which is that the people who run them eventually have more in common with each other than with the people they represent.

Endoran naming reflects the nation's founding logic: no single ancestry's convention dominates. Duergar clan names have softened over generations of integration into the guild structure — the apostrophe-separated clan prefix (Khor'Balin, in full dwarven form) has been absorbed into a Duardeen surname (Balin Khorwald) that human guild partners can actually say. Ûrgon craft epithets are used socially in Endoras in a way they are not in Urgônia itself — a visiting Ûrgon will find their craft name in active use at the first guild table they sit at, which is either a courtesy or an assumption, depending on the Ûrgon. Human names in Endoras tend to run Germanic-adjacent, close enough to Val Cadma's register to create a specific problem for resistance operatives who need to distinguish Endoran collaborators from Cadman agents by sound alone. They cannot.

### **Urgônian culture**

Urgônian culture is organized around craft mastery and the specific honor attached to doing one thing excellently for a long time. The Ûrgon clans are not organized by what they believe or who they follow but by what they make — a stonemason clan, a river-navigation clan, a mediator clan whose hereditary specialization is resolving disputes between other clans. This produces extraordinary depth in each domain and a cultural tendency to regard generalists with mild suspicion. An Ûrgon who is competent at many things and exceptional at none is, by their value system, someone who has not yet decided what they are. Whether this is wisdom or limitation is a question Ûrgons do not consider interesting.

Ûrgon names follow a specific structure: a short first name in a Slavic-Turkic-adjacent register, followed by a clan craft epithet that describes the clan's hereditary specialization, not the individual's personal history. Krag Stoneshaper is not personally a stoneshaper — they are a member of the Stoneshaper clan, which has practiced stone masonry across generations. This means meeting an Ûrgon whose epithet is personal rather than clan-derived — a descriptor that reflects something the individual specifically did rather than what their people do — is a signal worth reading. That Ûrgon has left their clan, or been removed from it, or has done something significant enough that a personal epithet has been attached, regardless of the clan's. A clanless Ûrgon is a person navigating the world without the framework that normally structures their identity, and the name tells you that before they say a word about it.

### **Badaki culture**

Badaki culture is discussed in Chapter 4 through the lens of Boros, but the practice of it at the daily level is worth adding: Gwan'tor's social hierarchy is fluid within the clan and fixed between them. Within a clan, a Badaki who masters their chosen skill rises. Between clans, the hierarchy is historical and contested and the subject of the clan disputes that make up a significant portion of Gwan'tor's internal politics. A Badaki mercenary operating in the Inner Nations is navigating a world without the clan framework, which most of them find clarifying in ways they would not have predicted — the absence of the structure that governs them at home reveals what their actual values are, as opposed to the ones the structure enforced.

Badaki names follow the structure of a short first name — strong vowels, occasional fricative clusters — followed by an earned epithet awarded by the clan elders after a notable act. Khavra Ironwall. Bahu Laughing-Death. Sura Three-Cuts. The epithet marks a specific moment; it is the most compressed form of biography the Badaki use. A Badaki who does not yet carry an epithet is young, still in the process of earning one, or — in the cases that matter — has had one revoked. Revocation is not standard. It happens when an act of significant disgrace requires the elders to make a collective judgment that the old epithet no longer fits and that no new one is yet warranted. A Badaki operating under only a first name knows what that absence communicates and rarely volunteers an explanation.

### **Messalyan Culture**

Messalya has no capital that stays in one place and no unified government that speaks for more than the city-states currently willing to agree on something, which means it functions better than most of the Known World's formally organized nations and considerably more chaotically than any of them would admit to. The confederation's coherence comes from shared geography — the desert conditions that make unilateral survival impractical — and from Achelon's circuit, which brings the mobile city to every major oasis settlement over the course of eight months and generates the face-to-face negotiations that produce the decisions that written communication cannot. A Messalyan diplomat who wants something to happen arranges to be at the right oasis when Achelon arrives. The timing of the turtle's circuit is the single most strategically important piece of information in Messalyan politics.

The three resources that define desert power — water, shade, and navigable route — structure the confederation's internal hierarchy more honestly than any formal treaty. The city-states that control the largest oases control the most leverage, and the leverage is not subtle: if your caravan needs to cross territory where another city-state has the only reliable water in three days of travel, the negotiation is happening at that city-state's terms. The Messalyan political tradition has developed extremely sophisticated customs around hospitality precisely because the power differential at a water source is too naked to leave unmediated. The hospitality is genuine and the customs are enforced, and both facts are true simultaneously.

The stone Raiders occupy their own social category inside Messalyan culture — respected, somewhat apart, and organized around the specific expertise of following Kaira's path and extracting value from the geological disturbances she creates. The Raiders are not a formal guild; membership is based on demonstrated competence and the willingness of existing Raiders to work alongside a candidate, which is a form of credentialing that produces a smaller, more reliable group than any formal institution would. Their relationship with Achelon's leadership — the community of priests, navigators, and caretakers who manage the turtle's welfare and interpret her movements — is part commercial and part religious, which the Raiders themselves do not distinguish between.

Messalya's strategic position in 1,380 TE is that of a nation Val Cadma cannot easily project military power into — the desert is a better defense than most walls — but that is commercially vulnerable to the trade route disruptions that Cadman expansion is beginning to produce. The three great Messalyan trading families (all with Messalyan-origin names now carried through Solaran institutions as well) understand this precisely and are currently calculating whether the republican alliance or a separate accommodation serves their interests better. The calculation is not settled.

Adventurers come to Messalya for the Stone Raiders' gems, for city-state contracts that come without standing armies attached, for the transit economy that pays well for reliable route-clearing, and for work involving Kaira directly — the creature's passage creates conditions that produce both valuable discoveries and things that should not have been disturbed.

Names. Messalyan names draw from three overlapping strands: the Arabic-Egyptian register of the desert city-states, the West African substrate of the trading families who built Achelon's early institutions, and the hybrid forms that develop wherever both traditions have coexisted long enough. Farid, Amon-Drak, Nassira, Yusra, Ibou, Aissata. Surnames in the desert tradition are locational — attached to the oasis, city, or feature that a family controls or was named for — because in the Messalyan desert, geography is identity before it is anything else. The Stone Raiders have their own naming subculture: short names, often single-syllable or doubled, with personal epithets that reflect the work. Skarn, Raka, Doru Two-Palms. A Stone Raider carrying a long, formal Messalyan surname came from money, whether or not they still have it.

### **Val’arian Culture**

The Val'arian civil war is being conducted with considerable restraint by both sides, which is either evidence of cultural sophistication or evidence that neither faction has yet acquired sufficient external funding to escalate. The northern conservatives control the Ancestors' Fields and the narrative of Val'arian identity. The southern commercialists control Shadowport and most of the current cash flow. The High King at Tor Mantar controls the formal apparatus of unified governance and the increasingly ceremonial authority attached to it. All three understand that the Val'arian navigational advantage — the seven centuries of accumulated knowledge that makes Val'arian pilots the most sought-after in the Inner Sea — is the thing every external power actually wants, and that destroying the nation in the process of fighting over it would eliminate the leverage both factions are trying to claim.

Val'arian maritime culture is the substrate that both factions draw from and argue about simultaneously. The navigational tradition was built by the people who arrived seven centuries ago from wherever they came from — the origin question being one that the Val'arians themselves disagree on, or decline to answer, depending on who is asking — and has been refined through continuous practice ever since. The techniques are not written down in forms accessible to outsiders. They are transmitted through apprenticeship, embedded in the specific vocabulary of Val'arian seamanship that does not fully translate into Common, and tied to the interpretation of weather patterns and current behavior that requires years of accumulated personal observation. A Val'arian pilot who defects to another nation takes their knowledge with them. They do not take the tradition. The tradition requires the community to sustain.

The northern faction's relationship to the Ancestors' Fields is both deeply sincere and politically instrumental, in proportions that vary by individual. The Fields — the burial grounds and ceremonial sites that predate the current civil war and in some cases predate any Val'arian alive today — are what the northern faction means when it says "Val'aria." The southern faction's response is that Val'aria is the people, not the land, and that a nation willing to trade in the Inner Sea's most sought-after expertise is better positioned than a nation defending graves. Both arguments contain real Val'arian values, which is precisely what makes the civil war so difficult to resolve: there is no position here that is simply wrong.

The external powers that are funding both sides — selectively, usually through commercial arrangements rather than direct military investment — have concluded that a Val'aria in permanent low-intensity civil conflict is, from their perspective, approximately as useful as a unified one, and considerably less dangerous. A unified Val'aria with a settled succession and a coherent foreign policy would be the most powerful naval nation in the Known World. Neither the Magic Bloc nor Val Cadma is entirely unhappy with the current situation, which is something that thoughtful Val'arians across both factions have noticed and are not pleased about.

Adventurers come to Val'aria for navigation contracts, for the civil war's perpetual need for discreet operators on both sides, for the specific artifacts that Val'arian culture produces, and for whatever is in the Ancestors' Fields that both factions claim the other is misappropriating.

**Names.** Val'arian names divide along the civil war's fault line. Northern Val'arians use names in a soft elvish register — longer, with liquid consonants and endings that trail rather than stop. Felorin, Ondathil, Silvander, Vella, Mirwen. Family names reference vessels or geographic features from the Ancestors' Fields records: Tidewalker, Longkeel, Deepanchor. These names appear in the land registry, in the ceremonial records, in the legal documents the northern faction is currently using to contest the south's commercial autonomy — they are property as much as identity. Southern Val'arians use shorter names with warmer sounds and an ease of pronunciation that reflects years of talking to merchants from every nation on the Inner Sea. Bello, Tomlin, Sira, Unda. Family names are port-of-origin or trade-based: Harbormere, Saltmast, Portsmith. The two naming traditions are now so associated with their respective factions that a Val'arian introducing themselves with a northern-register name in a southern port is either from the north, or making a political statement — and the locals will assume the latter until given reason otherwise.

### **Vartran Culture**

The Vartrans have been in a deteriorating strategic situation for long enough that they have stopped being surprised by how deteriorating it has become. What has changed in the past fifteen years — since the Cadman occupation of Norderenn removed the overland route to Bellaria — is that the deterioration now has a pace, and the pace is visible. Val Cadma has not yet moved against Bal'mekh directly. The Tar'amaan theological position on draconic ancestry — that it is heresy, full stop, with the same institutional response that magical practice receives in Cadman-controlled territory — is well-established doctrine. The timing of application is a strategic question, and Kranzler has not yet answered it. The Vartran clans are operating on the assumption that the answer will arrive before they have finished preparing for it.

The adaptation that makes Vartran life possible in Bal'mekh — geothermal springs for heat, rare crystal trade for the currency that buys what the territory cannot produce, deep community organization that distributes resources through extended clan networks rather than market mechanisms — is also the adaptation that makes Bal'mekh difficult to occupy and expensive to destroy. The Vartrans know the terrain in a way that no external military command can quickly replicate. They have maintained that knowledge across eleven centuries of isolation, and the Northern Wall's Eymanarynn vestiges — the ancient construction that predates the Second Era and that the Vartrans have had longer to study than anyone else currently living — have provided both practical resources and the kind of historical context that changes what you think is possible.

The Order of Magnus's Tower of Nyrün sits on Bal'mekh's eastern coast by an arrangement that was negotiated without the Vartran clans fully understanding what the Order intended to do there, which is a diplomatic wound that has not fully closed. The Vartrans permit it because they need allies and the Order is the ally available. What the Order has actually been doing at Nyrün — the research that senior staff have declined to describe to anyone outside the tower — is a standing source of Vartran suspicion that the Order has not addressed directly. The arrangement holds because the Vartrans have concluded that an imperfect ally is better than the alternative, which is no ally at all. The Order has concluded, correctly, that the Vartrans have nowhere else to go. Both parties know the other has made the same calculation.

The clan elders who govern Vartran society are responsible for decisions across timescales that individual lifespans do not fully cover, which produces a specific relationship to urgency: measured where others would be hasty, and capable of sudden commitment when the calculation is complete, because delay after clarity is waste. A Vartran elder who has been listening to a situation develop for an hour and has not yet spoken has not decided. One who speaks has decided, and the decision is not a starting position for negotiation.

Adventurers come to Bal'mekh for the Northern Wall's artifacts — Eymanarynn construction of a type that appears nowhere else in the accessible Known World — for Vartran contracts that pay in rare crystal and in information the clans have accumulated over eleven centuries of proximity to the Dead Lands' eastern edge, and for the Tower of Nyrün, which maintains a standing offer for qualified researchers and an equally standing reputation for not sharing its findings with the people who share its walls.

**Names.** Vartrans use single names only — long, consonant-heavy, built around fricatives and clusters that carry the audible mark of a throat shaped differently from a human one. Vryndakth, Kzaravel, Brakthnyr, Threkval. The phonology is not ornamental. It reflects the draconic ancestry that Tar'amaan doctrine classifies as heresy, which means a Vartran speaking their own name in Cadman-adjacent territory is announcing what they are. This is understood. Vartrans operating outside Bal'mekh typically do not introduce themselves by name to strangers. A name is given when the Vartran has decided the relationship warrants it, which is a decision they make deliberately and not on a timeline anyone else sets. An NPC Vartran who has not yet given their name is not an oversight in the text — the scene is working correctly.

### **Major Factions**

The following organizations operate across national borders, pursue goals that no single government controls, and represent the layer of power that sits above or beside the political map. Each entry covers what the faction wants, what it fears, who it is currently in conflict with, and how adventurers are likely to encounter it.

---

#### **The Chroniclers**

**What they want:** Control of the information flow from the Dead Lands, and through that control, influence over the obsidian economy that is currently reshaping the world. The Chroniclers present themselves as scholars dedicated to preserving Eymanarynn and Vysandri knowledge. This is accurate. It is also incomplete. The Chroniclers understood before most governments did that controlling access to the Dead Lands meant controlling access to the resource that would determine the next century's military and economic hierarchy, and they moved to institutionalize that control before anyone had the capacity to contest it.

Their stated mission — preservation of historical knowledge — and their actual mission — monopoly on Dead Lands access — are not in contradiction. They genuinely preserve things. The Great Library in King's Hold contains knowledge that would otherwise not exist. What they do not do is share that knowledge on any terms except their own, and their own terms happen to coincide with maintaining the monopoly.

The monopoly framing describes most of what the Chroniclers withhold, but not all of it. Their most senior scholars have reached the deepest classification tier by different paths — decades of fieldwork, the restricted archive, a pattern in field reports that the general membership never reads in aggregate — and have arrived at a conclusion they do not circulate even internally. A subset of what they hold is classified for reasons that have nothing to do with market position. They cannot fully articulate what catastrophe the containment prevents. They have concluded that knowing is not required to justify the containment, and that distributing the inference would require distributing the evidence, and the evidence is precisely what the containment exists to suppress.

**What they fear:** Discovery of alternative obsidian sources that bypass their controlled access points. Sustained military pressure on Bellaria that compromises the political cover their operation depends on. Internal defection — a senior member with access to their classification system decides to sell to another party. They have faced all three threats at various points and have responded to each with a competence that suggests they think about contingencies the way other organizations think about daily operations.

**Who they conflict with:** The Crimson Company, whose smuggling operations directly undercut the monopoly. The Ordo Draconis, who want access to Dead Lands sites for reasons the Chroniclers find professionally offensive and strategically alarming. Val Cadma, whose strategic interest in cutting off the Magic Bloc's obsidian supply requires contesting Chronicler control. Individual adventuring companies that return from unlicensed expeditions with material the Chroniclers consider confiscable.

**How adventurers encounter them:** As employers, as obstacles, or as both simultaneously. Chronicler contracts are well-paying, professionally organized, and accompanied by legal agreements that transfer ownership of any significant discovery to the Chroniclers' institutional custody. Parties that sign them get access and support. Parties that do not get patrols and confiscation orders. The Chroniclers also maintain an active intelligence operation — their observer network in cities along the Emerald Corridor and in every major port is not purely archaeological.

The Chroniclers' presence on both sides of the Norderenn occupation is the clearest expression of their institutional position: they have concluded that political neutrality is more valuable than political alignment, and they maintain it with the rigor of people who have done the calculation and committed to the result. A Norderenn resistance fighter and a Cadman administrator can both find themselves escorting a Chronicler expedition to the same Dead Lands site. The Chroniclers consider this a successful outcome.

---

#### **The Order of Magnus**

**What they want:** The preservation and expansion of magical knowledge, and specifically the protection of magical practice from the institutional persecution that Val Cadma's expansion represents. The Order was founded on the premise that magic is Ilya's gift to the mortal world and that using it responsibly is both a right and a practice requiring serious study. What it has become, under pressure from Cadman expansion, is also a political resistance organization, a refugee network, and the Known World's most sophisticated research institution — all three, simultaneously, whether it intended to be or not.

The Order's internal debate between pure scholarship and political engagement has been running for approximately sixty years, since Cadman persecution became severe enough that "we only do research" stopped being a defensible position when mages were being burned in territories the Order had members in. The current Archmagister has come down on the side of engagement without fully committing to it, which means the Order does more than pure scholarship and less than it could, and has members who think both things simultaneously.

**What they fear:** The obsidian supply being cut off, which would cripple both their research and the flying ship capacity the Magic Bloc depends on. Internal compromise — Cadman intelligence has been attempting to place agents inside the Order for decades, and the Order's internal security apparatus has caught several, which means there are probably some they have not caught. The Ordo Draconis, whose goals the Order considers the specific form of catastrophe that magical research, badly conducted, is supposed to prevent.

**Who they conflict with:** Val Cadma directly, as the institution that is burning their members and suppressing their practice. The Chroniclers indirectly, on the question of who controls access to Eymanarynn knowledge, the Order wants to study what the Chroniclers prefer to classify. Individual governments that want the Order's expertise without the Order's institutional independence.

**How adventurers encounter them:** As employers for research missions, recovery operations, and escort contracts involving magical materials or personnel. As a resource, the Order's libraries are the best outside the Chroniclers' own collection, and the Order charges for access in the form of favors rather than coin, which tends to be more expensive in practice. As a source of sanctuary for magic-users operating in Cadman-adjacent territory. The Tower of Nyrün in Bal'mekh is the Order's most secretive installation, staffed by researchers who applied specifically for a posting far from oversight, which tells you something about what they are doing there.

---

#### **The Crimson Company**

**What they want:** Money, operational freedom, and the continuation of conditions that make both available. The Company's origin story — a pirate vessel, an infamous captain, a reputation built on the Winter's Bay's treacherous waters — has calcified into institutional mythology that the current leadership maintains because mythology is good for recruitment and better for deterrence. What the Company actually is in 1,380 TE is the Known World's most sophisticated smuggling operation, with piracy as a secondary income stream and a strategic tool rather than a primary business model.

The obsidian trade made the Company indispensable. Every nation that wants obsidian outside the Chroniclers' licensing structure — and there are several, including some that officially comply with that structure — needs a distribution network that does not ask questions about origin documentation. The Company provides that network from Wightport, through routes that use Vysandri tunnel systems, Val'arian navigation expertise, and Messalyan port contacts in combinations that the Chroniclers' intelligence operation has been trying to map for twenty years without completing the picture.

**What they fear:** The Chroniclers developing a comprehensive map of their routes. Val Cadma is achieving the naval capacity to contest Winter’s Bay, which would threaten Wightport's geographic protection. Internal succession — the Council of Sea Wolves governs by consensus among five captains, and the history of that arrangement includes two violent reorganizations in the past century. A third is probably due.

**Who they conflict with:** The Chroniclers, as the entity whose monopoly they undercut. Bellaria's formal navy, with whom they maintain an undeclared detente that holds because both sides have concluded open conflict is more expensive than the current arrangement. Occasionally, each other, when a captain decides another captain's territory is now their territory.

**How adventurers encounter them:** As employers for operations, the Company cannot run using its own personnel — plausible deniability being a resource the Company manages carefully. As the only viable transport option for parties that need to move through Winter’s Bay without Chronicler documentation. As antagonists, when a party's mission intersects with something the Company is protecting, or when a party has been hired by someone the Company has decided is a threat. Membership in the Company is not open to casual applicants; the Blood Baptism that initiates full members is a genuine test, not a formality.

---

#### **The College of Whispers**

The College's founding mythology holds that it began in the final decades of the Second Era — scholars and bards who concluded, watching the Eymanarynn collapse, that information was the one weapon the resistance had not deployed. Whether this is accurate history or a founding narrative constructed to give the institution moral weight it found useful is a question the College has never troubled to resolve, and may have left unresolved deliberately, because the ambiguity serves them in the present. What the mythology implies, if accurate, is that the College predates every nation currently seated at any political table in the Known World by eleven centuries. The Known World's empires have risen and become the ruins that expeditions now catalog. The College has been watching the whole time. Their decisions come from that position — a material institutional condition, not a rhetorical one — and their institutional memory is correspondingly longer than anything else available.

**What they want:** The visible objective, assembled from the College's documented history of actions, is something like: prevent any single faction from achieving the dominance that would make the rest of the board irrelevant. Whether this is principled or self-serving depends on how you define the College's interests. An organization specializing in information and covert capabilities has a structural incentive to maintain a world where those capabilities have value. A single dominant power would have no use for the College and limited tolerance for its existence. The balance the College maintains is the balance in which the College is indispensable. Their most honest members have engaged with this problem directly and have not resolved it.

The structure that outside intelligence services have partially mapped — and that the College has never confirmed or denied — runs in three layers. The Outer Circle consists of independent operators who receive contracts through cutouts and frequently have no knowledge that they serve the College; they believe they work for a private patron, a merchant consortium, a well-connected fixer. Many have spent entire careers inside this layer without learning otherwise. The Middle Circle knows it serves the College, knows its local handlers, and knows nothing above them. The Inner Circle — the actual decision-making body — has maintained its anonymity against the combined intelligence efforts of the Inquisition, the Chroniclers' observer network, the Order of Magnus, and every competent government in the Known World for the entire period any of those organizations have been looking. Either the College is extraordinarily careful, or the structure is more decentralized than the three-circle model assumes. Possibly both.

**What they fear:** Being mapped. The College's operational security is its primary asset; an adversary who understands its structure, personnel, and methods can dismantle it, and the College invests more in counter-intelligence than most nations commit to their entire intelligence apparatus. Val Cadma's expansion represents the specific strategic outcome their stated mission was built to prevent, and the Church of Tar'amaan is the closest thing the College has to a standing enemy for this reason, not because the Church has disrupted specific College operations, but because the Cadman project, achieved, produces a Known World in which the College cannot operate. A Tar'amaan-dominated world is one in which the College's services have no buyers outside Val Cadma, and Val Cadma has no interest in contracting with an institution it cannot control.

The Inquisition's methods create a specific operational problem that standard College networks were not designed to withstand. An Inquisition sweep through a Middle Circle network can collapse a decade of careful construction in a week, because the methods used in Inquisition hearings were designed specifically to extract what people know, and Middle Circle operators know enough to be damaging. The College's current operations in Cadman-controlled territory run at maximum compartmentalization and minimal personnel. No operator knows more than their immediate handler and their immediate task. Handlers know their operator and their upward contact, nothing else. This is expensive and slow, and the College has accepted both costs.

**Who they conflict with:** Everyone, selectively, on a rotating basis. The College maintains no permanent enemies — it maintains current operational conflicts that shift as the political situation shifts. Their relationship with the Chroniclers is the most complex: overlapping intelligence interests produce occasional information-sharing and occasional direct opposition, depending on whether their current assessments of what the balance requires have converged or diverged. The Church of Tar'amaan is the exception that holds regardless of political context — an expansionist project with a theological mandate toward comprehensive dominance is precisely what the College was organized to resist.

Assassination is the College's most visible reputation and its least frequent tool. The calculation is direct: killing someone generates an investigation, removes an asset from the board, and signals capability in ways that compromise future operations. An adversary who knows the College kills will look for College activity with different attention than one who believes they are managing a purely political situation. The College prefers to redirect, discredit, neutralize through information, or simply wait out problems that a killing would solve more quickly. When the College has decided that someone needs to die, it means they have exhausted the other methods. That conclusion is the more alarming thing to know — if you have learned the College has moved to that step, you have survived everything they attempted before it without noticing they were attempting it.

**How adventurers encounter them:** Rarely in ways they recognize at the time. A party that realizes mid-contract that they have been working for the College is either being deliberately recruited — the College wants them to know, because what comes next requires knowing — or has been less observant than the College's operational security requires. Recruitment, when it happens overtly, is a conversation in which the College demonstrates it has been watching the party for a specific period and already knows a significant amount about them. The code of loyalty it demands is specific and enforced through methods the College does not disclose. Former members who have left the organization and discussed what they knew have not, as a category, done well afterward.

Bards constitute the College's most effective operational class — not because of magic, but because performers move between courts, mercenary camps, scholarly institutions, and harbors without generating the attention that a recognizable agent would. A bard performing at a minor noble's supper knows that noble's guest list, debts, anxieties, and strategic position before the evening ends, without having asked a question that could be described as intelligence-gathering. The College institutionalized this capability; they did not invent it. Most bards operating in the Known World have no connection to the College. The ones that do are indistinguishable from the ones that don't, which is the point.

The College's relationship with the Vithurian Orchestra has not been confirmed or described by either organization, a silence that tends to mean something. The Orchestra maintains what it describes as a historical observation network. The College operates through performers and information assets across national borders. Both institutions are genuinely old, both function without national affiliation, and both have a sustained interest in understanding the Known World's political landscape. Whether this produces coordination, competition, or some arrangement that is neither has not been established from outside either organization. Neither has clarified.

#### **The Iron Brothers**

The Brotherhood's founding mythology centers on dragon-slaying. Whether the specific accounts are accurate is a question the Brotherhood considers irrelevant — what the mythology establishes is the standard: the kind of work a Brother is for. That standard has held across centuries of expanding scope, from the creature threats along the Emerald Corridor to the Darkness-corrupted things that now emerge from the Dead Lands in forms the Brotherhood's historical files cannot fully predict.

**What they want:** The Brotherhood's purpose is, by the standards of the factions operating at a continental scale, unusually direct. Creatures that threaten populations are dead. Contracts that fund the capability. The operational independence to pursue the first without the second distorting it. The oath — sworn at initiation by every member, from Initiates newly accepted to Masters who have coordinated regional chapters for decades — is the mechanism that keeps those three things aligned. Brothers do not kill human targets. Brothers do not deploy their capabilities in the service of political objectives. The limitation is structural, not aspirational: the Brotherhood's value depends on every government and employer knowing that a hired Brother cannot be redirected toward human problems, which means the oath cannot bend for exceptions without collapsing the thing that makes the Brotherhood worth contracting.

Enforcement runs through the Fallen Brother designation — a professional blacklisting circulated through every mercenary network in the Known World. The Brotherhood does not pursue oath-breakers. They circulate the designation, and the mercenary market for a Fallen Brother collapses accordingly: no retainer that recognizes it will work alongside one; most contracts explicitly exclude them. The Brotherhood calculates, accurately, that this holds well enough to maintain even accounting for the members it has failed to deter.

Internally, the Brotherhood operates through regional chapters, each led by a Warden. Continental coordination falls to a rotating Masters Council whose convocations are announced shortly before they occur, at locations that change each cycle. There is no single head. The Council governs by consensus among senior Wardens — a slow process, and one that cannot be disrupted by eliminating any individual. Organizational efficiency was the trade. Structural resilience was what they got, and they have calculated correctly each time someone has tested it.

**What they fear:** Being co-opted — not through direct corruption, but through the gradual reframing of their mandate that well-resourced organizations are patient enough to attempt. The Invictus faction inside the Church of Tar'amaan has approached the Brotherhood twice with contracts framed as creature elimination that the Brotherhood's Masters identified as political assassination, with a pretext constructed around it. Both were declined with a courtesy that the Masters calculated was more useful than a refusal that communicated the full extent of their contempt. The Invictus has not stopped. Their next approach will be more carefully built, and the Brotherhood maintains a file.

The Dead Lands corrupted creatures represent a different category of threat — not to the oath, but to members in the field. Uncorrupted creatures follow behavioral patterns that the Brotherhood's accumulated files can reliably predict. Corrupted ones do not behave consistently with any prior record, and the gap between historical expectation and field encounter has killed more Brothers in the past two decades than in the fifty years before that. The Brotherhood's creature files — the most comprehensive and most honestly written documents of their kind in the Known World — are being revised in real time, and the revisions are arriving after the chapters already in the field have committed to engagements based on the previous edition.

**Who they conflict with:** The Ordo Draconis, whose activities in and around the Dead Lands disturb territories and release corrupted creatures, the Brotherhood then deploys to address — a dynamic that neither organization advertises and that the Brotherhood has only partially mapped. Individual governments that want the Brotherhood's combat capability directed at human problems, and who keep arriving with contract framings that do not clear the oath; several have tried across the past century, and all have been declined. The Brotherhood's relationship with the Chroniclers is not hostile but has friction: both organizations accumulate intelligence about the Dead Lands as a consequence of their primary work, and their assessments of what that intelligence means do not consistently align.

The intelligence the Brotherhood generates is the faction's most politically inconvenient asset. Tracking creature movements at a continental scale produces data that functions, practically, as territorial surveillance — the Brotherhood knows what is moving where in the Dead Lands, which sectors have been newly disturbed, which Vysandri ruins have acquired a different class of occupant. Every significant obsidian rush in the past century was preceded by Iron Brothers chapters withdrawing from specific sectors. The expeditions that pay attention to this pattern have better survival rates. The Brotherhood has declined to formalize or sell this intelligence, citing the oath's restriction on deploying their capabilities for political purposes — which is accurate — and the fact that selling territorial intelligence about the Dead Lands would function as serving one obsidian interest against another, which is also accurate. What is not stated, and can be inferred from the Brotherhood's behavior, is that the intelligence is worth more as structural leverage than as a commodity. Every faction that wants Dead Lands access has concluded it cannot afford to be the one that makes the Brotherhood hostile.

**How adventurers encounter them:** The Brotherhood maintains referral lists of independent operators who have demonstrated competence against specific creature categories. A party with a documented kill record will find contact arriving before they seek it. When a chapter-level threat exceeds local capacity and the Masters decide to supplement with proven independents, the invitation comes through the referral network — directly, without intermediaries, which is itself a signal about what the contract involves. The Brotherhood's creature files are shared with any party preparing to engage a species they have encountered. A well-informed party kills the creature more often, and the creature being dead is what the Brotherhood cares about.

Rangers constitute the plurality of Brotherhood membership — not because the Brotherhood recruits for class, but because the skill set a Ranger develops through experience with a specific creature type is precisely what the Brotherhood's training program builds toward. The Brotherhood's Initiate program accelerates this deliberately: first-year members operate in the field under a senior Brother, working their assigned creature category across a range of encounters, before the Brotherhood considers them ready to operate independently. Assignments are matched to prior experience. The Masters consider this obvious rather than sophisticated, which says something about how the Brotherhood thinks about preparation.

#### **The Duskhold Weapon Masters**

Duskhold is not easy to find, which is part of the point. The fortress sits deep in Gwan'tor territory, carved into a mountain face that the Badaki have never seen fit to name on maps intended for outside eyes. Warriors who travel there for the first time do so by following a sponsor, not directions.

It trains Fighters, Paladins, and Champions in the warrior tradition of Boros — not the theory of it, not an interpretation of it, but the practice as the Badaki have preserved and refined it across centuries of mountain warfare and mercenary deployment. What that training looks like from the inside is not widely discussed by those who have completed it. What is widely known is what a Duskhold-trained warrior looks like in a fight: someone who has been taught to solve problems rather than to perform solving them.

**Admission.** Badaki warriors enter as a matter of ancestry and community standing. For everyone else, entry requires a Badaki sponsor — a Duskhold graduate willing to put their own name behind the candidate's potential. The sponsor's responsibility does not end at the gate. If the person they vouched for later taints the academy's name, the sponsor's standing inside Gwan'tor's warrior communities goes with it.

The one requirement non-negotiable for any candidate, Badaki or otherwise, is adherence to Boros's ethos. This does not demand formal conversion to the faith. It demands that the candidate understand what the Way of Boros requires of a warrior — honesty in combat, direct confrontation over cleverness, mastery pursued for its own sake rather than for advantage — and demonstrate, to the Badaki masters' satisfaction, that this understanding is not a performance. Candidates who arrive treating the ethos as a formality to be cleared tend to leave before the first trial is finished.

**The Final Trial and the Pendant of the Two Hammers.** The full training at Duskhold ends with a final trial whose details are not disclosed outside the fortress. Graduates who pass receive a pendant bearing two crossed hammers, consecrated by Badaki Clerics in a ceremony restricted to those who have trained there.

The pendant cannot be worn by anyone other than its rightful owner. Anyone else who attempts it experiences severe and immediate pain — no visible injury, no lasting mark, but sufficient to make the situation clear. This is not a flaw in the consecration.

A graduate wearing the pendant is recognized across warrior communities throughout the Known World — mercenary companies, Boroite communities, military orders, the courts of nations that employ professional soldiers — as a **Duskhold Weapon Master**. The recognition is rarely explained and rarely needs to be.

**Accountability.** Duskhold does not pursue graduates who have simply failed to live up to their training. It has no interest in patrolling the moral failures of every warrior who passed through its gates. What it will not tolerate is deliberate disgrace: a Weapon Master who acts in direct contradiction of the Way of Boros while trading on Duskhold's reputation. When this happens — rarely, and remembered each time — the response is formal. The Badaki Clerics revoke the consecration. The pendant goes inert and is returned to Duskhold. The sponsor, if still living, bears the social cost within Gwan'tor's warrior communities. The former Weapon Master keeps whatever the training gave them in skill. Nothing else.

### **Regional Factions**

The following organizations operate within narrower geographic ranges. They matter enormously to campaigns set in their regions and are a background context for campaigns set elsewhere.

---

**The Merchant Council** (Endoras) controls the guild federation that effectively governs Endoras's economic life alongside the Great Council. Its seven affiliated guilds — Grain, Iron, Spice, Livestock, Drink, Trade, and the politely named Guild of Vices — represent the commercial infrastructure of the most productive agricultural nation in the Known World. What the Merchant Council wants is price stability and route security; what it fears is the Great Council developing enough independence to regulate it; what it is currently dealing with is Val Cadma's economic pressure campaign and the question of which guild leaderships have been penetrated by Cadman agents.

**The Collector's Guild** (primarily Solara, with contacts in every major port) operates the Known World's sophisticated black market for Dead Lands artifacts — antiquarians, rare booksellers, and jewelers with legitimate storefronts and very selective back rooms. The Guild's principal fear is the Chroniclers, whose confiscation authority it undermines with every transaction. Its principal asset is information: the Guild knows who has what and who wants what, and that knowledge is worth more than most of the objects it brokers.

**The Invictus** (Val Cadma, internal) is the ultra-conservative faction inside the Church of Tar'amaan that considers the current arrangement — Empress Evelyn Poldark governing alongside Church authority — an unsatisfactory compromise. The Invictus wants a theocratic state where temporal and spiritual authority are unified under the High Pontiff. Its members identify each other by inverted solar rune rings and operate through a network of "Purifiers" — internal inquisitors who investigate the orthodoxy of the Church's own hierarchy. The Invictus is not the Church's leadership, but a persistent pressure inside the Church that shapes decisions at the margins and is waiting for a crisis large enough to produce the restructuring it wants.

**The Ordo Draconis** (cells throughout the Known World) believes the Darkness's imprisonment was an act of cosmic injustice — that the "balance" Ilianism describes was broken when Ilya drove the greatest Illyan into the Abyss, and that restoring the Darkness to the mortal world would restore a proper order. Its members are individually smart enough to know how this sounds, which is why recruitment focuses on people who have concluded that the world's current arrangement is fundamentally corrupt and are looking for a framework that explains why. The Ordo's operational cells are small and independent; central coordination exists but is not accessible to anyone below a specific internal rank. The Order of Magnus tracks the Ordo as its most serious adversarial concern and is currently aware of three cells. There are more.

**The Stone Raiders** (Messalya) began as a collective of miners following Kaira's geological disturbances and have become the most specialized gem prospecting operation in the Known World, with expertise in identifying, extracting, and valuing crystals formed under conditions that exist nowhere except in the path of a centuries-old giant turtle. The Stone Raiders' operational problem is that their entire business model depends on Kaira's continued circuit, and Kaira's health and motivation are not fully understood by anyone. The Raiders maintain a dedicated relationship with Achelon's leadership — specifically, with the people responsible for Kaira's welfare — that is part commercial partnership and part religious observance.

**The Norderenn Resistance** is not a single organization, but a collection of regional cells, clan-based networks, and individual operators who share the goal of ending the Cadman occupation and the difficulty of coordinating without a communications infrastructure that the occupation's intelligence services have spent fifteen years trying to intercept. Bellarian material support flows through the resistance's northern networks; the Chroniclers maintain contact with specific cells for reasons of Dead Lands access logistics; and Val Cadma's occupation administration has a standing bounty schedule for resistance leadership that has been operating long enough to have developed its own economics. A party that works for the resistance in one region may find that the cell in the next region has no knowledge of them and limited interest in trusting them on the previous cell's word.

### **For the GM: Factions as Moving Parts**

Factions work at the table when they have active agendas — things they are doing right now that the party can affect, interrupt, benefit from, or be caught between.

**Every contract has a context.** A Chronicler expedition contract is not just a paying job, but the Chroniclers asserting their access monopoly over a specific Dead Lands site, which means someone else wanted access to that site, which means there is a conflict the party is now a part of. Who wanted access and why is a question the Chroniclers may or may not have disclosed, and the answer matters when the party gets there and finds evidence of a previous unauthorized expedition.

**Faction conflicts create opportunities.** The Crimson Company and the Chroniclers are in permanent low-intensity conflict over the obsidian trade. A party that understands both organizations can identify moments when serving one creates leverage with the other, or when being seen to serve one makes the other an enemy. Neither organization offers neutrality as an option once a party has worked visibly for either.

**The Ordo Draconis does not announce itself.** Cells operate behind legitimate covers. An academic at Solara's university who has been studying Eymanarynn inscriptions for twenty years may be an Ordo member whose research is oriented toward a specific goal that becomes clear only when the party understands what the inscriptions describe. The Ordo is not cartoonish. Its members have real reasons for their beliefs. Those reasons are wrong in ways that, if the party understands the cosmological stakes from Chapter 1, are specifically alarming.

**The Church's fear has a rational origin.** Tar'amaan theology on arcane magic did not emerge from ignorance — the Church's founding scholars had access to Second Era records describing the First Era's collapse, and what those records contained established a causal connection between arcane practice and civilizational catastrophe that a careful reader would find coherent. The diagnosis they constructed was wrong about cause and correct about scale. If the party ever examines Church founding documents closely — the early encyclicals, not the devotional texts — they will find an argument more reasoned than superstition: evidence assembled by people who believed, with some justification, that they were preventing a recurrence. They were wrong about the variable. The fear was earned.

**Faction loyalty is not fixed.** The Invictus has approach scripts for every type of organization it thinks might be useful. An Iron Brothers chapter, a Chronicler expedition leader, and an Order of Magnus researcher — all have been approached, all have varying degrees of resistance to those approaches, and some have accepted terms they have not disclosed to their home organizations. When a faction NPC behaves inconsistently with their stated affiliation, the Invictus is one of the better explanations.

**The resistance is not a monolith, and the occupation is not uniform.** Running Norderenn stories honestly means running a resistance that has regional leaders who do not trust each other, supply lines that are sometimes interrupted by people on the resistance's own side, and a political goal — restoring Boris I's full authority — that not every resistance member actually supports over the alternative of a post-occupation settlement that might look different. The occupation, meanwhile, has administrators who are true believers, administrators who are careerists, and administrators who find themselves in Norderenn because this is where their career sent them, and who are quietly appalled by the Inquisition's methods and not quite appalled enough to do anything about it.
