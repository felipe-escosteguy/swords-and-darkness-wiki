# **Chapter 1: The Shape of Things That Were**

*The Structure of Reality, the Divine Order, and What Lies Beyond*

---

There are no written records of the First Era. Whatever the Eymanarynn and the Vysandri knew about the shape of reality — whatever they inscribed, carved, or preserved — is gone. The Dead Lands hold ruins but not libraries. What the Known World calls its creation mythology is assembled from oral tradition, from fragmentary Third Era accounts, and from theological arguments between scholars who have been disputing the same details for eleven centuries.

That caveat matters. The account below represents the version that scholars of Val Cadma and the Druidic Compact — deeply opposed on nearly every other subject — broadly agree on. It is the Known World's best understanding of its own origins, arrived at without access to primary sources. Where traditions diverge sharply, this chapter notes it. Where they converge, that convergence is worth something, even if it falls short of proof.

What is not in dispute is the consequence.

---

## **THE THREE SISTERS AND THE NINE**

The world was made by three, shaped by nine, and then handed forward.

Ilya, Wysla, and Ayla are the Three Sisters. Ilya is the force of creation — the first mover, the impulse that gave form to formlessness. Wysla is entropy, dissolution, the mechanism by which things become other things; she is not malicious, only indifferent to the difference between building and unmaking. Ayla is order, structure, the insistence that things hold their shape long enough to matter. The creation was an act of all three simultaneously. What Ilya willed into being would have collapsed without Ayla's structure and solidified into sterile permanence without Wysla's dissolution. The wheel of life — growth, death, renewal — turns because none of the three has absolute dominance over the other two.

Before mortals existed, the Sisters shaped the Nine. These were beings of a different order — neither divine nor mortal — given the task of sculpting the world's raw material into something that could eventually hold life. Not architects in any simple sense: the Nine worked in geological time, in deep currents, in the slow differentiation of land and sea and the first movements of weather. They predate mortal civilization by longer than mortal civilization has existed. Their work preceded the Eymanarynn, the Vysandri, the Illyans — preceded everything the Known World calls history.

The Nine are still present. Where is a question that produces speculation more readily than answers. What matters for the Known World is that their existence is not mythology in the sense of invention. Something did the work that their existence explains. The Val'arian faith preserved their number and something of their nature, whether by proximity, memory, or something the Val'arians have chosen not to explain. Nine divine beings who sang the world into being, their Chorus theology holds. Ilianist doctrine does not contradict this so much as address a different part of the same event.

The alignment axis governing mortal behavior has its foundation here. Chaos and order are not abstractions — they are Wysla and Ayla's forces made manifest in mortal inclination. A mortal who inclines toward extreme chaos pulls toward dissolution without structure to shape it. A mortal who inclines toward extreme order pulls toward permanence without the dissolution that allows renewal. Both tendencies, carried far enough, break the system they came from. The Sisters built the balance because the world requires balance. A mortal who understands this understands why their alignment has stakes beyond personal preference.

The Church of Tar'amaan reads the three moons differently. In their theology, Tar'amaan is the sun — singular, triumphant, the true god — and the three moons are the Sisters, diminished, rendered cold. This reading requires that the Sisters be dead. The theological consequences of that claim are not subtle: if the Sisters are dead, the balance is gone, and something else fills the vacuum. The Church has a candidate for what fills it.

---

## **THE FIRST ILLYANS**

Ilya knew what magic was — it was, in a sense, her — and she understood what happened when it was handled without comprehension. She created the Illyans to watch over it.

The dragons of the First Era were not the creatures later histories describe: not monsters, not elemental forces, not embodiments of greed. They were divine instruments — vast, ancient, possessed of an intelligence that operated on a different scale than mortal reasoning, tasked with ensuring that the magic emerging in the mortal world did not outpace the wisdom to contain it. Shepherds. That the analogy eventually failed is the premise of everything that followed.

The Eymanarynn awakened first among mortal peoples and were the first to develop significant magical practice. The Vysandri followed — a civilization whose understanding of the physical world reached heights the Third Era has not approached. Their methods were not magical in any sense that later traditions would recognize. They built mechanisms governed by principles, systematic and material, that in a different world might have been called engineering. In the Known World, where magic is the dominant interpretive framework for everything extraordinary, the ruins of Vysandri construction are routinely misread by everyone who encounters them. A thing that still functions after three thousand years, not because it is enchanted but because it was built to last, is more unsettling than a magic item in ways that most scholars are not prepared to sit with.

The elves came after the Vysandri, then the dwarves. Humans stirred near the end of the First Era, when the world was already shaped by older hands.

What this world had achieved is not mythology. The Known World's tendency is to treat anything lost as imperfect in ways the record cannot preserve — a corrective instinct that fails here. Magic was woven into the fabric of things, and the civilizations of the First Era knew how to work with it. The Vysandri built on principles that did not require magic at all, and their work has outlasted everything the Third Era could manage. Every people had their function in a system that had not been arranged by force or compromise but had simply, across generations, become coherent. Something was genuinely good.

For a time, the Illyans held to their purpose. For a time, the balance held.

## **THE NAME THAT WAS ERASED**

The greatest of the Illyans was also the first to reach the wrong conclusion.

What went wrong is not what later traditions call corruption. The entity did not decay or lose what it had been — it reached a conclusion. It had watched the First Era achieve something real, and it was not willing to watch that achievement fail the way everything eventually does. Preservation required control. Someone had to hold the balance in place when the balance showed signs of tilting. Whether that conclusion was wrong is a question with three thousand years of evidence on one side and no resolution on the other. What is not in dispute is the outcome: the First Era ended because one of its guardians decided that guardianship, from then on, required a tighter hand.

Its name is gone. This is not a matter of records being lost — the name was deliberately erased from the memory of every living thing. The creature is called the Darkness in common usage, and the Illyan in certain Ilianist texts, a word that once named the species and came, by the weight of what the greatest among them did, to mean its corruption. The true name exists nowhere in the Known World.

Common tradition holds that the Eymanarynn performed the erasure — that the great civilization of the Second Era had both the power and the will to spend it on something as precise as removing a name from the world's memory. The story has the satisfying shape of symmetry: the same people who bore the highest cost of the Second Era used its enemy's own scale against it.

Scholars find the timing difficult. The Eymanarynn at the end of the Second Era were not intact. Generations of conflict had diminished them, and whether what remained commanded the kind of power a working of that scale required is genuinely contested. Some argue the erasure happened earlier, at the height of Eymanarynn capability rather than its end. Others argue the Eymanarynn were not the authors at all — that something else, with reasons the historical record either doesn't preserve or never knew, performed the work and received no credit because no candidate was visible to the scholars who came after.

The debate continues because it matters. Whoever erased the name understood the Darkness's nature well enough to identify the name itself as a vector — a way of reaching the thing, or summoning its attention, or opening something that should stay closed. That understanding, wherever it originated, is also gone.

---

## **THE VYSANDRI**

The Vysandri are the great unsolved problem of Second Era history.

Common understanding holds that the Darkness destroyed them — exterminated them outright or drove them from everything they had built, leaving behind the ruins that expeditions have been systematically examining for generations. It fits the pattern established elsewhere: the Darkness was the force of subjugation, the Vysandri were among the most capable people alive, and capable people who refused to submit were destroyed. The ruins are evidence of absence. The simplest explanation survives because it is the simplest.

The ruins complicate it. What the Darkness's violence produced elsewhere is recognizable in the record — the marks of destruction, forced removal, the specific damage that military power leaves in a place it has moved through. The deep Vysandri sites do not look like that. They look like a departure. Not flight — the absence of panic is conspicuous. Tools set down mid-use with the implication that someone was expected to come back. Structures sealed rather than breached. An absence that reads, to careful eyes, less like extermination and more like something was taken out of the Vysandri, and they continued to function, simply without whatever had been taken.

What was taken is the question driving every serious expedition into Vysandárien. The mechanisms that still operate after three thousand years run on principles that current engineering cannot reconstruct. The knowledge that produced them is gone. The people who built them are present: the giants of the Known World today are Vysandri by descent, functional and undiminished in body, missing only the part of their civilization that made them worth fearing. Whether that knowledge was destroyed, hidden, or removed — and by whom, and for what reason — most scholars who arrive at this question do not commit their conclusions to paper.

## **THE EYMANARYNN**

The scholarly consensus on the Eymanarynn is cleaner than it deserves to be, partly because the evidence that complicates it has been in plain view for three centuries and nobody has put the right name to it.

What expeditions find in the Dead Lands does not look like the aftermath of a war. The structures emerging from the ice are the wrong kind for a defeated civilization: too regular, too continuous, extending in every direction without obvious center or wall. Corridors below ground run in straight lines for distances that no military purpose requires. In tunnel sections, two parallel metal rails appear, laid on stone crossties, running toward destinations that no current map identifies. Towers of a grey material that predates any Third Era construction technique rise from the ice in clusters, separated by intervals too regular for accident, as if something that once occupied the space between them has collapsed or been buried. The standard interpretation holds that these were ceremonial structures — temples, monuments, the architecture of a civilization that built to honor its gods. The minority interpretation, which most scholars decline to attach their names to, is that they were ordinary infrastructure: the scaffolding of a civilization that built for common use at a scale the Known World associates only with divine work. The two interpretations lead to different questions. The first leads nowhere.

Common tradition describes them as the great civilization of the Second Era: the people who led the resistance against the Darkness, who bore the highest cost, who ultimately gave everything so that the Known World could continue. A sacrifice. Heroic, tragic, and complete. Eymanarann is their monument. The Dead Lands are what the monument became.

The heroism is real. The sacrifice is real. What common tradition does not address, and what most scholarship elides by framing it as defeat rather than choice, is the mechanism.

A minority of historians — working from the same fragmentary Third Era sources as everyone else — note that the Eymanarynn's end does not read like defeat. The final accounts describe not a people being overrun but a people preparing. A mass choosing, if the fragments can be trusted, was conducted with something that looks like deliberate intention rather than desperation. What they chose, and what it produced, the sources either do not say or do not survive to say.

The dominant interpretation holds that they were consumed by the weight of the Second Era's final conflict. The minority interpretation — that the consumption was voluntary, that the Eymanarynn gave themselves as fuel for something that required exactly that kind of power — assigns to a mortal civilization a working of a scale that most scholars are not comfortable with. Neither interpretation changes the outcome. Eymanarann is frozen and empty. The people who built it are gone. The Known World exists because they are gone, in a way that the Known World has not, on the whole, examined carefully.

## **THE SACRIFICE**

The Second Era didn’t end with just a battle.

The Darkness could not be simply killed. To unmake what a god created requires a god to unmake it — and destruction of that kind would release what the Darkness carried into the world rather than remove it. The Abyss was the solution. Not a location in any geography, but a plane adjacent to reality, bounded differently, governed by neither Wysla's dissolution nor Ayla's order: the space where what cannot coexist with the mortal world can be held.

Ilya drove the Darkness into it. Then, because the door had to close from the inside, she followed.

The Third Era began in a world from which both the Darkness and the goddess of creation had been removed. The two moons that still rise full — Ayla's, steady in its cycle; Wysla's, smaller and irregular — are unbalanced without their counterpart. The shattered arc that crosses the sky on certain nights is what Ilianism calls Ilya's moon: broken when she descended, unrestored because she has not returned. The cost is written in the sky every night.

The Darkness did not die in the Abyss. Ilianism is explicit about this because death would have been the wrong outcome. The Darkness is imprisoned, and Ilya fights it there, and the war continues in a plane that mortal senses cannot perceive. It has continued since the end of the Second Era. It is continuing now.

The faith's promise to its followers is that a mortal who spends their life striving toward worthiness may, after death, pass through the threshold into that war and join it. Reincarnation is the path for those who die unready: another life, another chance, until they are. This is why Ilianist doctrine does not frame death as tragedy in the abstract. Death as failure — death before anything worthwhile was done — carries grief. Death after a life of genuine striving is the threshold. The two look similar from the outside. They feel entirely different from inside the faith.

## **MAGIC**

What magic is depends on who you ask, and the question is not merely academic — the answer has direct implications for how magic should be practiced and who has the standing to regulate it.

The Ilianist position, held also by the Old Faith and most religious institutions in the inner nations, is that Ilya wove magic into the fabric of creation. Her gift is present in the world whether she is or not. Mortals who work magic are working with something designed to be worked — a trust left for children the creator hoped would develop the wisdom to use it well. The Druidic Compact holds this most emphatically: the natural world is suffused with Ilya's creative force, and to attune to it is to attune to something intended. The fluctuations that the Magic Bloc's instruments have recorded for three centuries are, in this reading, instrumentation error or natural variation in something vast and organic. A river is not constant. Neither is a gift.

The scholars of the Magic Bloc are less certain. Their readings are not inconsistent. The fluctuations follow patterns — irregular, but patterns — that correlate with nothing in the natural world they have so far identified. Not seasons, not celestial cycles, not proximity to the Dead Lands, not the density of magical practice in any given region. Something else produces them. The Order of Magnus considers this their most pressing open question. The Old Faith considers it an instrument problem dressed in scholarship. Neither has changed the other's position.

What Elder Dragons know, they have not chosen to share. A dragon old enough to have watched three civilizations complete their arcs has had sufficient time to form an opinion on the question. Whether any of them has communicated that opinion to mortal scholars is a matter of record so sparse it amounts to absence.

## **THE SHAPE OF REALITY**

Three planes form the structure that scholars, theologians, and experienced mages broadly agree on — which means they agree on the map while disputing nearly everything about the territory.

**The Mortal World** is the plane of the Known World itself: physical, bounded by its own geography, governed by the interaction of Wysla's entropy and Ayla's order, operating without Ilya's direct presence since the Second Era ended. Magic exists here. Its precise nature is contested, as described above. What is not contested is that working it has consequences.

**The Abyss** is not adjacent in any spatial sense. The frame of "further in some direction" is wrong for it. It presses against the mortal world wherever the Second Era's violence was most concentrated — wherever the most blood of the most powerful creatures saturated the most scarred ground. The Dead Lands are the thinnest point. Eymanarann and Vysandárien sit above what remains of that contact. Every expedition into those frozen wastelands crosses ground close to the edge of something with no bottom. The obsidian extracted from the Dead Lands was forged in the bodies of Illyans who served the Darkness. The Known World is building its new economy on that material. The obvious conclusion has been reached by several people. None of them has put their name to it publicly.

**The Threshold** is the space between dying and what follows — neither fully mortal world nor fully the Abyss. Necromancy operates here. So do certain forms of divination that reach the knowledge of the dead. The Church of Tar'amaan denies the Threshold exists: the faithful pass directly into Tar'amaan's presence; heretics pass into nothing. Ilianism holds that the Threshold is where the dead are assessed for readiness — for the war Ilya is still fighting. Neither account can be confirmed by the living, which is part of why both have survived without resolution.

---

## **BEYOND THE MAP'S EDGE**

The Known World takes its name from a claim worth examining: these are the lands that are known. The implication is acknowledged, if rarely examined.

To the north and west of the Dead Lands, geography becomes speculation. The great winter pressing down from Eymanarann has no identified source or boundary. Expeditions that have gone looking for where the cold ends have not returned with an answer.

To the east, beyond the sea, the accounts grow stranger. The Val'arians arrived on the Known World's eastern shores roughly seven centuries ago — a maritime people, carrying navigational knowledge that local sailors studied for generations. They settled near the dwarves of Endoras and the peoples of Bal'mekh, founded communities, and eventually established the kingdom of Val'aria. Where they came from, their descendants answer selectively. Why they left, they do not answer at all.

The Faith of the Nine — the Val'arian tradition — describes creation as the work of nine divine beings who sang the world into being. These Nine are not the Three Sisters. They are something older and more material: the entities the Sisters shaped before mortals, given the task of preparing the world for life. Whether the Val'arians understand what they are preserving, or whether they carry a tradition whose full meaning was lost somewhere on the crossing, is not clear. What is clear is that Val'arians behave as people who consider this knowledge significant — not mythology, but something with weight — and who have decided it is not information they are sharing.

## **FOR THE GM: COSMOLOGY AT THE TABLE**

Cosmology chapters can become background material — context absorbed once and forgotten under the pressure of play. The Known World's cosmological structure is built to generate consequences, not merely atmosphere. What follows is guidance on keeping it active.

**Magic carries real stakes.** The character casting a spell is reaching into something that exists because two fundamental forces are fighting in a plane adjacent to this one. The setting does not require mechanically imposing that risk on every spell — that would make magic exhausting rather than consequential. What it requires is that the risk feel present in the atmosphere, and that it becomes relevant when magical practice crosses certain thresholds: large-scale research, rituals that reach toward the Threshold, anything that pushes at the boundary between the mortal world and the Abyss. When that moment arrives, the cost should feel earned, not arbitrary. The Order of Magnus exists because someone in the Third Era looked at unregulated magical practice and asked what happens if the wrong kind of resonance reaches through. They did not like their answer.

**The Dead Lands are readable, for those paying attention.** Adventurers who spend time in the ruins of Eymanarann will encounter things the setting does not explain to them: corridors too straight, rails without purpose, structures whose scale fits no function the party can name. Describe them accurately and let the players make what they will. Some will make nothing. Some will eventually assemble the truth, or part of it. Neither outcome requires management. What the GM should avoid is offering a tidy account of what these things were — no NPC in the Known World has one. The ruins are more useful as an open question than as a solved one.

**The Darkness is not absent.** The Inquisitors in occupied Norderenn burn magicians. The Dead Lands hold things that move when they shouldn't. The Ordo Dracul has cells in cities that consider themselves safe. None of these requires invoking the cosmological stakes directly. They are all downstream of the Second Era, and players who know the cosmology will feel that connection without being told to.

**The three moons are a working calendar.** Ayla's moon — steady, bright, reliable — marks the cycles that civilization runs on: seasons, planting and harvest, the tides. Wysla's moon, smaller and irregular, is associated with unpredictability. Sailors who trust only Ayla's moon and ignore Wysla's are the ones caught in unexpected weather. Ilya's shattered arc is not tracked for calendrical purposes. It does not follow a cycle that repeats reliably. Ilianist theology holds this is intentional: the war in the Abyss has no rhythm mortals can use, no phase, no schedule. What mages experience as magical fluctuation is the variation of that battle — hotter or colder, one force gaining briefly before the other responds. The Magic Bloc has catalogued these variations for three centuries without identifying their cause.

**The Threshold is accessible.** Necromancy in S\&D reaches the space between death and what follows. The dead in the Threshold retain memory and can be reached by specific means. The ethical weight of pulling a dead person's consciousness back for interrogation should be present at the table, not handled as a mechanical transaction. The mechanism exists. Whether to use it is the players' question, and it should feel like one.

**The Val'arians are an open question.** Where they came from and why they left is unknown — by design, and by the Val'arians' own preference. A GM who develops it has room to do so. A GM who leaves it permanently unanswered loses nothing. What matters is that Val'arian characters and NPCs behave as people who know the answer and have decided not to share it. That costs nothing to play and makes every Val'arian encounter more interesting.

**The Nine are present but distant.** The GM who wants to incorporate the Nine as active players has room to. The GM who treats them as an ancient background has an equally valid footing. They do not require resolution. What they require is that any NPC with knowledge of them treats it as significant — not trivia, not mythology, but information with weight.

## **WHAT NO MORTAL KNOWS**

*The following is not known to any living mortal in any nation, from any scholarly tradition. The Known World's best historians have fragments of it. None has assembled them correctly. This section is for the GM who needs to know what is actually true, not what the Known World believes.*

---

### **The First Era**

The cosmological record the Known World's scholars work from treats the First Era as essentially mythological — a time before mortal memory, accessible only through theological argument and fragmentary archaeology. What it was, in fact, is the simplest and hardest thing to accept: a world that worked.

Magic and the material world were in genuine balance. The Eymanarynn built at scale, and their scale was the scale of cities — not in any sense the Known World uses the word, but in the sense that the entire region now called the Dead Lands was, in the First Era, a single continuous inhabited place. Streets. Transit infrastructure. The machinery of a civilization that expected to continue. The Vysandri's engineering ran alongside this, on physical principles rather than magical ones, producing work the Third Era cannot replicate or fully understand. The elves, the dwarves, the early humans — each occupied a position in a larger order that had, across the long middle of the First Era, simply become coherent. The Illyans watched over magic because magic needed tending, and what it needed tending inside was worth it.

This is the context the Darkness acted from. An entity that had watched this world develop across geological time reached a conclusion about fragility. What existed was too good to survive on its own terms. What followed was not an attack on the First Era but an attempt to own it — a distinction that produced a war, and the war produced what the Dead Lands are now.

The Húldar are the only mortal people who carry this loss as a physical condition. Their curse — the slow dimming of their population, the grief that has no object they can name — is calibrated to something real. They are mourning a world they have no memory of. That world existed.

The Known World's scholars do not know any of this, and will not find it in any text the Chroniclers allow to survive. The Five know. They are not speaking.

---

### **The Five**

The Second Era did not end because Ilya descended into the Abyss. Ilya descended because the conditions were prepared for her to do so. The preparation required five entities, a plan that had been in development for longer than most civilizations had existed, and a series of costs that no single party could have paid alone.

They are called \- or would be, if their existence were known \- the Five. They have no collective name they use among themselves. Folk tradition calls them the Unnamed Beings, which is accurate enough to have persisted across three thousand years of oral culture without anyone being able to confirm or deny it. No mortal scholar has correctly identified what they are or what they did. This is not accidental.

One is a dragon — among the Illyans who remained loyal to Ilya's original purpose and survived the Second Era by doing what survivors do. Ancient enough to have known the Darkness before its breaking. What it remembers of the First Era is not scholarship but experience, and the distance between those two things is the distance between a map and the ground it describes.

One is a Vysandri — the only member of the Five who watched their own people become what the Vysandri became, and who helped make that happen. The giants of the Known World today are that entity's people, stripped of what made them dangerous and still alive. Whether that member carries this as grief, as settled necessity, or as something that has had three thousand years to become unrecognizable — that is not a question the GM needs to resolve. It should be felt whenever the Vysandri member appears.

One is the original Vrykola — the only member of the Five with no soul-tie above them, the entity from which every Vrykola lineage ultimately descends. Once, a Húldar druid who made the first betrayal and survived every consequence of it. What the transformation cost them in the context of the Second Era — whether it was already underway when the Five gathered, whether it was the price paid for participation in the plan — is not recorded anywhere, because the original Vrykola is the only source and has never spoken about it.

Two are unknown. The GM determines who or what they are. They should be entities of sufficient age and power to have acted at the moment when the Second Era ended — which means something that existed before or during that conflict, that had reason to want the Darkness contained, and that has persisted into the Third Era. Whatever the GM places in these slots will be correct, because correctness here is a function of coherence, not canon.

### **What They Did**

The plan had four components. Each required a cost paid by someone other than the Five.

The Eymanarynn were the fuel. The collective magical force of an entire civilization — not their army, not their mages specifically, but the accumulated power of a people who had spent generations attuning to Ilya's gift — was channeled into the working that gave Ilya what she needed to push the Darkness into the Abyss. Whether individual Eymanarynn understood what they were contributing to, whether consent was sought or assumed, or simply not considered, is not recorded anywhere. The Five know. They are not speaking.

The name erasure came next. The Five performed it using the Eymanarynn's power while it remained available — before the people who generated it ceased to exist. The dominant scholarly theory attributing the erasure to the Eymanarynn is not wrong about the source of the power, but wrong about who directed it.

The Vysandri were the second problem. They were the Darkness's most capable allies — not by faith but by utility, their engineering making them irreplaceable in any campaign of subjugation. Exterminating them would have produced martyrs and destroyed knowledge that existed nowhere else. The Five's solution was surgical: not death but removal. The Vysandri's understanding of the mechanical principles that built their civilization was taken from them and scattered. Where the knowledge went, whether it survived in any form, whether the Vysandri member of the Five retained any of it — the GM should leave this open. The people remained. The civilization did not.

Ilya's descent was last, the culmination of everything preceding it. The transaction, in its final form: Ilya went into the Abyss after the Darkness, the Eymanarynn's power sealed the threshold behind them, and the Five held what remained.

### **Why They Stay Silent**

The Five do not act openly in the Third Era. They do not present themselves as the world's protectors, offer their knowledge to scholars who need it, or intervene in conflicts they have the power to affect. The reason is the one the plan itself makes obvious: they know what it cost last time. An open intervention means the Known World learns who they are and what they did. The Eymanarynn question, examined honestly, does not produce gratitude. The Vysandri question, examined honestly, does not produce trust. The Five have survived three thousand years by remaining the things that nobody can quite confirm exist. That posture is not abandoned lightly, and not for anything that falls short of the only question that actually matters: whether the battle in the Abyss is at risk.

### **The Oldest Game**

The Five play a game that has no other name among the entities capable of knowing about it. Its rules are simple: keep the Known World intact, prevent any working that could destabilize the battle in the Abyss, and eliminate any attempt to recover Darkness's true name. Everything else — politics, war, the rise and fall of nations, the obsidian economy, the occupation of Norderenn, the Church of Tar'amaan's expansion — is noise, except when it isn't.

The game's moves take decades. Some take centuries. A Vrykola created by one of the Five to serve as an instrument — an entity who still thinks in human decades rather than geological time — represents an enormous investment and a specific need. When one of the Five deploys such a piece, something is in motion that matters on the scale the Five operate at.

A handful of Elder Dragons who survived from the First Era play adjacent to the game without being formally part of it. Their interests overlap the Five's on the central question — neither group wants the Abyss opened — and diverge on everything else. Their competition is the oldest in the Known World, predating every nation, every faith, and every political arrangement mortals consider permanent.

Players will spend their careers living inside decisions made before their civilizations existed. In most campaigns, they will not know this. If a GM wants that knowledge to become available — if investigation into the Vysandri ruins finds something, if the right sequence of events brings a party into contact with one of the Five — the architecture is here to support it.

The question is not whether the Five are real. The question is what a mortal does with that knowledge once they have it.

It’s up to you, the GM, to use it wisely.
