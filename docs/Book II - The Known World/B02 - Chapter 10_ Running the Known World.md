# Chapter 10: Running the Known World

### ***Tone, Genre, the Adventures the Setting Naturally Generates, and What Separates This World from Generic Fantasy at the Table***

---

This book built the scaffolding. The factions have goals and conflicts. The religions have genuine stakes. The geography creates pressure. The history left wounds that haven't closed. What this chapter addresses is how to run all of that at a table, in two- to four-hour sessions, with players who came to play and not to receive a lecture on geopolitical history.

The short version: most of it stays in the background, most of the time. The Known World's complexity is an atmosphere the players breathe rather than a syllabus they study. A party that has never read Chapter 2 still feels the occupation of Norderenn when a Bellarian contact speaks in careful half-sentences about the supply route he cannot officially be part of. A party that has never heard of the Emerald Corridor Alliance still understands what is at stake when the Endoran warehouse they were hired to protect turns out to contain grain stockpiles that three factions all consider theirs.

The setting does the work. The GM's job is to let it.

---

### **What Kind of Game Is This**

S\&D occupies a specific position, and knowing where that position is makes every GMing decision easier.

It is not grimdark. Grimdark argues that heroism is an illusion, that good intentions reliably produce bad outcomes, and that the only honest ambition is survival. S\&D does not argue this. Heroism exists in the Known World. It costs something real, it is not guaranteed to succeed, and it does not produce clean endings — but it exists, and the world is capable of noticing when it happens. The Vithurian Orchestra exists precisely because this world knows the difference between dying and mattering. If the GM runs the Known World as a place where nothing the players do ever makes a dent, that is a misreading of the setting.

The Church of Tar'amaan is not an abstraction of evil dressed in robes, but a recognizable institution — sincere in its beliefs, coherent in its theology, willing to burn people for both. The occupation of Norderenn looks like how an occupation looks. The erasure of the Kronn looks like cultural erasure looks. The setting does not soften these things with fantasy distancing language, and the GM should not either. When the Cadman administration renamed the Kronn quarter and put up new signs in their own script, the locals stopped using those signs. That detail is specific, and specific is the only kind of detail that makes the setting feel true.

In short, the target is stories where decent people operate in indecent conditions and occasionally, at real cost, manage to change something. 

Not every session produces that. Not every contract carries moral weight. But the possibility should always be present — the sense that the players are operating in a world where their choices connect to something larger than the immediate task, and where making the harder choice costs something real and means something real.

### **The Three Things the Setting Does Automatically**

Before any GM prep, the Known World's structure generates three things that most settings have to manufacture artificially.

**Factions with incompatible legitimate interests.** The Chroniclers have a defensible reason to control Dead Lands access — if obsidian fell into Ordo Draconis's hands, the consequences would be catastrophic. The Order of Magnus has a defensible reason to want independent research access — knowledge locked behind commercial monopoly is knowledge that serves power rather than understanding. Val Cadma has a defensible reason to resist magical proliferation — the Second Era exists as historical evidence of what uncontrolled magical power produces. None of these factions is wrong in their core concerns. They are wrong in specific applications of those concerns, wrong in the compromises they have made to pursue them, and sometimes simply wrong in ways they would not recognize as wrongness. This means any contract the party takes has a political context that generates complications organically. The GM does not need to manufacture faction tension — the conflict is already present in the structure.

**Morally complex NPCs at every level.** The Cadman Acolyte who files a report about a suspected magic-user believes they are protecting their community. The Bellarian noble who refuses to commit troops to the Norderenn resistance has calculated that Bellaria's survival requires not provoking Val Cadma further, and they are not obviously wrong. The resistance fighter who has been operating underground for five years has made accommodations that she is not comfortable examining too closely. These are not characters who need GM intervention to become interesting — the setting's political texture produces them automatically. The GM's job is to give them specific faces and specific concerns, not to generate the structural tension that makes them worth talking to.

**Adventures that matter beyond the party.** In most generic fantasy settings, the dungeon is there for the party. The dragon is there for the party. The stakes are localized. In the Known World, the obsidian deposit the party just discovered in an unlicensed Dead Lands expedition has implications for the Chroniclers' monopoly, for the Order of Magnus's research capacity, for Val Cadma's strategic calculus, and potentially for the Ordo Draconis's plans, none of which the party intended and all of which will find them regardless of whether they want the complication. The world pushes back. Deeds have consequences beyond the immediate. The GM does not need to engineer this — they need to let it happen.

### **What the Setting Generates as Adventure Fodder**

The Known World in 1,380 TE is at a specific historical moment: enough pressure has accumulated that the equilibrium is breaking, but the break has not happened yet. This is the right moment for adventurers. The jobs are real, the stakes are legible, and the people giving the orders have comprehensible reasons for what they want.

The natural adventures the setting produces fall into several categories that the GM can return to repeatedly because the structure that generates them is ongoing rather than resolved.

**Escort and transport in politically contested territory.** Moving people, goods, or information through a world where every major trade route crosses at least one faction's interest is a perpetual source of work. The Emerald Corridor is watched by Cadman agents. The Winter Bay's approach channels are controlled by the Crimson Company. The Dead Lands access routes through Norderenn require navigating both the occupation's checkpoints and the resistance's suspicion. These jobs are not simple — they involve decisions about what to declare, who to trust, what to do when the planned route becomes unavailable — and they connect the party to the larger political texture without requiring them to understand it fully in advance.

**Retrieval and investigation in the Dead Lands.** Every expedition into Eymanarann or Vysandárien is a geopolitical act, regardless of what the commission says it is. The party that brings back significant obsidian has something multiple organizations want. The party that finds evidence of a Cadman clandestine expedition has information that several parties would pay for and that at least one would kill to suppress. The ruins themselves contain things that predate the current political order and that the current political order is not fully equipped to understand, which means the party's discoveries can carry implications nobody anticipated when the contract was written.

**Faction work that creates future complications.** Taking a contract from the Chroniclers closes certain doors with the Order of Magnus and opens questions with the Crimson Company. Taking work from the College of Whispers means operating in an information environment that the College partially controls. Every faction relationship the party builds is a future constraint on their freedom of action, which is not a problem, but the mechanism by which the party becomes embedded in the world. Embedded parties face more interesting decisions than neutral ones.

**Political and social work in cities.** The Known World's cities — covered in Chapter 11 — are environments with specific internal tensions that generate work independent of the wider geopolitical situation. A negotiation between guilds that has stalled, an inheritance dispute that three factions are trying to influence, an Inquisition investigation that has identified the wrong person and is accelerating toward a conclusion that will destabilize the whole quarter — these are local problems with local stakes that nevertheless reflect the setting's larger themes. They are also the kind of work that lets the party interact with NPCs who are not mercenaries, soldiers, or scholars, and that grounds the campaign in the texture of daily life rather than always operating at the level of world-historical consequence.

**Resistance and occupation work in Norderenn.** The occupied territory generates a specific category of adventure that draws from the setting's darkest material: the logistics of resistance, the consequences of collaboration, the specific difficulty of operating in a place where the legal system is an instrument of the occupier. This is the kind of campaign material that requires the GM to run honestly — resistance is not universally heroic, occupation produces genuine loyalties on all sides, and the party's moral clarity about what is right does not translate automatically into the capacity to achieve it. Run this material with the seriousness it deserves, and it produces some of the most memorable sessions the setting can generate.

### **Running Political Complexity Without Losing the Room**

The Known World's three-bloc structure — the Cadman theocratic empire, the Magic Bloc, and the Emerald Corridor Alliance — is genuinely complex. Acknowledging this is the first step to managing it.

The GM does not need players to understand the full structure for the setting to function. What players need is enough context to understand the specific decision they are facing right now. The information that matters is the information relevant to the current contract, the current city, and the current NPC asking them to do something. The full geopolitical picture assembles itself gradually over the course of a campaign, through accumulated experience rather than a session-zero briefing.

Practically, this means the GM manages political information through NPCs rather than exposition. The Endoran guild leader, who is nervous about the Cadman agent she thinks is watching her offices, communicates everything the party needs to know about Cadman economic pressure on Endoras without the GM having to describe the strategic situation in abstract. The Norderenn resistance fighter, who will not name his contacts even to allies he trusts, communicates everything the party needs to know about the occupation's intelligence methods without a history lesson.

Three principles help manage complexity at the table:

**Give every faction a face before they give it a policy.** The Chroniclers are not an institution, but the Chronicler expedition leader who is meticulously fair about contract terms and absolutely inflexible about confiscation rights, who has been doing this work for twenty years and has opinions about every Dead Lands site the party might visit. Val Cadma is not an empire, but the Cadman checkpoint commander who is doing his job correctly, who is not cruel but is thorough, and who has a family in the capital who would be endangered if he were reported for lenience. Factions become comprehensible through the people who embody them.

**Let players ask the wrong questions sometimes.** A party that has misread a faction's motivations will make choices that produce unexpected consequences, and unexpected consequences are the setting's primary mechanism for teaching players about the world without the GM having to explain it. The party that assumed the Chroniclers were purely corrupt will be surprised when the Chronicler expedition leader returns an artifact to a Vartran community because its cultural provenance outweighs its commercial value. That surprise is more educational than any briefing.

**Not every political situation resolves.** The Norderenn occupation does not end because the party completed a mission there. The Emerald Corridor Alliance's internal tensions do not dissolve because the party brokered a single successful negotiation. The Known World's problems are structural and ongoing, and the party's interventions make specific differences without resolving the underlying conditions. This is honest to the setting and honest to the scale of what adventurers can actually do. It is also, paradoxically, what makes individual deeds matter — they are specific achievements in a world that does not give easy wins, which means they mean something.

### **Dark Themes at the Table**

The setting depicts religious persecution, military occupation, cultural erasure, resource exploitation, and the specific kind of institutional violence that comes from sincere people doing terrible things for coherent reasons. Handling these themes correctly requires three distinctions.

**Depicting is not endorsing.** The Tar'amaanist Acolyte who files a report on a suspected magic-user is not a character the GM is holding up as admirable. They are a character the GM is presenting as real, as someone shaped by a specific institution into a specific kind of person, doing what that institution trained them to do. The horror is in the sincerity, not in cartoonish villainy. The GM who plays Tar'amaan figures as sincere believers produces more genuinely disturbing antagonists than the GM who plays them as knowing evildoers, because the former is recognizable in a way the latter is not.

**Specific detail over general atmosphere.** "The occupied city felt oppressive" is nothing. "The Kronn quarter has been renamed — the new signs are in Cadman script, and locals have stopped using them" is something. The setting's dark themes land hardest when they are specific and physical, when they manifest in details that are mundane and therefore more disturbing than spectacular violence would be. The GM should reach for the specific detail rather than the general mood.

**The three questions before a dark scene.** The Style Guide establishes these, and they are worth repeating in operational form: Does this make the world feel more true? Does this create a decision point or consequence that matters? Would cutting it make the writing — or the session — dishonest about what this world is? If the answer to all three is yes, run it. If the answer to any is no, cut or reframe. The distinction is between darkness that serves the themes and darkness that simply makes things worse without purpose. Those are different things, and the difference is usually legible if the GM asks the question honestly.

### **The Known World Is Not Waiting for the Party**

This is the most important practical principle for running the setting, and the one most likely to be lost under the weight of session prep.

The Known World's problems were in motion before the party arrived and will continue if the party does nothing. Val Cadma's strategic encirclement is on a timeline. The Emerald Corridor Alliance's internal tensions have a window before they produce a crisis. Bal'mekh is running out of options. These situations do not pause while the party is doing something else.

This does not mean the GM runs a countdown clock that punishes the party for taking a different job. It means that when the party returns to a city they visited six sessions ago, something has changed — a faction's position has shifted, an NPC they spoke with is no longer where they left them, the situation they walked away from has developed in their absence. The world has the texture of ongoing events rather than a static backdrop.

The practical method: at the end of each session, the GM asks what happened in the world that the party was not part of. Pick one or two things — small developments, not world-historical events — and decide what changed while the party was elsewhere. Most of these will never come up directly. The ones that do, when they surface through an NPC's changed manner or a political shift in a city the party arrives in, produce the sensation that the world is real rather than arranged for their benefit.

This also applies to faction NPCs. The Chronicler expedition leader the party worked with six months ago has continued working. She has made decisions since then, some of which were influenced by what the party did, some of which were not. She is not waiting in a holding pattern to be relevant again. When the party encounters her again, she is someone who has had six months of experience since they last spoke, and that experience should be legible.

### **What Separates This World from Generic Fantasy**

Three things, specifically.

**The absence of clean moral sorting.** Generic fantasy has good factions and evil factions. The Known World has factions with comprehensible goals, some of which are achievable only through methods that produce genuine harm to people who did nothing wrong. The Chroniclers' monopoly serves purposes that include genuine preservation of knowledge and results in the systematic denial of access to knowledge that other parties might use responsibly. Both things are true simultaneously. The party that wants a simple read on who to work for and who to work against will have to make peace with not getting one.

**The cost of magic as a lived reality.** Magic in the Known World is not a neutral tool. It draws on Ilya's gift, worked without Ilya's presence, in a world where the last time that gift was worked at full scale, it produced the Second Era. The Church of Tar'amaan's position on magical practice is theologically wrong and produces genuine atrocities, and is also not entirely disconnected from a real historical reality: the Illyans existed, the Second Era happened, and the Darkness is still imprisoned rather than dead, which means the risk it represented is still present in a form that magic can access. A campaign that takes the cosmological stakes seriously — where the magic-using characters in the party understand what they are reaching into when they cast spells — feels different from a campaign where magic is a neutral power source with no history attached.

**Reputation as something earned in a world that remembers.** The Vithurian Orchestra gives the Known World a mechanism for making character matter beyond the immediate. What the party does accrues — not as experience points, but as a record in the minds of NPCs who have heard things, in the files of factions that track who is worth watching, and potentially in the attention of an institution that has been documenting exceptional lives for four centuries. A party that operates with integrity in a world that notices integrity will find doors opening in proportion to that integrity, not because the game is rewarding correct behavior, but because that is how reputation works in a world with functioning social memory.

### **A Note on Open Space**

This book built the scaffolding. The factions have goals, the religions have stakes, the geography creates pressure, and the history left wounds. What this book does not do — deliberately — is fill every gap.

The Val'arians' homeland is unknown. The Ordo Draconis's central leadership is unspecified. The Tower of Nyrün's actual research agenda has not been revealed. The disappeared Observers in Norderenn have no canonical explanation. The city of Vithura may or may not have existed in the form that the most popular legend claims.

These are not oversights. They are space. Every table that runs S\&D has its own history, its own players, its own sense of what the setting means to them. The gaps are where that history goes. A GM who decides that the Val'arians came from a civilization that preceded the First Era and has been carrying that knowledge for centuries is not contradicting anything in this book — they are filling open space with their own answer, which is the right answer for their table.

The setting works because it is specific enough to feel real and open enough to feel like yours. Protect both qualities. Add specifics that serve your campaign. Leave open the spaces your players haven't reached yet. And when your players do something the setting didn't anticipate — when they find a third option in a situation designed to have two, when they forge an alliance that the faction chapter didn't envision, when they arrive at an answer to one of the open questions that is better than any answer this book could have given — let it stand. That is the game working correctly.
