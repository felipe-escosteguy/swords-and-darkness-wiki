# Chapter 9: Running Reputation

### ***When to Move the Orchestra from Background to Foreground, How to Make It Feel Earned, and How to Run the Premiere***

---

The reputation mechanics that determine how the world tracks and responds to a company's growing fame are covered in Book I, Chapter 17\. This chapter is not about those mechanics, but about the specific judgment calls that sit above them — the GM decisions that determine when the Orchestra moves from cultural texture to active presence in a campaign, how to run that transition without it feeling arbitrary or purchased, and what to do with the premiere if it arrives.

The short version, before the longer one: the Orchestra should feel inevitable in retrospect and uncertain throughout. If players look back at the end of a campaign and think "of course the Observers were watching — look at what we did," the GM has run it correctly. If they think "we did the right things at the right moments and the opera arrived on schedule," the GM has run a points system dressed in fictional clothes, and the wrong thing has been produced.

---

### **When to Introduce the Orchestra**

The Orchestra enters a campaign in stages. Those stages are not tied to session count or character level. They are tied to what has actually happened at the table.

**Stage One** is cultural texture. The Orchestra exists before the party has any relationship with it. A bard sings an aria in a tavern. A poster announces a premiere in a city the party is not visiting. An NPC wears the lyre medal and does not explain it unless pressed. This stage can last the entire campaign if the party never does anything that warrants moving further. It costs nothing to maintain and it builds the world correctly: the Orchestra is a real institution that predates these specific characters and will outlast them.

There is no minimum level or reputation tier for Stage One. It begins in the first session and never entirely ends.

**Stage Two** is unconfirmed observation. An Observer is present at events the party participates in, but their presence is not confirmed. A bard asks unusually specific questions about the decision the party made at the crossroads — not what they did, but why. A quiet merchant appears in the same three towns the party passes through over the course of a month. Someone in the back of the room during the negotiation seems more interested in the process than the outcome.

Move to Stage Two when the party has done something that meets the Orchestra's standard: a genuine choice under genuine pressure, where the easier option was visible and declined, with stakes that extended beyond the immediate. This is not a mechanical threshold, but an editorial judgment, and the standard is the same one described in Chapter 7 — moral complexity, extended consequence, deeds that reveal character rather than merely demonstrating capability.

If the GM is uncertain whether a deed qualifies, the question to ask is: would this make a good opera? Not a spectacle — an opera. Something with weight, with character visible under pressure, with a question the audience would leave the theater still arguing about. If yes, move to Stage Two.

**Stage Three** is partial revelation. An Observer confirms, or nearly confirms, what they are. This is the moment of highest uncertainty for the party: they know they are being watched, they do not know what has been assessed, and they understand that awareness of being watched changes what is being watched. The Observer should not make promises. They should not imply the commission is coming. They communicate only that the Orchestra has found the situation interesting and is continuing to pay attention.

Move to Stage Three when an extended period of Stage Two observation has accumulated enough material that a Composer, internally, would be requesting additional evidence rather than closing the thread. The party has not done one notable thing — they have done several things, across time, that collectively suggest a pattern of character rather than isolated competence.

**Stage Four** is the invitation. It arrives when the Composers have concluded their debate and the commission is approved. The mechanics of what follows — how fame changes NPC interactions, what the reputation tiers mean in practice — are in Book I Chapter 17\. What this chapter addresses is how to run the premiere itself.

---

### **Making It Feel Earned**

The single largest risk in running the Orchestra is the party optimizing for it. Once players understand there is an in-world institution that rewards exceptional moral complexity, some of them will begin producing it as a performance rather than living it as characters. This produces technically correct behavior and emotionally hollow sessions.

Several practical approaches reduce this risk.

**Keep Stage Two ambiguous for as long as possible.** The longer the party is uncertain whether they are being watched, the longer they are behaving according to their actual values rather than a performance of them. An Observer who is definitively identified ends the ambiguity. One who is suspected but not confirmed keeps it open. Resist the impulse to confirm suspicions prematurely, even when players ask directly. "I don't know what you mean" from a suspected Observer is more useful than either yes or no.

**Let the Orchestra go quiet after exceptional moments.** If the party does something genuinely impressive and nothing immediately signals Orchestra interest, some players will conclude they missed the mark and return to playing naturally. Others will conclude the Orchestra noticed and is being discreet. Both conclusions produce better play than certainty. The absence of a signal is not the same as the absence of attention.

**The Observer's questions reveal values.** When an Observer makes contact and asks questions — about why a decision was made, about what the party understood the stakes to be, about what they would do differently — the answers reveal character. Players who have been performing for an imagined audience answer differently than players who have been playing honestly. Pay attention to that difference. It tells you something about what is actually being witnessed.

**Make the costs visible.** The deeds the Orchestra finds interesting are not costless. A choice that reveals character under pressure costs something — a relationship strained, a resource spent, a compromise that cannot be unmade. If the party is consistently making hard choices with no apparent cost, either the stakes are not real or the GM needs to make the consequences more present. The Orchestra is not interested in characters who face difficulty elegantly, but in characters for whom difficulty is actually difficult.

### **Minimum Level Guidance**

The Orchestra does not observe by character level. It observes by deed. A first-level party that makes a genuinely consequential moral choice under real pressure is more interesting to the Orchestra than a tenth-level party that defeats a powerful enemy with no complexity attached.

That said, practical experience at the table suggests some loose guidance on timing.

Parties in the early stages of their careers — levels one through four — are establishing themselves. The deeds that attract Stage Two observation typically require enough accumulated context to be understood as choices rather than accidents. A first-level party that stumbles into a morally complex situation and survives it has demonstrated luck and perhaps instinct. The Orchestra is waiting to see whether the next choice shows the same character, and the one after that. Early-career observation is real but rarely advances quickly.

By levels five through eight, a party that has been making consistent choices has accumulated the pattern of character the Orchestra is actually evaluating. This is the range where Stage Three revelations most often occur naturally — not because the game's mechanics have changed, but because enough time has passed for the Observatory to have built a case and for the party's choices to have had visible consequences in the world.

By levels nine and above, a party has been operating in the world long enough that their deeds have begun to have a reputation independent of Orchestra attention. The commission, if it is coming, is likely already in the Composers' debate. If it has not come by this point in a campaign — if the party has reached name level and the Orchestra has not emerged from Stage One — that is itself information. It may mean the thread was closed for political reasons. It may mean the party's deeds, however impressive, have not contained the specific quality the Orchestra is looking for. It may mean the GM has not yet moved the thread forward and should consider whether this campaign warrants doing so.

Not every campaign does. Not every company gets a song. This is not failure.

### **Running the Premiere**

If the commission is approved and the invitation arrives, the premiere is the emotional climax of an extended narrative arc. It deserves space, preparation, and the respect of not being rushed.

**The invitation.** High-quality parchment, silver wax seal, the lyre-and-lyre mark. A date, a city, a theater, formal attire requested. No explanation of what the opera is about. Most parties assume it is about someone else until the curtain opens. That assumption is worth preserving for as long as possible — let them arrive expecting someone else's story. The moment of recognition, when the soprano's opening aria describes the pass in the mountains or the negotiation in the merchant's back room or the night the choice was made that cost something real, lands differently when they did not see it coming.

**What to show them.** The GM does not need to stage the entire opera. Two or three scenes, described rather than performed, are sufficient and usually more effective than a comprehensive retelling. Choose scenes the party would least expect — not the dramatic action sequences but the quiet moments that revealed character, the decisions that seemed small at the time and turned out not to be. The opera's perspective is external: this is how those moments looked to people who were not inside them. That external vantage is part of what makes the premiere significant. The party has been living these events from the inside. For the first time, they see them from the outside.

**The opera is an interpretation, not a transcript.** The Composers have made artistic choices. Some details have been adjusted for dramatic clarity. Some moments have been given weight the party may not have felt at the time. Some choices that felt pragmatic in the moment are framed, through music and staging, as something more deliberate. The party may agree with the Composers' interpretation. They may find it uncomfortably accurate. They may find it slightly wrong in ways they cannot quite articulate. All of these reactions are correct. The opera is a story about them, not a recording of them, and the distance between those two things is as interesting as the GM chooses to make it.

**The aftermath.** Book I Chapter 17 covers the mechanical consequences of a commissioned work: how fame affects NPC interactions, what the Vithurian Endowment delivers, how the reputation tiers shift in response to widespread recognition. What the GM needs to manage beyond the mechanics is the texture of having become a story.

People recognize them. People have opinions about them based on the opera, which is not the same as opinions based on their actual choices. People who have never met them feel they know them. Enemies who want to disprove the story exist. Young adventurers who want to become them exist. The weight of expectation — that future choices will be measured against the choices the opera immortalized — is present now in a way it was not before.

A useful beat to run in the premiere's immediate aftermath: return the party to a place they have been before. A tavern from an early session, a city they passed through without incident, a road they traveled when they were no one in particular. Show them how the same place responds to them now. The contrast between who they were when they first walked in and what they are now is the opera's real consequence — not the fame, but the permanence. They have been seen. The seeing cannot be undone.

**If the commission was close but did not arrive.** Campaigns that advance through Stages Two and Three but do not reach Stage Four deserve honest handling. A thread that was built and then closed — for political reasons, because the Composers' debate went the other way, because something about the investigation was compromised — is its own kind of story. The party may or may not discover this. If they do, the question of why the song was not written can extend a campaign or seed a new one. If they do not, their lives continue without the premiere and without knowing it was once a possibility. This is the life most people in the Known World live. It is not a lesser story for that.

### **A Final Note on What the Orchestra Is For**

The Orchestra exists in the game to answer the question the game is built around: what does it mean to matter?

The Known World is full of people who do things and die and are forgotten. Most of the people in every city the party passes through will never be remembered beyond the circle of those who knew them personally. This is not tragedy, but the scale at which most lives operate. The party is different only in that they have chosen to operate at a scale where what they do has consequences beyond the immediate. Whether those consequences accumulate into something the Orchestra would call worthy is genuinely uncertain, genuinely contested, and genuinely not guaranteed.

This uncertainty is the point. A game that promises the opera if players do the right things has replaced one kind of reward with another. A game that keeps the outcome genuinely open — where the party may do everything right and still not get the song, or may stumble into something extraordinary and find themselves the subjects of a commission they never sought — is a game that takes the question seriously.

The GM's job is not to administer a reward, but to build a world in which the reward is possible, and then to be honest about whether the party has earned it. That honesty, applied consistently and without sentimentality, is what makes the premiere — when it comes — mean something.
