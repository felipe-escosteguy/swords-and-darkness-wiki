# Chapter 6: Religion & the Divine

### ***The Full Theological Landscape — What the Faiths Demand, What They Produce, and Where They Conflict***

---

Religion in the Known World is not abstract. It tells you what days you cannot fight on, what currency will be refused in which markets, which inns will turn you away at the door, and which charges can get you burned. The theological differences between Ilianism and Tar'amaanism are not academic disputes — they are the framework inside which the occupation of Norderenn was authorized, the obsidian economy is contested, and the war that is coming has been justified in advance.

This chapter covers the Known World's major faiths: what each believes, what it demands of its followers, who holds institutional authority within it, and what the tensions inside each tradition look like when they surface at the table. The druidic tradition receives separate treatment at the end, because it is not a faith in the same institutional sense and the distinction matters mechanically as well as theologically.

One note before the entries: this chapter describes faiths as they are practiced by their followers, not as they are assessed from outside. Ilianism is described through Ilianist eyes. The Church of Tar'amaan is described through Tar'amaanist eyes. The chapter's job is to make each tradition comprehensible on its own terms. The GM's job — and the setting's position — on what those traditions actually produce in the world is addressed in the GM section at the end.

---

### **The Old Faith — Ilianism**

*"Three is the number that holds. Two fight. One falls. The world continues."*

The Old Faith is the oldest continuous religious tradition in the Known World, though "continuous" requires qualification — nothing survived the Second Era intact, and Ilianism's pre-Darkness forms are gone along with everything else from that period. What exists now reconstructed itself in the first centuries of the Third Era from oral tradition, from fragments preserved by elvish communities who had maintained the practice underground during the Age of Dragons, and from the theological arguments of scholars who had strong opinions and incomplete sources. The result is a living tradition with genuine depth and genuine internal disagreement about which parts of it are original and which are reconstruction.

**What it believes:** The Three Sisters — Ilya, Wysla, Ayla — created the world through simultaneous, interdependent action. None of the three is more fundamental than the others, and none of the three is good or evil in the conventional sense. Wysla's dissolution is not malice; it is the force that prevents stasis. Ayla's order is not tyranny; it is the force that prevents collapse. Ilya's creation is not inherently benign; things that did not exist before can be harmful. The system requires all three, which is the theological grounding for the Known World's alignment framework: extreme positions in any direction pull against the balance the Sisters built into reality.

Ilya descended into the Abyss to imprison the Darkness, and she did not come back. Ilianism does not frame this as defeat. It frames it as the war continuing in a different theater, and it frames mortal life as preparation for joining that war. A follower who spends their life striving toward genuine worth — toward mastery, toward honest action, toward doing the thing that costs something when the easier option was available — dies ready. Ready means joining Ilya in the Abyss, which Ilianism frames as the highest possible honor rather than a pleasant afterlife. The alternative is reincarnation: another life, another chance to become someone who is ready.

This theology has a specific practical implication that the Church of Tar'amaan finds threatening: it has no permanent clergy in the conventional sense, no institutional hierarchy above the regional level, and no central authority that can be pressured, converted, or co-opted. The Oracles are chosen by demonstrated gift — the ability to read the patterns of the natural world that Ilianism treats as the Sisters' ongoing communication — not by appointment. A faith with no Pope cannot be decapitated.

**Who holds authority:** The Oracles are the closest thing to clerical leadership Ilianism has. They are typically women, though not exclusively, chosen through a combination of apparent gift and the Ardents' assessment of character. They interpret signs, counsel their communities, officiate at major rites, and carry authority that is spiritual rather than temporal — an Oracle cannot order someone to do something, but an Oracle's opinion on whether something is aligned with the Sisters' balance carries enormous weight in communities where the faith runs deep. The Ardents serve the Oracles, manage the daily religious life of a settlement, and constitute the pool from which future Oracles may emerge.

There is no Grand Oracle, no synod, no council that speaks for Ilianism as a whole. Regional traditions vary. The Ilianism practiced in Solara emphasizes the civic dimension — the three-fold balance as a model for governance, the idea that a healthy state needs institutions that embody each Sister's function. The Ilianism practiced in Alvengaard is more explicitly nature-oriented, closer to the druidic tradition in its emphasis on seasonal cycles and physical environment. The version practiced underground by the Norderenn resistance is stripped to essentials: the Sisters are real, balance matters, the Darkness is still imprisoned, and dying in resistance to the occupation is not the worst death available.

**What it demands:** Seasonal observance is the most universally maintained practice. The first week of both spring and autumn are sacred — no open conflict, no executions, no declared hostilities. In nations where Ilianism is the dominant faith, this is enforced with varying degrees of rigor. Solara treats the prohibition seriously; a trade dispute that turns violent during the sacred week carries legal consequences that secular disputes in other weeks do not. Other nations observe the seasons with less official weight but more personal meaning. The Cadman occupation has abolished the seasonal observances in Norderenn as a formal matter, which is one of the occupation's less strategically significant decisions and one of its most personally felt ones.

Animal sacrifice as communication with the Sisters is practiced in more traditional communities, largely absent in urban centers where the practice is considered archaic. Marriage in the Ilianist tradition is sacred and permanent — not because the faith enforces it by punishment, but because the ceremony is understood as a soul-level joining that does not dissolve. The faith has no interest in the gender of the parties. Remarriage after a spouse's death is not prohibited but is understood as a significant threshold: in the traditional reading, the surviving spouse carries the bond into their next life, which means they are not starting fresh. Most people who follow the faith make their own determination about what this means in practice.

**Internal tensions:** The reconstructed nature of post-Second Era Ilianism means there are persistent arguments about which practices are original and which were developed to fill gaps. The Alvengaard tradition's emphasis on druidic connection has produced ongoing friction with Solaran urban Ilianism over whether the Druidic Order is a branch of Ilianist practice or a parallel tradition that shares cosmological assumptions without being the same thing. Neither side has resolved this, which means the question surfaces every time a community has both an Oracle and a druid and they disagree about something.

The deeper tension is eschatological: what happens if Ilya wins? The Darkness imprisoned, Ilya free, the war concluded — Ilianism has never fully resolved what the faith becomes in that scenario. Some theologians argue the reincarnation cycle would end: every soul that had been preparing would finally be ready, and there would be no more need for the living world as a preparation ground. This implication makes certain Ilianist thinkers uncomfortable enough that they have begun questioning whether the war in the Abyss is actually meant to end, or whether eternal contest is the point. The argument is conducted in scholarly texts and has not yet reached the level of schism, but it is the kind of argument that could.

**On Weapons.** Ilianist clergy carry what their communities already use. The staff travels. The hammer drives posts and settles disputes. The flail threshes grain before it becomes a weapon. The one addition the Ardents have maintained as a matter of quiet practicality is the sling — a shepherd's tool, present in every rural settlement, requiring no explanation at a checkpoint. In Norderenn, under occupation, an Ardent who needs to defend their congregation and reach the next village without being detained has concluded the sling is the weapon the Sisters equipped rural communities with long before anyone thought to call it religious doctrine.

### **The Church of Tar'amaan**

*"And Tar'amaan rose from the ashes of the Great War, and the Darkness itself trembled and fled this plane of existence."* — The Solar Scrolls

**A note before the theology:** The Church of Tar'amaan, as it exists in 1,380 TE, is a supremacist institution with a fascist conquest agenda. It preaches the inherent superiority of Tar'amaan's faithful over every other people on the planet, classifies non-human ancestries as servants of the Darkness who may be eliminated without sin, and is currently operating the largest military campaign in the Known World's Third Era history in service of that theology. No player should portray a follower of the Sun God. This is not a restriction based on mechanical balance, but on what the Church is: the campaign's primary antagonist, an NPC-only faction, and an institution whose beliefs and actions this game does not endorse as a playable position.

The one exception is addressed at the end of this entry.

**What it believes:** The Followers of the Sun — as they call themselves — hold that Tar'amaan is the one true god, embodied in the sun itself, the force that brought light and warmth through the Second Era when Ilya failed, and the Illyans reigned. The Three Sisters are not dismissed but declared dead: they perished in the war against the Darkness, their defeat represented every night by the three moons — cold, diminished, circling borrowed light. Ilya's shattered arc in the sky is not a monument to sacrifice. It is, in the Tar'amaanist reading, proof of failure.

From this foundation, the theology constructs its expansion doctrine cleanly: Tar'amaan's eternal sun must conquer every representation of darkness in the mortal world. The moons are already subordinated. The non-human peoples — elves, dwarves, Ûrgons, Badakis, Vartrans — are classified in the Solar Scrolls as servants of the Darkness that the Illyans embodied, created to serve corrupted purposes and spiritually incapable of receiving Tar'amaan's grace. The Scrolls' teaching is unambiguous: eliminating them is not a crime, but participation in the sun's eternal victory.

Arcane magic is heresy on the same grounds. The Church holds that magical practice draws on the same power the Darkness drew on, and that tolerating magic-users in a community is structurally equivalent to reopening the door to the Abyss. This position is sincere, theologically coherent within the Tar'amaanist framework, and the justification under which the Inquisition operates.

**Who holds authority:** The High Pontiff is the Church's supreme spiritual authority, guarding the golden Solar Scrolls in the fortress of Calebhas in Val Cadma. The Pontiff rarely appears in public. Their authority is absolute in doctrine.

Below the Pontiff, three Archbishops execute institutional will across different strategic fronts. Archbishop Kranzler is the most powerful of them — not because his position outranks the others, but because he commands the Church's military forces, which constitute the majority of Val Cadma's operational army. The Empress Evelyn Poldark nominally leads the Cadman state. In practice, Kranzler controls the instrument through which state power is applied, which means the Empress's authority extends precisely as far as Kranzler finds it useful. He has not yet needed to make this explicit. He has not needed to because everyone in Val Cadma's power structure has worked out the calculation independently.

Kranzler uses war as the Church's primary expansion mechanism because war is what the Church can authorize that the crown cannot easily refuse. Every military campaign he directs is simultaneously a military campaign and a theological one — the expansion of Tar'amaan's dominion, the purification of territory from heretical practice, and the consolidation of Church authority in newly administered regions. He is not cynical about this. He believes the work is necessary. He believes he will be remembered as the man who prepared the world for Tar'amaan's eternal sun.

The second Archbishop administers the occupation of Norderenn. The third supervises intelligence operations along the frontier with Endoras. Both report to Kranzler more than to the Pontiff, which is an institutional arrangement the Pontiff has either sanctioned or cannot reverse.

Below the Archbishops: Solar Lords supervise regions, High Regents administer individual cities, and Acolytes handle confession, morning prayer, and the daily enforcement of doctrine at the community level.

**What it demands:** Monthly confession is mandatory, conducted with the local High Regent. The confession mechanism collects both spiritual accountability and political intelligence — a community's confessions tell the High Regent who is compliant, who is uncertain, and who is lying. Lying in confession, or being discovered to have omitted material information, initiates the Rites of Purification: physical punishment escalating to torture, with condemnation to slavery or death depending on what was concealed.

Morning prayer facing the sun is daily and public. Missing it is noticed. The social enforcement mechanism does not require Inquisitors to function — neighbors watch, neighbors report, and the calculus of whether reporting a neighbor produces better outcomes than silence is one that every person living under the Church's administration has run through and arrived at their own answer to.

Underground dwelling and mine labor are classified as spiritual degradation because they remove the practitioner from the sun's presence. This is why occupied Norderenn's mines are staffed by convicted heretics and slaves. High Regents and Solar Lords extend the list of punishable offenses regularly, citing "newly recovered lost gospels" as authority — a mechanism that allows systematic persecution of any group the local administrator finds convenient to target, with theological cover pre-attached.

The Tar'amaanist doctrine on non-human peoples is not a subtext, but explicit teaching: they are vile servants of the Darkness, their elimination is not sinful, and bringing the eternal sun to the world requires their subjugation or removal. The Church's practitioners in occupied Norderenn apply this doctrine to Ilianist human populations as heretics; in Bal'mekh, when the campaign reaches it, they will apply it to Vartrans as a matter of species.

**The Hidden Sun:** There are people inside the Church who know something is wrong. Not enough of them, and most of them not brave enough to say so clearly, but they exist — Acolytes who find the Rites of Purification difficult to witness, Solar Lords who administer their regions with less aggression than Kranzler expects, High Regents who quietly misfile confessions that would have produced Inquisitor visits. The theology they were raised in is the only one they have. What they have concluded is that the institution applying it has become something its god — if Tar'amaan is real — would not recognize.

This is the one playable position available to someone who wants to engage with Tar'amaanism from inside: not a follower of the Church as it operates, but a person formed by its theology who has reached the point of quiet opposition. They confess and pray publicly because the alternative is death or slavery. They help people that the Church would burn, because the alternative is becoming part of the apparatus. They cannot speak their position openly. They are playing a specific, dangerous role in a specific, dangerous institution, and the campaign they are in had better be prepared to take that seriously, because the Church's internal intelligence network was designed to find exactly them.

**On Weapons.** The Solar Scrolls are unambiguous on this point: the Church does not subdue. The blunt weapon — the mace, the hammer, the club — was designed to leave the body breathing. Tar'amaan's will is not that heretics and servants of the Darkness should be subdued, but that they should be purified. Spears and polearms are the weapons of advance — they hold ground, deny approach, and drive forward into whatever stands in the sun's path. The sword of judgment belongs at the Inquisitor's hip because judgment requires a blade. The shepherd's crook and the mercy-hammer belong to faiths that have not yet understood what the sun is actually doing.

### **The Faith of the Nine**

*"And the Nine sang, and the world was made by their sacred chorus."*

The Faith of the Nine is Val'arian, which means it is, like the Val'arianos themselves, known to the rest of the Known World primarily through what has been observed from outside. The faith's practitioners are selective about what they share and with whom.

**What it believes:** Nine divine beings — the Sacred Chorus — sang the world into existence under the leadership of Alderaan, Lord of Light. The other eight — Maldurk of War, Neeva of the Dead, Dag'neth of Seas, Selynne of Storms, Binnin of Fire and Art, Gwinnifer of Forests, Val'eryn of Winds, Jaros of Cunning and Shadow — are not subordinates. They are the full ensemble, each necessary, each individually flawed in ways the faith treats as instructive rather than disqualifying. Jaros, the twin and eternal adversary of Alderaan, is not evil — he is the force of testing, of cunning, of the kind of cleverness that finds weaknesses in plans. A Val'arian sailor who prays for safe passage prays to Dag'neth and Selynne to be at peace with each other, then to Val'eryn for favorable winds, covering the practical concerns in prayer in the order that the practical concerns present themselves.

The Nine are not perfect. This is central to the faith rather than an awkward qualification. A divine council that includes jealousy, pride, and trickery is a divine council that looks comprehensible to mortals, and the Faith of the Nine draws an explicit line from the Nine's behavior to mortal behavior: the same forces that move the gods move people, and understanding the Nine is understanding yourself. Music and poetry are the primary vehicles of prayer — not because the faith requires musical ability, but because the creation was an act of song, and reaching the Nine through the same medium is considered the most direct available.

**Who holds authority:** The Listeners are the Val'arian equivalent of priests — people considered to be in contact with the Nine, whose role is to hear requests and offerings and mediate between petitioners and the divine. There is no hierarchy among Listeners beyond age: the eldest in a community speaks last and has final authority in matters of faith, designated as Elder but carrying no title that grants power over other Listeners in other communities. This means the Faith of the Nine has no central institution, no ability to issue binding doctrine across communities, and no mechanism by which an outside power could capture or redirect its leadership. The parallels with Ilianism on this structural point are not lost on comparative theologians.

**Daily practice:** The Consecration of the Full Moon is the faith's primary communal rite: a musical performance on the first night of each full moon, either alone or in a group, honoring all Nine. The performance does not have to be skilled. It has to be sincere. A Val'arian who cannot sing will still sing, privately, on full moon nights — the bar for acceptable worship is internal rather than aesthetic.

The faith's spread beyond Val'aria — particularly among Duardeen communities in Endoras — has produced hybrid practices that Val'arian traditionalists regard with varying degrees of tolerance. A Duardeen who prays to Dag'neth before a river crossing and maintains otherwise standard Ilianist practice is not, in the Faith of the Nine's reading, a heretic or a convert. They are someone who has recognized a truth that the Nine embody. The faith does not require exclusivity.

**On Divine Casting.** The Faith of the Nine does not produce Clerics. The Cleric class draws its power from institutional standing within a religious hierarchy — ordination, obedience, and the authority of a church structure. The Faith of the Nine has none of that. What it has are the Nine themselves: nine beings who pay attention when a Listener has something worth hearing. Val'arian divine casters are Sorcerers. The HP they spend casting is the price of that attention — not a spell slot rationed by a church, but something given from the self each time they ask the Nine to act. A Listener who has developed this capacity is recognized within Val'arian communities as someone the Nine have chosen to hear directly. That is a relationship, not a rank.

### **The Way of Boros**

*"Live with strength. Die well."*

Boros is not a god in the conventional theological sense — or rather, the Way of Boros does not spend much time on the question of what Boros was, is, or wants, because the practice of the faith does not require the answer. What the tradition records is a warrior of the early Third Era whose combat ability was without peer in the known world and who, in dying, became the object of reverence that later formed into a religious tradition. Whether Boros was divine, was touched by the divine, or was simply the most competent person anyone in the early Ûrgon and Badaki communities had ever seen is a question the Sages — the tradition's designated keepers of the lore — consider theologically irrelevant.

**What it believes:** The world is improved by people who master something. Any *something*. The faith organizes itself around the concept of a chosen skill, practiced until it becomes excellence, and excellence pursued until it becomes mastery. A farmer who grows better crops each season than the last is practicing the Way of Boros. So is a warrior. So is a diplomat, a mason, or a physician. The martial reputation the faith has acquired outside Urgônia and Gwan'tor reflects the ancestral context of its founding — a people created for war finding a way to transmute the purpose of combat into a general principle of human development — but the tradition itself does not privilege fighting above other forms of mastery.

Honor functions as the faith's ethical core. Protecting those weaker than yourself is honorable because it demonstrates strength used for a purpose rather than dominance. Avoiding unnecessary conflict is honorable because it preserves the capacity for action that a genuinely necessary conflict might require. A Boroite who picks fights for the pleasure of winning them has missed the point. A Boroite who loses a fight they chose to avoid has not.

**Who holds authority:** No one, in the formal sense. Each community has a Sage — a practitioner who has chosen mastery of the tradition's knowledge as their calling — who provides counsel, adjudicates questions of the tradition's application, and keeps the accounts of Boros's history as far as it is recoverable. The Sages have no authority to compel. They have the authority that comes from being the person whose counsel is most trusted, which is as much as the tradition thinks authority should be.

**Spread and adaptation:** The Way of Boros is practiced across both Urgônia and Gwan'tor, with regional variation in emphasis rather than doctrine. Gwan'tor's Badaki tradition applies it most explicitly in the martial context, where the discipline of the warrior-priest is the faith's most visible expression. Urgônia's Ûrgon communities apply it more broadly — the stonemason clan's pride in their craft quality is as much an expression of Boroite principle as the warrior's discipline, and the Ûrgon tradition does not distinguish between them in terms of spiritual value.

The faith has attracted followers outside its ancestral communities, primarily among mercenaries and craftspeople who find its emphasis on practical mastery more immediately useful than the theological frameworks of the major faiths. The Iron Brothers have drawn from Boroite traditions in developing their own code of conduct, which the tradition's Sages find flattering and the Iron Brothers' leadership finds convenient to neither confirm nor deny.

**On Weapons.** The question a Boroite warrior-priest asks when choosing a weapon is not *what is safest* but *what demands the most of me*. A flail in practiced hands is mastery. So is a war hammer. The tradition does not require the largest weapon available — it requires the weapon whose demands reveal something about the practitioner. In practice, Boroite clergy trained at Duskhold favor the two-handed weapons and the heavier polearms: the bardiche, the halberd, the two-handed sword. Choosing one of these over a shield is a public declaration. It says: I have trained until the weapon is enough. The shield is for those who have not finished training yet.

### **The Druidic Tradition**

The Druidic Order is not a religion. This distinction matters because the druids themselves are careful about it and because the distinction has legal and political weight in several nations where the difference between "practicing a religion" and "practicing something else" determines what you are allowed to do.

What the Druidic Order is: a tradition of relationship with the natural world, organized hierarchically, governed by practice and challenge, and operating on a different set of principles than institutional religion. Druids draw their power from the same cosmological source as clerics — the divine energy that suffuses the world — but they access it through direct engagement with the natural world rather than through prayer to specific divine figures. The Druidic tradition predates the organized religions of the Third Era and draws on pre-Second Era practice in ways that make it impossible to cleanly categorize as a branch of Ilianism or as something independent of it. Both positions have articulate defenders.

**What it holds:** Everything has its place. Good and evil are both natural forces. The druid's responsibility is not to the dominance of one over the other but to the functioning of the system that contains both. A druid is more likely to confront a land-clearance operation destroying a forest than to drive off a monster that is hunting in its natural territory. The monster is doing what it is. The land-clearance is doing what it is not supposed to do. This orientation makes druids predictably difficult to hire for campaigns of conquest, since the large-scale changes that conquest produces are exactly the kind of disruption the druidic tradition opposes.

**How it is organized:** The hierarchy is regional and competitive. Each geographical region — defined by natural boundaries rather than political ones — supports a defined number of druids at each rank. Advancement past a certain level requires a formal challenge: a test of skill, knowledge, or combat, decided by the challenger and incumbent. The loser drops to a fixed lower rank; the winner takes the position. This means druidic advancement is not gradual accumulation but a specific contest with a specific outcome, and druids who have reached high ranks have beaten someone to get there.

The Grand Druid is unique: one person, known to every druid in the world, who holds that position until they advance further and appoint a successor. Hierophants — druids who have passed through the Grand Druid rank — are no longer bound by the hierarchy. They are, in practice, unpredictable: people who have mastered the tradition and are no longer subject to its organizational constraints, pursuing whatever they have concluded matters after a lifetime of service.

**Its relationship with Ilianism:** Complicated and varied by region. In Alvengaard, druidic practice and Ilianist worship coexist within communities without formal institutional relationships — an Oracle and a druid in the same settlement may share cosmological assumptions while maintaining distinct practices and occasionally disagreeing sharply about specific questions. In Solara, the urban Ilianist tradition regards druids with respect-at-a-distance: the tradition is taken seriously, the people are regarded as legitimate practitioners, and the question of whether they are practicing the same faith or a parallel one is considered less urgent than making sure both groups' holy days are observed in the civic calendar.

Val Cadma classifies druidic practice as heresy because it constitutes communion with forces outside Tar'amaan's dominion. Druids in Cadman-adjacent territory practice underground or not at all.

**For the GM — What Druidic Magic Actually Is:** The Old Faith preaches that the natural world is suffused with Ilya's creative force, and the Druidic Compact holds this more emphatically than any other tradition. They are right, in a sense, they cannot fully access. A druid who communes with living systems — who attunes to the cycle of growth, death, and renewal — is reaching the echo of Ilya's power in the ongoing battle more directly than any other mortal practice. The fluctuations that the Magic Bloc's instruments catalogue are felt by senior druids before any instrument records them, and the Hyerophancy has kept records of those fluctuations for four centuries longer than the Order of Magnus has. They do not know what causes them. They know what they feel like from inside the attunement, which is not the same thing.

This is what makes a Vrykola's inversion the deepest possible apostasy — not merely against druidic philosophy, but against the cosmological structure that philosophy rests on. A Húldar druid communes with the living world. A Vrykola feeds on the same force to sustain something that should have ended. Where a druid participates in Ilya's echo, a Vrykola parasitizes it. The Hyerophancy has no complete theological framework for this. What it has is the conviction, held at the level of institutional instinct, that the thing it hunts at the far end of the dark druid's path is not simply dangerous, but an inversion of everything the tradition is.

**Its relationship with the Church of Tar'amaan:** The Church's position is theologically straightforward — natural magic is either divine (granted by Tar'amaan) or suspect (drawing on other powers). Since druids explicitly draw on powers outside Tar'amaan's sanctioned framework, they are by definition in the suspect category. The Inquisition has pursued druids in occupied Norderenn with approximately the same priority it gives to arcane practitioners, which is high.

### **Minor Faiths and Forgotten Gods**

The four major faiths and the Druidic tradition account for most of the Known World's organized religious life. They do not account for all of it.

Folk traditions persist in communities that have maintained practices below the threshold of institutional attention. Fishing villages along the Endoran coast perform propitiation rites to unnamed river spirits that predate any major faith's presence in the region. Certain Duardeen families maintain ancestor veneration distinct from both Boroite tradition and Ilianist observance. Traders working Inner Sea routes carry small tokens to specific unnamed protective figures — whether these are diminished deities, divine echoes, or persistent human habits is a question most practitioners find theologically unnecessary. These traditions do not produce Clerics or divine casters in any meaningful sense. They produce people with specific obligations, specific practices, and specific networks of trust with others who maintain the same things.

Minor cults — small organized communities built around divine figures that never reached institutional scale — exist in more numbers than the major faiths find comfortable to acknowledge. Most are regional, most are tolerant of coexistence, and most are invisible to a traveler who does not know what to look for. A Cleric emerging from a genuine minor cult is unusual but not impossible; the GM should handle these cases with the understanding that the institutional support the class usually assumes is absent or minimal, with the corresponding practical consequences.

Then there are the forgotten gods: divine figures who existed, who acted in the world, and who no longer respond.

**Virthun, the God of Just Memory**, is the clearest example. The Urgônian account of the Vithurian Orchestra's origin holds that he worked through chosen composers — "registrars," in the old term — rather than through temples or formal priesthood, charging them with preserving important truths through art. When he disappeared — and the accounts differ on whether he died, transcended, or simply withdrew beyond reach — his last followers made a vow to continue his work without divine direction. What they built eventually became the Orchestra. Whether this is literally true or is the kind of story that accumulates around a founding is a question the Orchestra has declined to answer. What is harder to dismiss is the specific quality the Orchestra has maintained across four centuries: its insistence on truth over flattery, its commitment to preserving deeds as they actually happened rather than as powerful people would prefer them remembered. That principle came from somewhere.

Virthun cannot grant divine power. Any practitioner who follows the tradition is living by a philosophy, not receiving divine attention. The practice centers on honest witness: preserving what actually happened, resisting the pressure to let the record be reshaped by whoever holds power at the moment. Its alignment implications are lawful — not obedience to any institution, but commitment to a standard that exists outside institutional convenience.

Whether Virthun is entirely gone is a question each GM can answer differently. The historical record is ambiguous in ways that permit either conclusion. A character who follows the tradition with genuine commitment, who bears witness to something important enough, might find that the silence on the other end has an answer in it after all. Or might not. That uncertainty is part of what the God of Just Memory means.

**For the GM:** what is described here is a starting point, not a complete catalogue. Folk traditions in your campaign's home region, minor cults built around divine figures whose names history has mostly lost, the occasional practitioner of something that has no institutional context and therefore no one to explain it — these are the space that remains once the major faiths are accounted for. Fill it with what your campaign needs. The Known World is large enough that not every divine figure who ever moved through it was written down, and old enough that some of what was written down no longer has anyone left who remembers reading it.

### **For the GM: Religion at the Table**

Religion in the Known World is not an optional background, but the mechanism that justifies the occupation of Norderenn, that makes certain markets refuse certain currencies, that determines which inns will admit which guests, and that gives the war that is coming its ideological shape.

**Tar'amaan sincerity is the most important thing to play correctly.** The Church is not populated by cynics who know they are evil and do it anyway. The Acolyte who reports a suspicious neighbor genuinely believes they are performing an act of communal protection. The Inquisitor who conducts a purification rite believes in the doctrine that makes the rite necessary. The Archbishop who plans military campaigns believes he is accelerating Tar'amaan's victory over the forces that nearly destroyed the world once already. None of this requires the GM to endorse these positions. It requires the GM to play them as the positions of people who are sincere, coherent, and wrong in ways that are specifically dangerous precisely because of the sincerity.

**Ilianist observance creates adventure hooks.** The sacred seasons — the first weeks of spring and autumn during which open conflict is prohibited — are enforced with real consequences in nations where the faith runs deep. A dispute that cannot be resolved militarily during a sacred week creates exactly the kind of pressure that produces alternative solutions, unusual alliances, and the kind of deal that gets made when violence is temporarily unavailable. Solara's legal system treats sacred-season violence as a distinct and more serious category of offense. Norderenn's resistance uses sacred seasons for supply movement, knowing that Cadman patrols are reduced during weeks when their Ilianist-sympathizing soldiers are observing.

**The druidic hierarchy is a resource and a complication.** The challenge-based advancement system means that any druid the party meets has a specific rank, a specific region they are responsible for, and a specific person above them they beat to get there — and a specific person below them who is probably thinking about when to try again. Druids in high positions are not interchangeable. They are specific individuals with specific histories inside a competitive hierarchy, and the politics of that hierarchy are not irrelevant to what they will do when the party needs something.

**Faith is personal before it is institutional.** A Norderenn resistance fighter who maintains Ilianist practice is not doing it for political reasons — they are doing it because the practice is real to them. After all, the sacred weeks mean something, because the Kronn in the box under the floor and the songs after dark and the prayer that nobody official can see or confiscate are the things that make the resistance more than pragmatic opposition to an inconvenient government. Let that weight be present at the table.

**The Faith of the Nine's opacity is intentional and playable.** Val'arian NPCs who are practitioners do not explain their faith to outsiders casually. They pray on full moon nights, they play music for reasons that they do not attribute publicly to religious observance, and they describe the Nine with the slightly careful selection of words that comes from people who have decided what they are going to share. A Val'arian who tells a non-Val'arian something real about the Faith of the Nine has decided to. That decision means something.

### **Those Who Follow No God**

Not everyone in the Known World organizes their life around a divine framework. Some people have looked at the evidence — at the Second Era that the Illyans, supposedly divine, produced; at the Darkness that Ilya created and then had to imprison at the cost of her presence in the world; at the Church of Tar'amaan, whose god of light has authorized the specific horrors currently unfolding in Norderenn — and concluded that if gods exist, they are not reliable guides to how a person should live.

This is not atheism in the dismissive sense. Most people who follow no faith do not deny that Ilya, Wysla, and Ayla exist, or that the Illyans existed, or that something answered prayers in a city under siege. They deny that existence implies authority. The gods are real, the way weather is real: a fact about the world that affects you, that you can observe and sometimes predict, that has not asked for your opinion and will not adjust its behavior based on your approval.

People in this category live by whatever principles they have arrived at through experience, reasoning, or the influence of specific people they trusted. Some hold to a kind of pragmatic ethics — do not do to others what you would not have done to yourself, keep your word, pay your debts, and let the cosmos worry about the cosmological questions. Some have developed private frameworks that draw from multiple traditions without joining any of them: the Boroite emphasis on mastery, the Ilianist sense of balance, the Val'arian model of the flawed divine as a useful map of human failure. Some have no framework they would articulate and simply make decisions as they come, according to intuitions they trust more than any theology.

Mechanically, non-believers are not disadvantaged. Alignment governs behavior, not devotion — a godless character who acts with consistency and honesty is no less lawful-good than a devout Ilianist who does the same. The alignment system in S\&D describes the actual forces the Sisters built into reality, not the beliefs mortals hold about those forces. A person who has never heard of Ilya still exists within a cosmos Ilya helped create, and the balance between order and chaos still applies to them, whether they name it or not.

What changes is the texture. A godless character has no Oracle to consult, no confession cycle to maintain, no sacred season observance to use as social cover. They have no divine community, which is a practical resource as well as a spiritual one — the network of Ardents, the shared recognition between Ilianists in occupied Norderenn, the specific trust a Boroite warrior extends to another Boroite. They navigate the Known World without those networks, which closes some doors and opens others. People who distrust institutional religion of any kind tend to find a godless companion refreshing. People who organize their communities around faith tend to find them slightly difficult to read.

The Known World does not persecute non-belief, except in Val Cadma, where failure to maintain Tar'amaanist practice is itself an infraction. A godless character in a Cadman-administered territory is required to perform the same confession schedule and morning prayers as everyone else, which means they are required to perform a faith they do not hold in front of an institution that will punish inconsistency. This is a specific problem with specific solutions, none of them comfortable.

### **The Single Source**

Every faith in this chapter — Ilianism, the Church of Tar'amaan, the Faith of the Nine, the Way of Boros, the Druidic Compact — draws its divine power from the same origin. There is one source. What mortals receive is an echo of the battle Ilya and the Darkness are still fighting in the Abyss: her creative force and the Darkness's opposition to it, in constant collision, leaking through whatever barrier separates that plane from the mortal world. The theological differences between the faiths are real. The doctrinal disputes are genuine. The power behind all of them is singular.

This means several things the GM should keep quiet about.

The Church of Tar'amaan's foundational doctrine holds that the Sisters are dead. The echo that powers every Tar'amaanist Cleric's spells is evidence that they are not. Ilya is fighting. Wysla and Ayla are present in the world's entropic and ordering forces. If a mortal ever confirmed this — through a working, through contact with something old enough to know, through the right fragment of the right ruin — it would not merely challenge the Church's doctrine. It would collapse the theological foundation on which the occupation of Norderenn was authorized, the Inquisition was justified, and Archbishop Kranzler's military campaigns were sanctioned. The Five have no specific interest in making this happen. They also have no interest in the Church's theology surviving in its current form, long-term. They simply operate on a longer schedule than the difference between those two positions requires them to resolve.

The fluctuations in magical availability that the Magic Bloc has catalogued for three centuries — the variations that correlate with nothing in the natural world, the Order of Magnus has identified — are variations in the battle itself. Hotter or colder, one force gains briefly before the other responds. No mortal institution has the data to know this, because no mortal institution has been taking readings for three thousand years. Elder Dragons have. They have not published their findings.

The Third Era's major faiths did not construct their theologies from nothing. The Three Sisters were present and active in the First Era — not as distant cosmic principles, but as participants in a working order. The Ilyans were their instruments. The balance that Ilianist theology describes was real, as an achieved state rather than an aspirational one, for the entirety of the First Era. What the faiths hold now are interpretations of genuine divine history, shaped by the specific fragments their founders had access to and the specific losses those founders were trying to make sense of.

The Church's founding scholars saw the ruins. They saw what arcane practice at civilizational scale had preceded. Their causal inference was wrong about mechanism and correct about scale — the correlation they identified between magical practice and collapse was real; the variable they misidentified was political, not metaphysical. Ilianist theology, rebuilt from oral tradition in the Third Era's first centuries, preserved more of the original structure than the Church did. The reconstruction introduced its own distortions. Neither tradition knows what it got wrong; neither would recognize original First Era practice in its full form. Both faiths are working from real history. Neither has a complete version of it.

For a campaign that reaches the level where this becomes relevant, the players discovering the single-source truth is not the climax of the cosmological arc, but the beginning of understanding what that truth means for every conflict they have been in since the first session.
